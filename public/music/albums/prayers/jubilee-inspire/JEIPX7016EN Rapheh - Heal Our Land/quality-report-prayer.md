# Prayer Quality Report — Rapheh - Heal Our Land (JEIPX7016EN)

## Overall Album Scores
- Effectiveness: 96/100
- Reverence: 97/100
- Composite: 96/100

## Album-Level Assessment

This album walks the full Temple journey from outer-court threshold to walking-out-with-shalom without a single broken station. The corporate-first grammar ("WE have sinned" — not "they") is held with Daniel-9 / Nehemiah-1 / Yoel-2:17 discipline from S2 onward, which is the single hardest move a national-prayer album has to make and is the move most national-prayer projects miss. The "repentance not triumph" posture is preserved across the whole body of the album; brightness is correctly held back until S1 (gathering) and S12 (walking-out), and the Mercy Seat song (S9) lands as the climactic worship moment without ever tipping into stadium voice. The Parokhet revelation at S8 ("the veil tore above every land") is the right hinge in the right place — and Verse 2 of that song does what a cinematic revelation song must do: it earns the chill rather than performs it.

The listener arrives at the Mercy Seat. The Personal Insertion Beat in S10 actually converts the album into an act of intercession (the listener speaks city/leader names aloud); the Personal Insertion Beat in S11 actually converts the album into a stewardship audit ("what is my part?"). The two-beat composite of S10 plus S11 is what gives this album its export value — any nation, any season, any believer. The Yeshua-quoted line (John 17:21 personalized) lives at S9 where the surrender song belongs, and Gethsemane voice is correctly isolated to S9 only. Strongest stretch: S8 - S9 - S10 (revelation, kavod, breastplate) — these three back-to-back are the manifestation engine of the album. Weakest stretch: none structural; the lightest-weight song is S7 (Smoke Above the Skyline) which is theologically sound and beautifully imaged but slightly less viscerally arresting than its neighbors — still a KEEP.

The album holds the user-specified mood: anthemic but humble, communal but not partisan, weighty but not despairing. No specific country named, no party identified, no living politician named — perfectly exportable. Every quality gate from the blueprint cumulative checklist passes. The album ships.
