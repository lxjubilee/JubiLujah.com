## Album-Level Assessment

Nechama - Held walks the full twelve-station Temple journey with unusual discipline for a grief record. The listener is delivered from the gate of tears in Track 1, through teshuvah and the laver, into the Holy Place where Ruach HaKodesh hovers over the empty side of the bed, through the menorah's seven lamps in the dark room, the Mourner's broken bread, the speechless incense altar, the cinematic revelation at the torn veil, the Gethsemane surrender at the Mercy Seat, the priestly intercession with names spoken aloud, the Hineni in the kitchen chair, and out into a dawn-thanksgiving that is dawn-light, not stadium-light. The Mercy Seat is reached and the listener does walk back out, not "fixed" but held — which is exactly what the user-specified mood lock demands. No song breaks the single-piano-single-voice promise except in the precise places the blueprint authorizes (heartbeat tof on Track 10 V2 only; full ensemble at the cinematic revelation in Track 8; first major-chord resolution on the Sealed Amen of Track 12). Praise mechanics are not inflated; the worship-dominant ratio sits at the targeted 14/86 average.

The strongest stretch is Tracks 6 through 9 — Yeshua wept (S6), Ruach Herself prays the unfinished groan (S7), held inside the tearing (S8), heavier than the grave with Gethsemane voice (S9). This is the theological spine of the album and it lands. Track 8 is the album's masterpiece: "He did not stop the grief, He stepped inside it" is a singable revelation grounded in Matthew 27:51 and John 11:35 simultaneously, and it earns the cinematic-twist designation outright. The weakest stretch is the laver song (Track 3) — not because the theology is weak but because the Hebrew word "demaot" (already plural) is used as a singular ("every demaot") in one line, which a Hebrew-speaking listener will notice and a Hebrew-respecting album cannot afford. Track 7 is on the lower end of the album's PDQ range but the lower declarative density is intentional (the song is about wordlessness) and is theologically defensible.

The album honors lament over lift throughout. Track 12's brightness is correctly framed as "the dawn that ends the night is His thumb on my cheek" — not "the grief is over." The user-specified mood lock is held: this is the most tender album in the catalog, single piano, single voice, no big resolution, just presence.

## SOP Compliance Findings

- LOW severity — Track 3 Hebrew grammar: the line "mayim chayim under every demaot" treats demaot (plural for "tears") as a singular countable noun. Recommended fix: replace with "mayim chayim under the demaot" or "mayim chayim falling on every tear." Affects: Track 3 chorus and final chorus.
- LOW severity — Track 5 Pre-Chorus phrasing: "the Ruach of Yahuah" is the literal Isaiah 11:2 reading of the seventh Spirit and is correct, but the line "and the seven do not need the dark to leave first / in order to keep burning" reads slightly as a self-standing declaration rather than addressed to Yahuah. No fix required; flagged for awareness only.
- INFO — Track 7 PDQ at 52 is the album floor. Intentional, defensible, do not raise. The song is about the breakdown of words; declarative density would betray its theology.
- INFO — Track 10 Personal Insertion Beat directive present with prompt "Speak their name now." Verified compliant with Master Manual V.
- INFO — Track 11 Personal Insertion Beat directive present with prompt "Bring it before Him now." Verified compliant.
- INFO — Yeshua-quoted line placed at Track 9 (Matthew 26:39 Gethsemane). Correctly located in the only station where Gethsemane voice is permitted.

No HIGH-severity findings. No banned content. No real-artist name-drops. No conditional-voice petition closers. No "they're in a better place" Hallmark drift.
