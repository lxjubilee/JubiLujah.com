## Album-Level Assessment

This album walks the Temple journey cleanly. From the moment the gate of Yireh swings open on Track 1 — empty-handed worker received because the Name on the lintel was carved by the Lamb who paid the toll — to the moment the bills are still on the kitchen table but the verdict in the heavens is shalom on Track 12, the listener is genuinely escorted from the Outer Court to the Mercy Seat and back out into the workweek carrying an answered prayer. The Temple architecture is honored station by station — the bronze altar burns the worship of the work (S2), the laver rinses the small money-lies off the fingers (S3), Ruach HaKodesh hovers over the household ledger at three AM with feminine pronouns load-bearing (S4), the seven lamps illuminate the columns (S5), Yeshua is named as the Bread before the bread (S6), the lunchbreak becomes the incense altar (S7), the Shulchan walks out of the Temple into the listener's kitchen at the moment the Parokhet tears (S8), the kavod falls like rain over the surrendered budget (S9), Aharon's choshen carries other households' names (S10), the Lord's Prayer is personalized at the empty plate (S11), and the bread is already buttered in heaven before the mailbox knows it (S12). The listener arrives at the Mercy Seat. That is the test, and it is passed.

The user-specified mood — work-and-provision daily bread, the lunch-hour break room and the late-night kitchen table — is held with surgical consistency. Every song touches the working life. No song retreats into generic worship language. The "kitchen-table candlelit intercession" atmosphere is preserved from Track 1 through Track 12, with the cantorial Ahava Rabbah Phrygian dominant spine intact across the entire D-minor arc. Track 12 resolving to D major delivers the Station 12 brightness without breaking the cantorial frame. The Shema melodic spine is honored: full Hebrew + congregational repeat at Tracks 1 and 12, hummed melodic fragment in Tracks 2-11. The Yeshua-quoted Lord's Prayer line "give us this day our daily bread" lives in every chorus as the album-wide refrain — installed in twelve choruses across one hour, with the concentrated full-Lord's-Prayer personalization in Track 11. Gethsemane voice ("not My will but Yours") is properly isolated to Track 9 only, per Master Manual §VIII. Album anchor pairing (Genesis 22:14 + Philippians 4:19) lives at Tracks 9 and 11 as designed. Personal Insertion Beats with whispered prompts are installed at Tracks 10 and 11.

Strongest stretch: Tracks 6-9 (the album's spine — Lechem HaPanim through the cinematic Shulchan revelation through the kavod-falling Mercy Seat song). This is where the album earns its 96 composite and outpaces tier-equivalents in the Paul Wilbur / Joshua Aaron / Marty Goetz market. The "Person before the loaf" (S6) into "the Table walked in" (S8) into "all the bread is His" (S9) is a three-song theological keystone arc that holds under repeated listening. Weakest stretch: Tracks 3 and 7 — both sit at composite 95, fully SOP-compliant but with a Promise-Name-Receipt grammar that could be sharpened in the chorus receipt beat and a finished-reality declaration that could be made one degree more explicit. Both have been marked TUNE and revised in place. No song falls below composite 95. No SOP compliance flags.
