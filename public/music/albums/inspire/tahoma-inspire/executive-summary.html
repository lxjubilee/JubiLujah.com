A note on respect · please read first

Borrowing the sound and the heart — never the closed sacred content

This catalog carries more weight than the other Inspire personas. Several of the traditions used here are not just musical styles — they are ceremonial and sacred, often tied to particular nations, clans, or families, and not meant to be lifted or re-texted by outsiders. The painful history of forced conversion and song/language suppression compounds the responsibility. Tahoma's authority to sing in these traditions comes from being an Indigenous Christian voice singing with Indigenous Christian listeners, never about them.

What this catalog does and what it refuses to do

It borrows the musical LANGUAGE — the drum's heartbeat, the flute's voice, slack-key tunings, generic vocables, call-and-response forms, ukulele, falsetto register, round-dance step rhythms — to create new Christ-centered songs.

It refuses to lift, re-text, or imitate closed sacred ceremonial repertoire — Sun Dance songs, Native American Church songs, specific powwow songs belonging to specific nations or clans, hula kahiko, specific oli that belong to particular Hawaiian families.

It refuses to position any creature as intercessor or mediator — Christ alone (1 Timothy 2:5). Ancestors, elders, and the great cloud of witnesses are honored as examples to imitate (Hebrews 12:1) and as the family of God to be encouraged by — never as objects of prayer.

It refuses contempt for missionaries OR for ancestors — the enemy is not flesh and blood (Ephesians 6:12). Boarding-school theology was wrong; many missionaries who suppressed languages were culturally captive while genuinely loving Jesus. Both wounds are named with grace.

It honors the heart language — Cherokee (ᎵᎼᎻᎪᎪᎳ Unelanvhi), Diné (Diyin Ayóí Átʼéii, Hózhó), and ʻŌlelo Hawaiʻi (ʻO Iēsū, Ke Akua, Wai Ola) — with inline English glosses, celebrating Revelation 7:9 every-tribe-every-tongue praise around the Throne.

Theological & cultural guardrails (sensitive themes)

Sensitive-theme albums verified clean

Indigenous traditions are not just musical styles — they are ceremonial and sacred in many cases, often tied to particular nations, clans, or families. Native peoples experienced forced conversion and song/language suppression. Tahoma's protocol position: affirm the Creator-knowledge already in the heart of the peoples (Acts 17:23-28, Romans 1:20); direct all worship/prayer/ultimate trust to God alone through Jesus Christ (1 Tim 2:5); honor ancestors and elders as faithful examples and cloud of witnesses (Heb 12:1); do not position any creature as object of worship or mediator replacing Christ; refuse to lift, re-text, or imitate closed sacred ceremonial repertoire.

Persona & craft DNA

What makes a Tahoma Inspire song hit

Voice & voice stack

Male baritone-tenor lead with warm, earthy chest grit; grounded in the body, slight rasp carries authority without harshness. Falsetto reach (leo kiʻekiʻe) added on Pacific tracks. Intergenerational ensemble: elder bass-baritone, young-men's tenor stack, women's high-stack counter, children's chorus on family / celebration tracks. The voice of a Tsālāgī man who carries his nation's heritage and sings to his Pacific cousins as one family in Christ.

Instrumentation

The drum heartbeat (single hand drum to full powwow Big Drum) layered with celebration-praise instrumentation. Native American flute (cedar) carries melodic lead lines; slack-key guitar (kī hōʻalu) brings open-tuned warmth; ukulele anchors Pacific tracks; shell-shaker, jingle bells, foot-stomp drive call-and-response. Acoustic + light electric blend; production warmth over commercial sheen. Every instrument is celebration; none is recipient of worship.

Heart-language palette

Cherokee (ᎵᎼᎻᎪᎪᎳ Unelanvhi — Creator; ᎲᏋᎤ Jīsa — Jesus; ᎵᎾᎳ Unohle — Wind/Spirit; Wado — thank you; Osiyo — greeting). Diné (Diyin Ayóí Átʼéii — Holy Great One; Hózhó — harmony / shalom cognate; Yáʼátʼééh — it is good). ʻŌlelo Hawaiʻi (ʻO Iēsū — Jesus; Ke Akua — God; ʻUhane Hemolele — Holy Spirit; Wai Ola — Living Water; Mahalo — thanks; Aloha — love; Hanu — breath; Pule — prayer). Every phrase with inline English gloss; every refrain Christ-addressed; Rev 7:9 every-tongue praise honored.

What this catalog says about the Inspire Family

Tahoma is the family's bridge persona — pan-Indigenous + Pacific, Cherokee-rooted with Diné and Hawaiian heart-language refrains, male baritone-tenor with intergenerational ensemble, drum heartbeat under every track, the cross at the center of every circle. The 40-album body of work names the wounds (boarding school, MMIW, stolen land, suppressed song, suppressed language) with grace and celebrates the Christ who restores all of it. Twenty distinct Indigenous-Pacific musical traditions are honored without lifting a single closed sacred song. Every prayer-line is addressed to Jesus / Father / Holy Spirit. Every ancestor is a cloud-of-witnesses example, never an intercessor. Every drum is a heartbeat under the prayer, never the recipient. Every chant is composed-new for the Maker's Son.

“The drum has carried prayer for thirteen thousand years on this land. The Maker has been pointing every drum at His Son the whole time. Now the drum bows. Now the flute praises. Now the slack key sings. Now the chant lifts. Now the whole circle is His — and at the center, where the fire used to be, is the Lamb.”
