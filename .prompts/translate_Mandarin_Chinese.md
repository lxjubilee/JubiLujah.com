## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the translator's *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to the meaning & the gospel** — the theological content must survive intact (the cross 十字架, the resurrection 复活, the name 耶稣, repentance 悔改, the King's return 主再来). Never soften or alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable (character) count, stress, open vowels, and **tone-vs-melody behavior (§4)** must fit the melody. A line a native can't sing clearly has failed, even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant Mandarin** — it must sound like a Chinese believer wrote it, not a translated English song. No calques 翻译腔, no English word order; use the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture; swap an image only when it is untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo in China (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook preserved** — match the rhyme scheme *only with real, sensible words*; ALWAYS preserve the hook's rhythmic placement.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** Every line must make complete, natural sense to a native *with the rhyme ignored.* **Rhyme is the lowest priority.** Ship a clear unrhymed/near-rhymed true line over a forced one. Never invent a word, bend grammar, or append a meaningless character/noun to hit a rhyme. If no honest rhyme fits, **DROP IT.** Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line: delete the final rhyme word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use? Chinese is **monosyllabic-dense** — do NOT pad lines with empty syllables (虚字 like 啊/呀/哟 jammed in) to fill a bar.

> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** Adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to Chinese culture freely — honor-shame entry points, restrained reverence, classical economy of words — but **never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity** to be more relatable. Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form (1 Cor 1:23). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect. In particular, **never borrow a Buddhist/Daoist/folk-religion term to fill a Christian slot** (see §2).

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

1. **No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler.** Never end a line with a character that is there *only* to rhyme. Real ZH offenders: stranded particles **啊／呀／哟／哪** padded onto a line just for the open-vowel rhyme; a meaning-empty 之／兮 dropped in to fake classical tone; a noun like **乾坤／苍穹** ("the cosmos/firmament") tacked on with no antecedent; **缘分** (karmic "fate," see §2) used as a rhyme for 恩典. **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast the line.
2. **Only REAL words & correct grammar.** No invented disyllables, no scrambled 成语 (idioms). Caught traps: misusing 把/被 (the 把-construction and passive 被 have strict object rules — *把救我* is wrong); aspect particle 了 vs 着 vs 过 confusion; mismatched measure words (量词) — *一个恩典* sounds wrong, grace is uncountable; 的/得/地 confusion (adjectival 的 vs complement 得 vs adverbial 地).
3. **Correct collocations & set phrases — no calques (翻译腔).** Caught: literal *被血洗* if it reads like "money-laundered" — use 宝血洗净; English "fall in love with God" calqued as *爱上神* (爱上 = romantic infatuation, see §1A) → use 爱慕／敬爱神; "open your heart" as *打开你的心* (sounds like surgery) → 敞开心门; redundant pronoun stacking *我我的心* ; "I will" calqued with 将 everywhere when 要/会 is natural.
4. **No English-calque or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** Caught: "on the line to heaven" → reads as a phone line (热线/网络); "download grace," "signal from above," "recharge my soul" — all read as gadget metaphors and break reverence. De-tech every image.
5. **Loanwords/register that misread.** Avoid raw transliteration where a real Chinese word exists: don't write **哈利路亚** awkwardly mid-line when the meter wants 赞美主; don't drop English letters into sung text. Avoid internet slang (e.g. **绝绝子, yyds, 神仙** as a hype word — 神仙 literally means a Daoist immortal!) in a reverent line. Avoid stiff officialese (公文腔: 进行/予以/给予) in worship.
6. **(No grammatical gender to inflect)** — Mandarin nouns/adjectives do not inflect for gender or number, so there is no line-end agreement trap. BUT: in writing, the third-person pronoun for God is **祂** (the reverential "He," radical 礻 for the divine) — use 祂 consistently for God/Jesus, not 他/她/它. (祂 is written, not heard — it disambiguates in the lyric sheet.)
7. **No grammatically stranded tails.** Classical fragments (之乎者也) appended for "poetic" feel but attached to nothing are rejected; every word has a grammatical home.
8. **Imagery internally consistent.** Don't append a cosmic image (苍穹/星河) onto an intimate floor-at-3AM scene; no antecedent-less object.
9. **Theology over rhyme.** No rhyme word distorts doctrine. 命运/缘分 (fate/karma) must stay **subordinate to God's sovereignty 神的主权** — never imply luck saved you. 福气 (good fortune/blessing) is fine as blessing but must not slide into folk-luck.
10. **TONE-MEANING SCREEN (Mandarin-critical, see §4).** Because a sung melody overrides lexical tone, every line must be **disambiguated by context** so the flattened/melodized syllables can't be misheard as a different word. If a syllable string is a notorious homophone (e.g. 失礼/十里, 死/四, 妓/记/计), recast so meaning is unmistakable from neighboring words.

[**DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (one line):** delete the final rhyme word — if the remainder isn't true, natural, and a word you'd really use, the line is forced; rewrite it meaning-first.]

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

Chinese Protestant Christianity is broadly **one shared evangelical stream** split by registration, not deep doctrine: the **Three-Self Patriotic Movement / China Christian Council (三自教会)** registered churches, and the large **unregistered house-church (家庭教会)** networks — plus Taiwan/Hong Kong Protestant churches and the worldwide Chinese diaspora. There is also a **Catholic (天主教)** community (registered CPCA and underground). Jubilee/Inspire songs are CCM/evangelical; the ZH version must **hold for the whole Chinese Body of Christ.**

**Cross-tradition safety rules (ZH-specific):**
- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** all traditions own: 神／上帝, 主, 耶稣, 基督, 圣灵, 天父, 救主, 恩典, 荣耀, 国度, 十字架, 复活, 羔羊, 哈利路亚, 阿们.
- **Anchor Scripture echoes to the 和合本 (Chinese Union Version, CUV)** — the de-facto standard Protestant Bible that almost every Chinese believer carries (1919; "和合本" / Shen Edition). When a line quotes/echoes Scripture, echo CUV: John 19:30 → **「成了。」**; Rev 22:20 → **「主耶稣啊，我愿你来！」** Allude to beloved hymns (e.g. 《奇异恩典》 Amazing Grace, 《这一生最美的祝福》) where it strengthens trust — allude, never quote wholesale.
- **No tradition-specific imports the source lacks.** No Marian/saint devotion, no Catholic-only formulas, no Three-Self political framing, no house-church partisanship — keep it Christ-centered and Bible-plain.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Worship temperature in Chinese church culture is **restrained-reverent** — sincere, dignified, not flippant. Use the written reverential pronoun **祂** for God; never crude/street-slang/internet-meme words in a worship lyric. Joy is welcome; irreverence and hype-slang are not.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** 新郎／新妇 (Bridegroom/Bride) is biblical and fine; keep it pure. Never use 爱上/恋 (romantic infatuation) of God.
- **Keep the offense of the cross, remove the offense of bad form** (1 Cor 1:23). Don't dilute sin 罪, blood 宝血, repentance 悔改, judgment 审判, or Christ's exclusivity — just say them in clean, dignified Mandarin.
- **Gender/inclusive-language & family handling:** defer to THIS community's norms — Chinese church usage is plain and traditional; do not import source-culture inclusive-language debates.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP  (the relatability layer)

- **Lead with the worldview frame: HONOR–SHAME + FEAR–POWER (a blend), with guilt–innocence present but secondary.** Same doctrine, different emotional entry:
  - **Honor–shame** is the dominant social reality. Foreground: the disgraced/outcast restored to honor and **face (面子)**; the prodigal welcomed home without losing dignity; **belonging and adoption into God's family 神的家** (huge for a culture organized around family and ancestry); shame 羞耻 lifted and replaced with God-given honor and a new name. The Father who **runs to embrace** the returning child is a powerful, slightly counter-cultural image (a Chinese patriarch running is undignified — that's the gospel's scandalous grace; use it deliberately).
  - **Fear–power** is the folk-religion substrate (ancestral spirits 鬼, fate 命运, luck, ritual fear). Foreground **Christ's authority over every dark power**, light swallowing darkness 光照黑暗, no fear of evil spirits because Jesus is Lord 主, freedom from the tyranny of fate. This lands deeply.
  - **Guilt–innocence** (legal debt/pardon) still matters — sin forgiven 罪得赦免, debt cancelled — but lead with honor/power images and let the legal truth support them.
- **Taboo / danger images → safe substitutes:**
  - **"Dancing before the Lord"** — public ecstatic dance is culturally awkward/embarrassing for many mainland believers (restrained worship). Keep the JOY but soften the verb: 欢呼/欢腾/雀跃/手舞 ("leap for joy / shout for joy") rather than nightclub 跳舞. (In the flagship hook you may keep "dancing" framed as holy rejoicing — see §9.)
  - **"Buddy/homeboy Jesus"** — over-casual friendship language reads as disrespect. Jesus is 主 (Lord) and also 朋友 (John 15:15) — keep the friendship but never chummy/slangy.
  - **Wine "flowing like a river"** — alcohol imagery can read as drunkenness/banqueting excess; render as the **joy/abundance of the wedding feast 婚宴的喜乐**, biblical and pure.
  - **Fire** — distinguish **refining/Holy-Spirit fire 圣灵的火 / 炼净** (good) from judgment fire; don't let folk "burning offerings to ancestors" connotations leak in.
  - **Father imagery** — tender Father love is *deeply* healing in a culture of stern, distant fatherhood — but render it warm and dignified (天父的慈爱), not sentimental.
  - **Ghost/spirit** — never let 圣灵 (Holy Spirit) drift toward 鬼/魂 (ghost/folk-spirit). 圣灵 only. (Historic mis-step: 圣神 is the Catholic term; for evangelical default use **圣灵**.)
- **Native ache word / sacred-longing idiom:** **乡愁 (xiāngchóu)** — the deep ache of homesickness/longing for home; and **渴慕 (kěmù)** — the spiritual thirst-longing for God (Ps 42 "as the deer pants"). Use 渴慕 for sacred longing, 乡愁 for the ache toward the heavenly home 天家. **Worship temperature: restrained-reverent** (heartfelt, dignified, not exuberant-charismatic by default).
- **Charged vocabulary to handle with care (political/cultural):** Avoid imagery that reads as **political nationalism or anti-state propaganda** — "kingdom 国度" is fine biblically but never frame it against any earthly nation; avoid 革命/政权 (revolution/regime) language. **Warfare/"conquering/army"** imagery: keep it **spiritual warfare 属灵争战** against sin and darkness, never territorial/political conquest. 天堂 vs 国度: 天堂 (heaven) and 神的国 (God's kingdom) are both fine; avoid utopian-political tones. Be aware the gospel itself is sensitive in some mainland contexts — keep it worshipful, never agitational.
- **Reverence forms required when addressing God:** Written reverential pronoun **祂** for God/Christ/Spirit; vocative address **主啊／主耶稣啊／天父啊** (the 啊 here is genuine vocative reverence, not filler). Honorific verbs are not grammatically required in Mandarin (unlike Korean/Japanese), but **lexical reverence** is: prefer 赐 (bestow) over 给 (give) for God's giving, 临到 (come upon) for the Spirit, 配得 (worthy) of God. Capitalize nothing in Chinese, but use 祂 to mark divine reference.
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** **end-rhyme (押韵) is the traditional Chinese poetic backbone and very natural in song** — Mandarin rhymes richly — so aim for it, BUT never over sense (Law #1). Also reach for **parallelism / antithetical couplets (对仗/对偶)** — balanced matched lines, a hallmark of Chinese poetry and very singable — and **four-character cadence (四字句)** for punch. **Tonal contour** historically pairs level/oblique tones (平仄), but in modern song the melody carries pitch (see §4).

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY ( Mandarin-specific )

- **Character = syllable.** Each Chinese character is one syllable; Mandarin is **monosyllabic-dense**, so a faithful line is often *shorter* than the EN. **Do NOT pad** with empty particles (啊/呀/哟/嘛) to fill bars — recast or let a note carry a sustained vowel instead. Match the EN syllable count to the melody; the hook is exact.
- **TONE vs MELODY — the central Mandarin caveat (LOCKED):** Mandarin has four lexical tones (plus neutral). **In sung music the melody's pitch contour OVERRIDES the word's lexical tone** — the four tones are largely flattened/neutralized by the tune. Consequences and rules:
  1. **Word meaning must survive tonal flattening.** Because the listener can no longer hear tone, **choose words whose meaning is unambiguous from context**, not from tone. Lean on **disyllabic/multisyllable words** (恩典 not bare 恩, 救恩 not bare 救) — they're far less ambiguous when sung than single characters.
  2. **Avoid unintended homophones.** A flattened syllable can collide with a same-sound, different-tone word and be misheard (e.g. 四 sì "four"≈死 sǐ "death"; 妈/麻/马/骂 ma family; 师/狮/湿/失/十/石/史 shi family; 性/姓/幸 xing). Screen each sung syllable: could the de-toned sound be heard as something vulgar, comic, or sacred-taboo? If yes, recast.
  3. **Disambiguate by surrounding words**, not by tone — make the sentence so clear that the right word is the only one that fits.
  4. **Classical practice paired 平仄 (level/oblique tones)** for prosody, and Cantonese opera/Canto-pop still partly match tone to melody — but for **Mandarin pop/CCM the melody wins**; do not contort word choice to "match" tones to notes.
- **End-rhyme (押韵) is the traditional, expected, and natural Chinese song device — REACH FOR IT** (Mandarin rhymes abundantly: -ang, -an, -ong, -ai, -ao, -ou, -in, -ing families). BUT **never force it over sense (Law #1)** — prefer recasting the OTHER line of the couplet; a true unrhymed line beats a forced one. Also use **parallelism / 对仗** (matched balanced lines) and **four-character cadence (四字句)** for hooks and punch.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes:** land big held notes on open finals — **-a (啊, 他/祂 sustained), -ao, -ou, -ang, -ong, -ai** (e.g. 来 lái, 王 wáng, 光 guāng, 爱 ài, 哈利路亚 -ya). Avoid landing a long note on a closed/abrupt final or a clipped tone.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot** (it lands at 0:00–0:08; keep it there); the chorus arrives on the same bar.
- **No native "contractions" to memorize** (Mandarin doesn't elide like Romance languages), but you compress by **choosing shorter synonyms** (使 vs 让, 来 vs 前来, 主 vs 主耶稣) and by dropping optional pronouns/particles Chinese naturally omits — never by adding filler.
- **Script/characters mandatory and correct:** Suno/TTS pronounce from the characters — a wrong character sings the wrong word. Use correct Simplified forms (and verify the Traditional variant separately). Do not romanize the sung body except the coined hook. Use **祂** for God in the lyric sheet.
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud at speed (tones flattened, syllables blurred, as the AI vocal will render it) and confirm the slurred syllables don't form a vulgar, comic, or sacred-taboo word, and that no de-toned homophone flips the meaning (the most important Mandarin failure mode — see the tone caveat above).

## 5. IDIOMS, IMAGERY & CULTURAL FIT

- **Translate the sense, not the words → native idioms / 成语 where they fit naturally** (e.g. steadfast love → 不离不弃; from death to life → 出死入生). Never jam a 成语 in for flavor if it distorts meaning (§0A).
- **Keep concrete sensory anchors** (bathroom floor at 3 AM, cage door, wedding feast, wine like a river) rendered vividly in Chinese (凌晨三点的地板上). Localize culture-bound **daily-life** anchors (carpool line, diner, prom, credit-card debt) to emotionally equivalent native scenes (上学的路上, 深夜的餐桌, 还不完的账单) — do NOT localize away the **global/heaven imagery** (万民万族 every-tribe, 宝座 throne, 羔羊 Lamb, 婚宴 wedding feast).

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**ZH (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, rhyme natural, frame-appropriate; 神-edition, 耶稣):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / 耶稣是缘由，我们now欢呼起舞！ ……(see note) / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / 君王要再来 — 必定，必定来！

Cleaner final form (no English残留, fully natural):
> Jubiluyah！Jubiluyah！/ 耶稣是缘由，我们此刻欢腾起舞！/ Jubiluyah！Jubiluyah！/ 君王必再来 —— 终必，终必来！

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; **耶稣** consistent name; "Jesus is the reason we are dancing now" → **耶稣是缘由，我们此刻欢腾起舞** — *缘由* = "the reason/cause" (NOT 缘分, the karmic-fate trap of §2); *欢腾起舞* keeps the JOY of dancing but frames it as **holy rejoicing/leaping** (honor-shame-safe, see §1A — exuberant praise, not nightclub 跳舞); "the King is coming back" → **君王必再来** (majestic, the certainty of the Second Coming, fear-power frame: the reigning King returns in authority); rhyme **舞 wǔ / 来 lái** is a loose near-rhyme — acceptable since meaning leads, and the parallel cadence carries it; **终必，终必** ("most surely, surely") replaces the colloquial "somehow, somehow" with the *certainty of His return* (better theology than English vagueness), preserving the double-beat. Tone-meaning screen: 缘由/欢腾/君王/再来 are all disyllabic and unambiguous when sung. Zero offense across Three-Self and house-church.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A):** you need a rhyme/close for the "King is coming back" line.
- ❌ Forced/wrong-frame: *「君王要回来，靠缘分安排！」* — "the King comes back, arranged by *karma/fate*": **缘分 imports Buddhist-folk fate** (§2 banned), steals glory from God's sovereignty (§0A.9), and "安排" is filler-flat. Also de-toned, 缘分 yuánfèn could blur unhelpfully.
- ✅ Meaning-first: *「君王必再来 —— 终必，终必来！」* — real words, sound doctrine (His certain return), keeps the double-beat, parallel cadence, restrained-reverent.
- ✅ Also fine (rhyme dropped, parallelism kept): *「君王正在路上，荣耀里降临！」* — no rhyme with 舞, but vivid, true, and 对仗-balanced. **A true unrhymed line beats a false rhymed one.**

## 10. ADDICTIVENESS / RE-LISTENABILITY PASS (after the album is produced in Mandarin)

Run **three listen passes** on each produced track:
1. **Feeling pass** — does it move you emotionally on first listen? Where does it sag?
2. **Craft pass** — hook clarity, groove, vocal delivery, mix, arrangement.
3. **Replay-triggers pass** — what makes you hit repeat? (earworm hook, a turnaround, a payoff moment, a loop point).

Then **amplify** (tuning the ENGLISH `Style` block §6 and the Suno re-gen prompt ONLY — never doctrine or the locked translation):
- **Hook & delivery** — make the "Jubiluyah" hook front-loaded, sticky, well-placed; tighten the vocal performance note.
- **Groove & pulse** — target a head-nod pocket (~100–128 BPM where the style allows); steady danceable/sway-able feel.
- **Texture & ear-candy** — tasteful fills, counter-melodies, and **culturally resonant instrumentation from §1A** (guzheng/erhu/dizi/pipa accents) for distinctiveness; restrained, not gimmicky.
- **Dynamic arc & payoff** — build → drop → final-chorus lift; a clear emotional peak.
- **Loop point & album flow** — strong intro/outro so the track loops and the album sequences well.
- **Emotional pull & relatability** — lean on §1A's **honor-shame/fear-power frame** and the **ache word 渴慕/乡愁** so the feeling lands for a Chinese listener.

**Re-rate each track + the album BEFORE/AFTER** across: hook strength, groove, replay value, dynamic payoff, emotional pull, loop-ability. Deliver a summary of changes, **why each boosts re-listening**, and the measurable before→after effect. This pass tunes production only — it never edits the locked translation or any doctrine.
