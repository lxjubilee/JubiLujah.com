## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the translator's *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to the meaning & the gospel** — the theology must survive intact (the cross, the resurrection, the name of İsa, repentance, the King's return). Never soften or alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, and open vowels fit the melody. A line a native can't sing comfortably has failed, even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant Turkish** — it must sound like a native Turkish-speaking believer wrote it, not a translated English song. No calques, no English word order; it uses the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture (bathroom floor, cage door, wedding feast). Swap an image only when it is untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo in Turkey (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook preserved** — match the rhyme scheme *only with real, sensible words*; ALWAYS preserve the hook's rhythmic placement.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**. Break a rhyme before you break the meaning or the meter.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** Every line must make complete, natural sense to a native Turkish speaker *with the rhyme ignored.* **Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement.** Ship a clear unrhymed/near-rhymed true line over a forced one. Never invent a word, bend grammar (no wrong suffix order, no broken vowel harmony to fake a rhyme), or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. If no honest rhyme fits, **DROP IT** — use blank verse, assonance, or near-rhyme. Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line (delete the final rhyme word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?).
>
> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** Adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to Turkish culture freely — but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or İsa's exclusivity to be more relatable. **Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form** (1 Cor 1:23). In a Muslim-majority / Muslim-background-believer (MBB) context this law is acutely tested: it is exactly the divine sonship of Christ (**Tanrı'nın Oğlu**) that the surrounding culture rejects, and it is THE flashpoint of the Turkish Bible-translation debates — **contextualize the form, never deny the doctrine** (see §2). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

Before finalizing any line, check it against this list — these are the failure modes a native Turkish-speaking believer instantly hears as wrong.

**1. No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler words.** Don't end a line with a word that's there only to rhyme. Real offenders to avoid: an empty **işte** ("here/so," dropped in as filler), **hani** ("you know") as a bar-filler, a stranded **canım** ("my dear") tacked on for a syllable, generic **sonsuza dek** ("forever") jammed where it doesn't fit, **vesselam** ("and that's that," archaic/Arabic filler), or padding with **ki** ("that," dangling). **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.
**2. Only REAL words & correct grammar — respect suffix order and harmony.** Turkish is agglutinative; suffixes stack in a fixed order (root + plural + possessive + case): *evlerimde* = ev-ler-im-de ("in my houses"). Wrong order or a missing buffer consonant (*-n-/-y-/-s-*) breaks the word (*evde-im* → correct **evdeyim** "I'm at home"). **Vowel harmony is mandatory:** the suffix vowel must match the last root vowel (back/front: *evlerde* but *kapılarda*; rounded: *gözüm* but *kolum*). A harmony-broken suffix (*kitabde* instead of **kitapta**, also note consonant assimilation p↔b) is instantly wrong.
**3. Correct postpositions & set phrases — no calques.** Turkish uses **postpositions and case**, not English prepositions: "wait for you" = *seni bekliyorum* (accusative, no "for"), NOT *senin için bekliyorum*; "believe in" = *-e inanmak* (dative: *Sana inanıyorum*), not *içinde inanmak*; "depend on" = *-e güvenmek / -e bağlı olmak*. Don't calque English idioms ("give up on," "look forward to") literally.
**4. No English-calque or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** Don't render "on the line to heaven" as a phone line (*göğe giden hat* reads as a phone/transport line), or "reach out / connect / signal" as networking metaphors. Recast into native sensory images (a spring/*pınar*, a door/*kapı*, light/*ışık*).
**5. Loanwords/register that misread.** Modern Turkish has Arabic/Persian (Ottoman) and English loans; mixing registers jars. Don't drop English slang (*okey, kanka* "buddy", *süper*) into worship. Beware **Islamic-theological loanwords** that carry mosque/Quranic register — using *Allah, dua etmek* is one thing, but words like *secde* (Islamic prostration), *namaz, abdest, cami, imanı bütün, inşallah/maşallah* as theological terms import Islamic framing — keep Christian-Turkish register (see §1, §2). Also avoid heavy Ottoman archaisms (*Cenâb-ı Hak, Mevlâ*) — these are Islamic-devotional, not Christian.
**6. Agreement / number.** After a number, the noun stays **singular** (*üç kitap*, not *üç kitaplar*). Plural -ler/-lar must harmonize. Don't pluralize where Turkish doesn't.
**7. No grammatically stranded tails.** Turkish is **head-final (Subject-Object-Verb)** — the verb normally ends the clause; don't leave a dangling participle/converb (*-ip, -erek, -ince*) with no main verb, or a possessive (*-ki, -in*) attached to nothing.
**8. Imagery internally consistent.** Don't append an image that contradicts the scene or introduce an object with no antecedent. Match the imagery already in the verse.
**9. Theology over rhyme.** No rhyme word that distorts doctrine. Keep grace from Christ alone; keep luck/fate (**şans** "luck", **kader/kısmet** "fate" — *kader/kısmet* carry heavy Islamic fatalism) strictly subordinate to God's sovereignty; never let "fate/kısmet" stand where the gospel means God's deliberate plan.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** silently delete the final rhyming word — does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use? If no, the line is forced; rewrite it meaning-first, even unrhymed.

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

Turkish-speaking Christianity is **tiny and historically layered**: a small but growing **Protestant/Evangelical** church (largely Turkish MBB and missionary-rooted), the **historic Orthodox** presence (the Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople/İstanbul, Greek Orthodox), and **Armenian Apostolic** and **Syriac (Süryani)** communities (many of whom worship in Armenian/Syriac but live in a Turkish-speaking society), inside an overwhelmingly **Muslim-majority, officially secular** state. Faith in Christ is a sensitive minority position; conversion can carry real social cost. Jubilee/Inspire songs are CCM/evangelical in origin, but the Turkish version must **hold for this whole community** and be **HIGH-sensitivity** toward the surrounding culture.

**Cross-tradition safety rules (Turkish-specific):**
- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** the believing community owns: *Tanrı / Rab / İsa / Mesih / Baba / Kutsal Ruh / lütuf / yücelik / egemenlik / haç / diriliş / Kuzu / Hallelujah / Amin.*
- **Anchor Scripture echoes to the Kutsal Kitap (Yeni Çeviri)** — the most broadly recognized modern Turkish Bible (Protestant/Evangelical standard, widely respected). When a line quotes/echoes Scripture, echo it: John 19:30 ("It is finished") → **"Tamamlandı"**; Rev 22:20 → **"Gel, ya Rab İsa"**.
- **Do NOT import Islamic theological framing or divine names** to "fit in." Christian Turkish has its own register: say **Tanrı** (God) / **Rab** (Lord); use *Allah* with care (see §2). Do **not** import Islamic devotional vocabulary (*namaz, secde, abdest, Mevlâ, Cenâb-ı Hak, inşallah/maşallah* as theology), and never frame the gospel in Quranic terms.
- **Do NOT import practices the source lacks** that would jar this community: no Marian/saint/icon devotion, no church-specific liturgical formulas, no glossolalia naming. Christ-centered, Bible-plain.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Use dignified standard Turkish; no street slang (*kanka, lan, ya* as expletive) in worship lines. Joy and warmth welcome; irreverence not.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** Bride/Bridegroom (**Gelin / Güvey/Damat**) is biblical and fine; keep it pure.
- **MBB security & offense sensitivity (CRITICAL):** in a Muslim-majority context, Christian faith — and especially the divine sonship of İsa — is socially explosive (the Quran explicitly denies God has a son). Keep the doctrine uncompromised (§2), but state the gospel **positively**; let Christ offend, not the lyricist. Never mock Islam.
- **Keep the offense of the cross, remove the offense of bad form** (1 Cor 1:23). Don't dilute sin, blood, repentance, judgment, or Christ's exclusivity — say them in clean, dignified Turkish.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP (the relatability layer)

- **Worldview frame: HONOR–SHAME (high), with a fear–power undercurrent.** Lead here. Foreground images of: **the shamed/outcast restored to honor** (*onur/şeref* — honor is central to Turkish social life); **the Father who runs to embrace** the returning child (the prodigal as scandalous honor-restoring grace); **belonging/adoption into God's family** (*ev/aile/yuva* — home/family/hearth, deeply resonant); God who **covers our shame** rather than exposing it; and, for the fear-power undercurrent, **Christ's authority over every dark power**, light over darkness, deliverance from fear of curse/evil eye (*nazar*)/jinn (frame as Christ's freedom, never endorsing the superstition). De-emphasize purely legal/courtroom debt framings as the primary entry (still true).
- **Taboo / danger images → safe native substitutes:**
  - "Buddy/homeboy Jesus," casual chumminess → **NO.** Warmth with respect; *Rab İsa*, never a back-slapping pal (*kanka İsa* would be offensive).
  - **Dancing** before the Lord — can read as frivolous/immodest in conservative settings, though Turkey has rich folk-dance (*halay*) and Sufi *sema* (avoid any whirling-dervish allusion — it's Islamic-mystical). Prefer **şarkı söylüyoruz / yüceltiyoruz / coşuyoruz** ("we sing/exalt/are filled with joy"); if "dance" must stay, use **sevinçle oynuyoruz** sparingly and flag it.
  - **Wine flowing like a river** — alcohol is sensitive for the Muslim-majority surround. Render the *joy/abundance* (**bolluk, taşan sevinç**) unless it is the explicit biblical communion/wedding-feast image, kept framed as the marriage supper of the Lamb.
  - **Fire** — keep clearly refining/Spirit vs judgment.
  - **Father imagery** — overwhelmingly *tender and protective* (the embrace, the home), the honor-shame goldmine; don't import a harsh/distant note.
- **Native ache word / sacred-longing idiom: hasret (longing/yearning for the absent beloved)** — the great theme of Turkish song; also **özlem** (longing/missing) and **hüzün** (a deep, dignified melancholy). Reach for *hasret/özlem* for the soul's longing for God. **Worship temperature: RESTRAINED + WARM** — Turkish devotional/folk feeling runs deep but dignified and intimate rather than loud and exuberant; aim for heartfelt warmth and *hüzün*-tinged longing resolving into quiet joy, not high-energy hype.
- **Charged vocabulary — handle with care:** **king/kingdom** (Kral/egemenlik) is fine and biblical (Christ's reign) but keep it heavenly, not tied to earthly politics; **warfare/army/"conquering"** imagery is sensitive — prefer Christ's *victory over death and the powers* (spiritual) over militaristic/nationalist conquest language; **homeland/nation/blood/flag** (*vatan, millet, bayrak*) is strongly nationalist-coded — keep the kingdom heavenly and **for every tribe**, never ethno-national. Avoid anything readable as a political statement.
- **Reverence forms required when addressing God:** Turkish has a **T–V distinction**; use the **respectful plural "siz"** form when addressing God (*Senden* informal vs the more reverent register). The vocative particle **"ya"** is natural and dignified for divine address: **ya Rab** ("O Lord"), **ya Rab İsa**. Keep divine address in elevated, respectful register; avoid the casual *sen* paired with slang.
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** **syllabic meter (hece vezni)** — Turkish folk poetry counts syllables (7, 8, 11, etc.) with a *durak* (caesura); and the **koşma/türkü** strophic forms with their AABA/ABAB schemes and recurring refrain. Reach for clean syllabic lines with a strong refrain — this is the native folk-song instinct. (Avoid *aruz*, the Arabic-Persian quantitative meter — it's Ottoman/Islamic-classical and reads archaic/religious-other.)

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY (Turkish-specific)

- **Match syllable count to the melody.** Turkish agglutination makes words long fast; control syllable load by choosing shorter stems and avoiding suffix pile-ups where a melody is tight. Natural compressions singers use: drop the copula *-dir* (it's optional: *güzeldir* → *güzel*); use the bare verb stem in commands/vocatives; the question particle *mı/mi* and conjunction *ve* are light; elide colloquially (*ne oldu* → *n'oldu*, *ne yapıyorsun* → *n'apıyorsun* — use sparingly, only where natural). Never pad with filler particles to fill a bar.
- **Respect Turkish stress.** Turkish stress is **mostly word-final** (on the last syllable), and it **shifts to the new last syllable as suffixes are added** (*ev* → *evLER* → *evlerDE*) — but certain suffixes (*-me/-ma* negation, *-ki*, some) and place-name/adverb patterns pull stress earlier. Put the natural final stress on strong beats; don't force an English-style initial stress. Read every line aloud in rhythm.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes** — Turkish is vowel-rich (a, e, ı, i, o, ö, u, ü); end big held notes on open vowels (*sevinç → -ince* won't sustain; prefer landing on *-a/-e/-o*: *Rabba, sana, yuvaya, İsa*). The hook "Jubiluyah" already ends open (-ya) — exploit it. Avoid landing a long note on a consonant cluster.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot** (lands at 0:00–0:08; keep it there); the chorus arrives by the same bar.
- **Rhyme stance for Turkish: rhyme comes easily — beware the "suffix rhyme" trap.** Because grammatical suffixes repeat, you can rhyme by stacking the same suffix on every line (all ending *-yorum* or *-lar*), which is cheap and monotonous. Folk poetry prizes *ayak* (true stem rhyme) over mere suffix echo. Prefer meaningful rhyme; the moment it forces awkward syntax, an invented word, a filler tail, or broken vowel harmony — **abandon it** (near-rhyme/assonance/blank). Recast the OTHER line of the couplet. A true unrhymed line beats a false rhymed one. Reach for syllabic *hece* meter (§1A).
- **Turkish-specific letters are MANDATORY** (ı/İ, ş, ç, ğ, ö, ü). Suno/TTS pronounce from the text — the dotless **ı** vs dotted **i**, and **ş/ç/ö/ü/ğ**, change the word entirely (*sıkıldım* "I'm bored" vs a wrong spelling). The **ğ (yumuşak ge)** lengthens the preceding vowel (*dağ* ≈ "daa"); write it correctly so the vocal holds the vowel. Always write full Turkish orthography in sung body and titles. Mind **İ/i** vs **I/ı** casing in titles.
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud slurred (as the AI vocal would) and check that blurred/elided syllables across word boundaries don't form a vulgar, comic, or sacred-taboo word (Turkish elisions like *n'apıyorsun* can collide unexpectedly).

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**Turkish (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, honor-shame frame, full orthography, restrained-warm):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / İsa'dır sebebimiz, şimdi yüceltiyoruz! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Kral geri dönüyor — biliyoruz, biliyoruz!

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; **İsa** (consistent Christian name, paired elsewhere as İsa Mesih); "Jesus is the reason we are dancing now" → **İsa'dır sebebimiz, şimdi yüceltiyoruz** — note the §1A taboo flip: literal "dancing" softened to **yüceltiyoruz** ("we exalt/glorify"), reverent and conservative-audience-safe (**I substituted the verb and say so**: dance → exalt, per §1A, because public dance can read frivolous/immodest and the restrained-warm temperature fits better); "the King is coming back" → **Kral geri dönüyor** (heavenly King, not political); the "somehow, somehow" certainty → **biliyoruz, biliyoruz** ("we know, we know") — faith-confident, and a true rhyme with *yüceltiyoruz* via the *-yoruz* present (note: meaningful here, not mere suffix-padding — drop-the-rhyme test: "the King is coming back, we know it" stands true). Full Turkish letters so the vocal pronounces it. Zero offense; no Islamic framing.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A):**
- ❌ Forced/wrong: *"İsa sebep, dans ediyoruz inşallah işte"* — literal **dans** (taboo dance), the Islamic devotional **inşallah** ("God willing," wrong framing) imported just to fill, plus filler **işte**; also missing/odd suffix harmony.
- ✅ Meaning-first: the line above — **yüceltiyoruz** (reverent joy), no Islamic loan, no filler, full orthography, restrained-warm honor-shame tone. **A true, clean, correctly-spelled line beats a forced, syncretized one.**
