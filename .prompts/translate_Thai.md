## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to the meaning & the gospel** — theology must survive intact (the cross, the resurrection, the name of Jesus, repentance, the King's return). Never alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, and open vowels fit the melody. In Thai also weigh **tone-vs-melody** (see §4): a line a native cannot sing comfortably — or that a sung melody renders ambiguous — has failed even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant Thai** — it must sound like a Thai believer wrote it, never like translated English; no calques, no English word order; uses the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture; swap an image only if untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook** — Thai rhyme (สัมผัส, syllable-echo) only with real, sensible words; ALWAYS keep the hook's rhythmic placement.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** Every line must make complete, natural sense to a Thai native *with the rhyme ignored*. Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement. Ship a clear unrhymed/near-rhymed true line over a forced one. Never invent a word, bend grammar, or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. If no honest rhyme fits, **DROP IT.** Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line: delete the final rhyme word — does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?

> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** Adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to Thai culture freely — but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity to be more relatable, and **never import Buddhist or animist concepts** (merit/บุญ, karma/กรรม, rebirth, deities, spirits/ผี, sacred amulets) to "fill" a Christian slot. Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form (1 Cor 1:23). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

1. **No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler.** Thai songwriting padding to avoid: empty tail particles dropped in only for rhythm/rhyme — **นะ / น่ะ / เอย / เอ๋ย / นั่นเอง / เลยจ้า / กันนา** at a reverent line-end; a stray **ฮัลโหล**-style interjection; ending a solemn line on a cute particle (**จ๊ะ / จ้า / เนอะ**) reads chummy and breaks worship register. **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.
2. **Only REAL Thai words & correct grammar.** Thai has no conjugation, but watch **classifier and word-order errors**, mis-segmented compounds, and wrong **serial-verb** order. Don't fuse two words into a non-word to save a syllable. Keep negation **ไม่** before the verb (not stranded). Tense is by context/markers (แล้ว, จะ, กำลัง) — don't drop the one that carries the meaning.
3. **Correct set phrases — no English calques.** Avoid calqued prepositions/idioms: do NOT calque "fall in love with God" as a romantic idiom; "give your heart to Jesus" → use the established **มอบชีวิตแด่พระเยซู / เปิดใจรับพระเยซู**, not a literal *ให้หัวใจ*. "Lift high" → **เทิดทูน / ยกย่อง / สรรเสริญ**, not a literal *ยกขึ้นสูง*. Don't calque English "the reason that…" structure.
4. **No English-calque or tech/telephone images.** "On the line to heaven," "dial / signal / connection to God," "download grace," "recharge my soul" — all misfire in Thai worship; render the relationship plainly (**ทูลต่อพระองค์**, *speak to Him*).
5. **Loanwords/register that misread (see §2E below).** English loans that flip register or meaning in Thai: **แฟน** (in everyday Thai = boyfriend/girlfriend, NOT "fan/supporter") — never call worshippers *แฟน* of Jesus; **ไฟ** ("fire") often reads as literal fire/electrical or slang "ไฟแรง" hype — use **ไฟแห่งพระวิญญาณ** explicitly for Pentecostal fire; **เจ้า** alone can read as a Buddhist/animist *spirit-lord (เจ้าที่/เจ้าป่า)* — say **พระเจ้า / องค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้า** for God.
6. **(Thai is uninflected — gender/number agreement N/A.)** Instead: keep the **pronoun/honorific level consistent** within a song; do not switch between intimate and royal registers for God mid-verse.
7. **No grammatically stranded tails** — every relative clause (**ที่ / ซึ่ง**) must resolve; don't leave a dangling **ของ** (of-) with no head noun.
8. **Imagery internally consistent** — no contradicting/antecedent-less image (don't introduce snow/winter scenes alien to Thai daily life unless they are the song's deliberate global/heaven imagery; don't pair "cold floor" with a sudden "blaze").
9. **Theology over rhyme** — no rhyme word distorts doctrine; **never let "merit/luck/fate" (บุญ / โชค / ดวง / กรรม)** stand as the source of blessing — blessing/grace comes from **พระเจ้า**. Keep luck/chance subordinate to God's sovereignty.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** delete the final syllable-rhyme; if the rest still says something true and natural and the dropped word is one you'd actually use, keep it — otherwise rewrite meaning-first, rhyme or no rhyme.

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

Thailand is **~93–95% Theravada Buddhist**, ~4–5% Muslim (mainly the deep South), and **Christians only ~1%** — Catholic, mainline Protestant (Church of Christ in Thailand), and a fast-growing Evangelical/Pentecostal/charismatic stream (incl. Hope of Bangkok and Thai branches of global movements). Because the church is tiny and visible, lyrics must **hold for the whole Thai Body of Christ** and avoid any wording that mocks or appropriates Buddhism (which would harm the church's standing) — while staying unmistakably Christian.

- **Shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary all traditions own:** *พระเจ้า, องค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้า, พระเยซู, พระคริสต์, พระบิดา, พระวิญญาณบริสุทธิ์, พระคุณ, ความรอด, ไม้กางเขน, การฟื้นคืนพระชนม์, อาเมน, ฮาเลลูยา.* Anchor Scripture echoes to the **Thai Standard Version (THSV, พระคริสตธรรมคัมภีร์ ฉบับมาตรฐาน)** — the most broadly recognized contemporary Thai Bible (the older **Thai King James / ฉบับ 1971** is also widely respected). John 19:30 → **"สำเร็จแล้ว"**; Rev 22:20 → **"ข้าแต่พระเยซูเจ้า ขอเสด็จมาเถิด"**.
- **No tradition-specific imports the source lacks** — no Marian/saint devotion, no one-church liturgical formulas, no glossolalia naming. Christ-centered, Bible-plain — the common ground.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Thai has a **sacred/royal register (ราชาศัพท์)** used for kings and for God: divine actions take **ทรง** (e.g., *ทรงรัก* "He loves," *ทรงครอบครอง* "He reigns), God "comes" = **เสด็จมา**, "says/speaks" = **ตรัส**, "gives" = **ประทาน**. Use this register toward God to signal reverence; do not address God with casual everyday verbs. No vulgar/street-slang anywhere.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** Bride/Bridegroom (**เจ้าสาว / เจ้าบ่าว**) is biblical — keep it pure, never romantic-pop longing. Never call God "แฟน" (boyfriend/girlfriend).
- **Gender/inclusive language & family:** defer to the Thai church's plain, Scripture-anchored norms.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP  (the relatability layer)

- **Worldview frame — lead with FEAR–POWER + HONOR–SHAME** (Theravada/animist Thailand lives with spirit-fear, merit-anxiety, and face/honor). Foreground: **Christ's authority over every dark power and spirit, light swallowing darkness, freedom from fear of ผี/karma/fate; the Father who receives the outcast and restores honor/belonging (กลับบ้าน, "coming home"); peace (สันติสุข) that no merit-striving can buy.** Frame salvation as *rest from striving and rescue from fear*, not primarily as courtroom acquittal (guilt-innocence is secondary in this culture).
- **Taboo/danger images → safe substitutes:**
  - *Buddhist concepts* — never use **บุญ (merit), กรรม (karma), ทำบุญ, นิพพาน (nirvana), เวียนว่ายตายเกิด (rebirth), เทพ/เทวดา (deities), ผี (spirits) approvingly** to describe Christian blessing/salvation. For "blessing" use **พระพร**; for "eternal life" use **ชีวิตนิรันดร์**.
  - *"Buddy/homeboy Jesus"* — Thai reverence forbids matey God-talk; keep the royal register, never slangy "เพื่อนซี้พระเยซู."
  - *Dancing before the Lord* — public ecstatic dancing reads oddly in restrained Thai worship; prefer **ชื่นชมยินดี / โห่ร้องสรรเสริญ / เต้นโลด (rejoice/leap for joy)** unless the track is deliberately celebratory; if you keep "dance," frame it as **joyful celebration before God**, and SAY you did.
  - *Fire* — distinguish redemptive **ไฟแห่งพระวิญญาณ** from judgment fire; bare *ไฟ* can read literal.
  - *The head / feet* — Thai body taboo: the head is sacred, feet are low. Avoid imagery of touching/patting heads or of God "at our feet" in a degrading sense; "bow at His feet" as worship (**กราบ / หมอบกราบ**) is good and resonant.
- **Native ache word / sacred-longing idiom:** **โหยหา** (deep yearning/longing) and **ใจที่ว้าเหว่** (a lonely, aching heart) — use for sacred longing for God. **สงบสุข / สันติสุข** (settled peace) is the deep Thai felt-need to resolve.
- **Worship temperature: gentle / reverent.** Even joyful tracks stay warm and dignified, not brash; Thai congregational worship prizes sweetness (**นุ่มนวล**) and sincerity over volume.
- **Charged vocabulary to handle with care:** anything that could read as mocking Buddhism or the **monarchy** (royal register is real reverence, not satire — never play with ราชาศัพท์ ironically); avoid political/color-coded slogans; "kingdom/king" is fine and resonant (Thailand reveres kingship) — use it for Christ's majesty.
- **Reverence/honorific forms required when addressing God:** the royal verbs **ทรง / เสด็จ / ตรัส / ประทาน / โปรด**, the pronoun **พระองค์** ("He/You" for God, reverent), worshipper self-reference **ข้าพระองค์ / ลูก** (humble). Keep one consistent level per song.
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** Thai verse lives on **สัมผัส** (chained syllable-rhyme linking line-ends to mid-points of the next line) and on **vowel-length/euphony**, NOT forced English end-rhyme; reach for gentle internal echo and balanced phrasing rather than hard AABB.

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY ( Thai-specific )

- **TONE-vs-MELODY caveat (CRITICAL — Thai is a 5-tone language: mid, low, falling, high, rising).** Lexical tone distinguishes words (เสือ "tiger" / เสื้อ "shirt" / เสื่อ "mat"; ใหม่ "new" / ไม้ "wood" / ไหม้ "burn"). **A sung melody overrides the speaking tone**, so the listener cannot rely on tone to disambiguate — this creates real homophone ambiguity in singing. Mitigation: (1) **choose words whose meaning stays clear from context even when the tone is flattened by the tune**; (2) avoid placing a tone-minimal-pair where mishearing flips the meaning (especially near sacred words — make sure a flattened **พระเจ้า / ไม้กางเขน / รอด** can't be misheard as something profane or comic); (3) prefer longer/disambiguated forms over bare monosyllables on exposed notes; (4) Thai song tradition already accepts melody-over-tone, so lean on **clear phrasing and context**, not on tone, to carry meaning.
- **Match syllable count.** Thai is largely monosyllabic, so it often FITS English tightly; compress by dropping optional particles, not by inventing words. Common natural reductions singers use: **ก็** (then), polite **ครับ/ค่ะ** dropped in song, **ที่จะ → จะ**. No filler particles to pad a bar (see §0A.1).
- **Stress/rhythm:** Thai has no strong lexical stress; rhythm rides **vowel length** (short vs long vowels) and tone contour. Put **long-vowel / open syllables on sustained or belted notes** (e.g., **-า, -ู, -ี, -อ**: *พระเจ้า, รอด, สิริ, ฮาเลลูยา*); avoid landing a long held note on a checked (dead) syllable ending in -ก/-ด/-บ.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot;** the chorus arrives on the same bar.
- **Rhyme is OPTIONAL and last; DON'T FORCE end-rhyme.** Thai poetry uses chained **สัมผัส** (syllable echo) and euphony, not English-style hard end-rhyme — forcing AABB produces filler particles. Prefer recasting the OTHER line of a couplet; reach for gentle internal/echo rhyme where it fits naturally.
- **Diacritics/script mandatory** — Thai is written with **no spaces between words**; tone marks (่ ้ ๊ ๋), vowels, and the **karan/silencer (์)** are pronounced by TTS/Suno from the text, so spelling must be complete and correct (e.g., **พระวิญญาณบริสุทธิ์**, not a stripped form). Segment the sung text the way it should phrase, but keep standard Thai orthography (spaces only at phrase/clause breaks, as Thai writing does).
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud as a flattened AI vocal would and check that blurred/toneless syllables don't form a vulgar or comic word (Thai has many tone-pair traps, e.g., near-homophones of crude words) — recast if so.

## 5. IDIOMS, IMAGERY & CULTURAL FIT

- **Translate the sense, not the words** → native Thai idioms. "Permission slip / I don't need permission" → **ไม่ต้องขออนุญาตใคร**; "the bell on Sunday morning" → keep church-bell imagery (**เสียงระฆังโบสถ์**, real for Thai churches).
- **Keep concrete sensory anchors** (bathroom floor at 3 AM, cage door, wedding feast), rendered vividly in Thai. **Localize culture-bound American daily-life anchors** (carpool line, diner, prom, credit-card debt) to emotionally equivalent Thai scenes (**รถติดตอนเช้า / โต๊ะข้าวในครัว / หนี้สินรัดตัว**) — but **do NOT localize away the global/heaven imagery** (every-tribe, throne, Lamb, wedding feast stay).
- Render warmth and family belonging strongly — the honor-shame "coming home to the Father" image lands hard in Thai (**กลับบ้าน, พ่อออกมารับ**).

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**Thai (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, worldview-frame-appropriate):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / พระเยซูคือเหตุที่เราเปรมปรีดิ์วันนี้! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / องค์กษัตริย์จะเสด็จกลับมา — แน่แท้ แน่นอน!

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; **พระเยซู** consistent; "the reason we are dancing now" rendered as **เหตุที่เราเปรมปรีดิ์วันนี้** ("the reason we rejoice today") — keeps the joy but trades the exposed "dance" verb for **เปรมปรีดิ์/ชื่นชมยินดี** to suit restrained Thai worship (substitution flagged); "the King is coming back" → **องค์กษัตริย์จะเสด็จกลับมา** uses the royal verb **เสด็จ** (reverent) and the revered **กษัตริย์**; line-ends rest on open vowels (**-ี้ / -อน**); no Buddhist concept; reverent throughout.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A):**
- ❌ Forced/wrong-frame: *"…พระเยซูให้ **บุญ** เรา เฮง ๆ นะจ๊ะ!"* — uses Buddhist **บุญ (merit)** + luck word **เฮง** + cutesy particle **นะจ๊ะ**: wrong theology, wrong register, filler particle.
- ✅ Meaning-first: *"…พระเยซูประทาน **พระคุณ** เรา ชื่นใจ!"* — **ประทาน (royal "gives") พระคุณ (grace, not merit/luck)**, reverent, real words, true doctrine; rhyme dropped in favor of meaning. **A true clear line beats a forced one.**
