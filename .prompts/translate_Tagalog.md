## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to the meaning & the gospel** — theology must survive intact (the cross, the resurrection, the name of Jesus, repentance, the King's return). Never alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, and open vowels fit the melody. A line a native cannot sing comfortably has failed even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant Filipino** — it must sound like a Filipino believer wrote it, never like translated English; no calques, no English word order; uses the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture; swap an image only if untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook** — Filipino rhyme (tugma) only with real, sensible words; ALWAYS keep the hook's rhythmic placement.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** Every line must make complete, natural sense to a Filipino native *with the rhyme ignored*. Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement. Ship a clear unrhymed/near-rhymed true line over a forced one. Never invent a word, bend grammar, or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. If no honest rhyme fits, **DROP IT.** Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line: delete the final rhyme word — does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?

> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** Adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to Filipino culture freely — but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity to be more relatable, and **never import folk-Catholic superstition or non-trinitarian usages** (see §1) to "fill" a Christian slot. Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form (1 Cor 1:23). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

1. **No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler.** Filipino padding to avoid: dumping a vowel-rhyme word with no meaning just to hit **-an / -in / -on / -ana / -aya** tugma; stray emphatics **nga / naman / kasi / eh / ba / daw** stuffed at a reverent line-end for rhythm; over-using **-an/-in** suffixed nonce words. **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.
2. **Only REAL Filipino words & correct grammar.** Watch **focus/trigger affixes** (mag-/-um-/ma-/i-/-in/-an) — wrong affix breaks the verb (e.g., *sinamba* vs *sumamba* vs *magsamba* are different); keep the **ang/ng/sa** marker system correct; **linker -ng/na** must attach correctly (*magandang balita*, not *maganda balita*); don't drop the aspect that carries tense (naka-/nag-/mag-). Don't invent affixed non-words to fit a beat.
3. **Correct set phrases — no English calques.** "Give your heart to Jesus" → established **ialay/ibigay ang buhay kay Hesus / tanggapin si Hesus**, not a flat literal *ibigay ang puso*. "Lift high" → **itaas / dakilain / itanghal**, not literal *iangat nang mataas*. Don't calque "the reason that…" (use **kaya / dahil / sapagkat**). Don't calque "reach out," "let go," "stand for" word-for-word.
4. **No English-calque or tech/telephone images.** "On the line to heaven," "signal/connection to God," "download grace," "recharge my soul" misfire; render the relationship plainly (**dalangin kay Hesus / tumawag sa Panginoon**).
5. **Loanwords/register that misread (see §2E below).** Filipino freely uses Spanish- and English-origin words, but watch register: heavy **Taglish/English code-switching** ("worship si Lord," "feeling blessed") reads casual — **keep the sung worship register clean Filipino**; **"crush/jowa/syota"** (romantic-crush slang) must never touch God; **"grasya"** can colloquially mean *luck/good fortune* — use **biyaya** for grace; deep-Tagalog archaic words (**lupalop, sintang, irog**) can read either poetic-beautiful or stilted — use only if natural. Never drop street slang (**petmalu, lodi, charot, ganern**) into a reverent line.
6. **(Filipino marks number by plural particle mga, not adjective agreement — agreement is N/A.)** Instead: keep **honorific register consistent** — the respect particles **po/opo** and reverent pronouns (**Kayó/Ninyó/Inyó** for God) must not flip mid-song to casual **ka/mo/iyo**.
7. **No grammatically stranded tails** — every **ng/nang** and linker **-ng/na** must resolve; don't strand a **na/-ng** modifier with no head, and don't leave a **ang**-phrase with no predicate.
8. **Imagery internally consistent** — no contradicting/antecedent-less image (don't pair "cold floor" with a sudden "blaze"; don't introduce an object with no antecedent).
9. **Theology over rhyme** — no rhyme word distorts doctrine; **never let "luck/fate" (suwerte / kapalaran / tadhana)** stand as the source of blessing — grace comes from **Diyos**. Keep luck/chance subordinate to God's sovereignty. Don't let folk **"swerte/malas"** thinking creep in.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** delete the final tugma; if the rest still says something true and natural and the dropped word is one you'd actually use, keep it — otherwise rewrite meaning-first, rhyme or no rhyme.

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

The Philippines is **~79% Roman Catholic** with a **large, fast-growing evangelical/Pentecostal/born-again stream** (Assemblies of God, CCF, Victory, Jesus Is Lord, etc.), plus Iglesia Filipina Independiente (Aglipayan) and others. **Iglesia ni Cristo (INC) exists and is non-trinitarian — do NOT adopt its distinctive usages or denial of Christ's deity.** The Filipino version must **hold for the whole trinitarian Filipino Body of Christ** while staying unmistakably Christian and orthodox.

- **Shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary all traditions own:** *Diyos, Panginoon, Hesus / Hesukristo, Kristo, Ama, Espiritu Santo, biyaya, kaligtasan, krus, muling pagkabuhay, Amen, Aleluya, Hosana.* Anchor Scripture echoes to the **Magandang Balita Biblia (MBB, the most broadly recognized contemporary Filipino Bible)** and **Ang Biblia (ABTAG, the classic/formal text)**. John 19:30 → **"Naganap na"** (MBB) / **"Naipatupad na"**; Rev 22:20 → **"Halina, Panginoong Hesus!"**
- **No tradition-specific imports the source lacks** — no Marian devotion (no "Mama Mary," no rosary/Santo Niño/saint petitions), no one-church liturgical formulas, no glossolalia naming, and **no Iglesia-ni-Cristo phrasings**. Christ-centered, Bible-plain, trinitarian — the common ground.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Filipino reverence is grammatical via the **po/opo** respect particles and **plural/honorific pronouns** for God (**Kayó** "You" reverent, **Ninyó/Inyó**, **Siyá/Kanyá** capitalized). Capitalize divine pronouns. **Code-switching ("Taglish") is everyday speech but keep the worship register clean Filipino** — Taglish is fine in casual talk, not in a sung worship line. No vulgar/street-slang anywhere.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** Bride/Bridegroom (**Babaeng Ikakasal / Lalaking Ikakasal**, or **Nobya / Nobyo**) is biblical — keep it pure, never romantic-pop/"crush" longing.
- **Gender/inclusive language & family:** defer to the Filipino church's plain, Scripture-anchored norms; family closeness and respect for elders are strengths to lean on.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP  (the relatability layer)

- **Worldview frame — lead with HONOR–SHAME + GUILT–INNOCENCE.** Filipino culture runs on **hiya (shame/sense of propriety), utang na loob (debt of gratitude), kapwa (shared inner self/belonging), and pakikisama (smooth relationship)**, alongside a strong Catholic conscience of sin and forgiveness. Foreground: **utang na loob to Christ who paid our debt; the shamed/outcast restored to honor and family (tinanggap muli, pinatawad); belonging in God's family (pamilya ng Diyos); the Father who runs to embrace the prodigal.** Guilt-innocence (debt paid, forgiveness, freedom) is also strong and welcome — the debt-of-gratitude image marries both frames beautifully.
- **Taboo/danger images → safe substitutes:**
  - *Folk-Catholic superstition* — never use **suwerte/malas (luck/bad luck), anting-anting (amulets), kapalaran/tadhana (fate)** approvingly for Christian blessing; "blessing" = **biyaya / pagpapala**; "eternal life" = **buhay na walang hanggan**.
  - *Marian/saint devotion* — no "Mama Mary," Santo Niño, rosary, or saint-petition imagery the source lacks.
  - *INC non-trinitarian usages* — never phrase Jesus as merely man or deny the Trinity.
  - *"Barkada/buddy Jesus"* — keep reverent **Panginoon/Hesus** with po-register; don't make Jesus a casual *barkada/tropa*.
  - *Dancing before the Lord* — Filipinos are warmly expressive and **dance/celebration before God reads JOYFULLY and is welcome** (fiesta culture); keep it pure celebration, not club/sensual. (This is a culture where you usually KEEP "sumayaw.")
  - *Fire* — distinguish redemptive **apoy ng Espiritu** from judgment fire; bare *apoy* can read literal.
- **Native ache word / sacred-longing idiom:** **pananabik** (longing/yearning, eager anticipation) and **hangad / mithiin** (deep desire); **sumpungin ng puso / hapdi ng puso** for heart-ache. Use **pananabik** for sacred longing for God. The deep felt-need is **kapayapaan (peace)** and **pag-uwi / pagbabalik sa piling ng Ama** (coming home to the Father).
- **Worship temperature: warm-exuberant.** Filipino worship is joyful, expressive, communal, fiesta-hearted — lean into celebration, clapping, and big choruses, while keeping reverence.
- **Charged vocabulary to handle with care:** **denominational** flashpoints (Marian terms vs INC usages — stay neutral/orthodox-trinitarian); avoid partisan political slogans; **kingdom/king (Hari, Kaharian)** is fine and resonant for Christ. Keep nationally neutral (don't favor one region/dialect's slang).
- **Reverence/honorific forms required when addressing God:** respect particle **po** woven naturally (e.g., *Panginoon ko po, Salamat po*), reverent pronouns **Kayó / Ninyó / Inyó / Siyá**, worshipper humility (**lingkod, anak**). Keep one consistent honorific level per song; don't slide into bare **ka/mo**.
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** Filipino **tugma at sukat** (rhyme-and-measure, the balagtasan/awit tradition often in 8- or 12-syllable lines), **talinghaga** (metaphor), and **assonant vowel-rhyme** (Tagalog rhymes richly on final vowels/syllables) — reach for natural tugma and balanced measure, but never force it past meaning.

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY ( Filipino-specific )

- **Match syllable count.** Filipino words can run long; compress with the **natural elisions Filipino singers actually use**: **ang → 'ng** after a vowel (*puso ko'ng* informal), **na → 'ng / -ng linker**, **ko, mo, ka** clitics ride the previous word, **sa'yo** (sa iyo), **'di** (hindi), **'wag** (huwag), **'yan/'yon** (iyan/iyon), **'kaw** (ikaw), **kita** (collapses ko + ikaw). No filler particles to pad a bar (§0A.1).
- **Respect Filipino stress.** Tagalog stress is phonemic (**baga** vs **bagá**, **buhay** vs **buháy**); put stressed syllables on strong beats and don't force an unnatural accent. Read every line aloud in rhythm. Most words stress the penult or final.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes** — Filipino is vowel-rich (a, e, i, o, u all open); land big held notes on open vowels (e.g., *Diyos, biyaya, Aleluya, kaluwalhatian, kalayaan*); avoid landing a long note on a final glottal-stop or closed cluster.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot;** the chorus arrives on the same bar.
- **Rhyme is OPTIONAL and last; Tagalog rhymes richly so use tugma WHERE NATURAL** — but the moment it forces an empty word, an odd affix, or distorted meaning, **abandon it** (near-rhyme/assonance/blank). Prefer recasting the OTHER line of a couplet. Reach for the **tugma at sukat** measure where it fits.
- **Diacritics:** standard written Filipino does **not** mark stress diacritics in normal text, so no special diacritic burden — but spell words fully and correctly (ñ in Spanish-origin names where used). TTS/Suno pronounce from the text; keep clean orthography.
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud as a slurred AI vocal would and check that blurred syllables don't form a vulgar/comic word (Tagalog has crude near-homophones, e.g., around p-/t-/k- syllables) — recast if so.

## 5. IDIOMS, IMAGERY & CULTURAL FIT

- **Translate the sense, not the words** → native Filipino idioms. "I don't need permission" → **'di na kailangang humingi ng pahintulot**; "the bell on Sunday morning" → **kampana tuwing Linggo ng umaga** (church bells are universal in the PH).
- **Keep concrete sensory anchors** (bathroom floor at 3 AM, cage door, wedding feast), rendered vividly in Filipino. **Localize culture-bound American daily-life anchors** (carpool line, diner, prom, credit-card debt) to emotionally equivalent Filipino scenes (**trapik sa umaga / hapag-kainan / baon-baong utang / jeepney ride**) — but **do NOT localize away the global/heaven imagery** (every-tribe, throne, Lamb, wedding feast stay).
- Lean on **utang na loob, kapwa, and coming home to the Father/family** — the honor-shame frame lands powerfully (**inuwi ng Ama, niyakap muli, pinatawad**).

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**Filipino (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, worldview-frame-appropriate):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Si Hesus ang dahilan kaya tayo'y sumasayaw! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Ang Hari'y babalik na — tiyak, tiyak, darating!

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; **Hesus** consistent; "the reason we are dancing now" → **ang dahilan kaya tayo'y sumasayaw** — and because **dancing/celebration before God is JOYFULLY welcome in fiesta-hearted Filipino worship, "sumasayaw" is KEPT** (no substitution needed, per §1A); "the King is coming back" → **Ang Hari'y babalik na** (Hari = King, reverent, future **na/babalik**); elision **tayo'y** (tayo ay) and **Hari'y** (Hari ay) are natural singer contractions; line-ends on open vowels (**-ayaw / -ating**); warm-exuberant; no superstition, fully trinitarian.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A):**
- ❌ Forced/wrong-frame: *"…Si Hesus nagbigay ng **suwerte**, **swabe** lang, charot!"* — uses **suwerte (luck)** + slang **swabe/charot**: wrong theology (blessing reduced to luck), wrong register, slang fillers.
- ✅ Meaning-first: *"…Si Hesus nagbigay ng **biyaya**, pag-ibig na wagas!"* — **biyaya (grace, not luck)** + **pag-ibig na wagas (pure love)**, reverent, real words, true doctrine; clean tugma. **A true clear line beats a forced one.**

## 10. ADDICTIVENESS / RE-LISTENABILITY PASS (after the album is produced in Filipino)

Run the full §10 protocol on the produced Filipino tracks. **Three listen passes:** (1) *feeling* — does it move a Filipino listener on first hearing? (2) *craft* — hook clarity, vocal delivery, mix; (3) *replay-triggers* — what makes you want it again? Then amplify:
- **Hook & delivery** — make "Jubilujah" land instantly and singable for Filipino mouths.
- **Groove & pulse** — favor a head-nod pocket (~100–128 BPM where the style allows); OPM/worship loves a big melodic, sing-along groove.
- **Texture & ear-candy** — tasteful fills; optional Filipino instrument colors (acoustic/rondalla strings, fiesta brass/percussion) for resonance.
- **Dynamic arc & payoff** — clear build to a big, communal chorus/bridge lift (Filipino worship loves the swell).
