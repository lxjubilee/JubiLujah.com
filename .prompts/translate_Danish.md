## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the translator's *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to meaning & the gospel** — theology must survive intact (the cross, the resurrection, the name of Jesus, repentance, the King's return); never alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, and open vowels fit the original melody. A line a native can't sing comfortably has failed, even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant Danish** — sounds like a native Danish believer wrote it; no calques, no English word order (Danish is **verb-second / V2** — the finite verb is the second element in main clauses, with subject inversion after a fronted element); uses the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture; swap an image only if untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook** — match the rhyme scheme ONLY with real, sensible words; always keep the hook's rhythmic placement.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**. Break a rhyme before you break the meaning or the meter.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** every line must make complete, natural sense to a native Danish speaker *with the rhyme ignored.* **Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement.** Ship a clear unrhymed/near-rhymed true line over a forced one. Never invent a word, bend grammar, or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. If no honest rhyme fits, **DROP IT.** Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line (delete the final rhyme word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?).
>
> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to the culture freely — but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity to be more relatable. Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form (1 Cor 1:23). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

These are the exact failure modes that make a Danish believer instantly hear "translated English." Check every line.

**1. No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler words.** Never end a line with a word that exists *only* to rhyme. Real Danish offenders to watch: stranded particles **"jo," "vel," "nu," "da," "nok," "altså"** jammed at line-end to fill a beat; archaic/elevated tags **"forvist," "fordum," "sandelig," "ak"** dropped in only for rhyme; throwaway **"o Gud"** as a rhyme-plug. **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.

**2. Only REAL words & correct forms.** Danish verbs do **not** conjugate for person (one form per tense: *jeg er, du er, han er*; *jeg synger, vi synger*) — don't invent person-endings. Past tense and past participle must be right (*synge → sang → sunget*; *gå → gik → gået*; never *"gåede," "syngede"*). Keep the past participle after *har/havde* (*"har frelst," "har sunget"* — not *"har frelse"*). The Danish **passive** has two forms (*-s* form *frelses* vs *blive*-passive *bliver frelst*) — use the natural one, don't calque.

**3. Correct prepositions & set phrases — no calques.** Danish prepositions don't map 1:1 to English. Caught-style traps: *"vente **for** dig"* (English "wait for") → **"vente **på** dig"**; *"tænke **om** dig"* (remembering sense) → **"tænke **på** dig"**; *"afhængig **på**"* → **"afhængig **af**"**; *"stole **på**"* (trust/rely on) NOT *"stole i"*; the English calque *"Det giver ingen mening"* is actually idiomatic Danish (fine), but *"Det gør ikke mening"* is wrong → **"Det giver ikke mening."** *"Jeg savner dig"* (I miss you) NOT *"jeg misser dig."*

**4. No English-calque word order or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** Danish is V2: a fronted adverb/object/time-phrase forces the verb second and the subject **inverts** (*"Nu **synger vi**"* not *"Nu vi synger"*; *"I dag **kommer Kongen**"*). Never copy English SVO after a fronted element. Don't render "reach out" literally; don't render "on the line to heaven" as a phone line → **"jeg råber mod himlen."**

**5. Loanwords/register that misread in Danish.** False friends: English **"eventually"** ≠ Danish **eventuelt** (= *possibly*; "eventually" → *til sidst/efterhånden*); **"actual"** ≠ **aktuel** (= *current/topical*; → *virkelig/faktisk*); **"gift"** = Danish **gift** (= *married* OR *poison*! — never the English "present" sense → use *gave*); **"frelst"** in secular Danish slang can mean "self-righteous / holier-than-thou" (be aware — context as Savior-language keeps it sacred, but never use it sarcastically); **"slot"** = Danish *castle/palace*. Don't drop casual Anglicisms (*fedt, cool, chille, vibe*) or slang into a reverent line. Keep register elevated, not hverdagsdansk-casual.

**6. Adjective & noun agreement must hold.** Danish has two genders (**fælleskøn / en**-words and **intetkøn / et**-words) and the adjective inflects: *"en hellig Gud"* (en-word) vs *"et helligt ord"* (et-word, +t) vs plural/definite *"den hellige Gud / de hellige"* (+e). A rhyme-chosen adjective that breaks gender/number is instantly wrong — fix the rhyme partner, not the grammar. The **definite suffix** is attached (*konge → kongen, nåde → nåden*) — don't strand a free-floating article.

**7. No grammatically stranded tails.** Don't strand a dangling genitive or relative clause with no head. Every word needs a grammatical home in the line or the next.

**8. Imagery internally consistent.** Don't append an image that fights the scene (cold floor + a sudden *flamme/glød* only for rhyme) or introduce an object with no antecedent. Match the picture already established (the dawn = *daggryet*, not a random *blomst*).

**9. Theology over rhyme.** No rhyme word may distort doctrine. **"Held/lykke"** = luck/fortune/happiness — keep luck/chance subordinate to God's sovereignty (prefer **"velsignelse," "nåde," "frelse"**); don't imply salvation is luck. **"Skæbne"** (fate/destiny) must never replace God's providence. Grace comes from Christ, never circumstance.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** silently delete the final rhyme word. Does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use? If no — rewrite meaning-first, even with no rhyme.

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

Denmark is **one of the most secular societies on earth**, yet carries a deep Lutheran cultural backbone: the **Folkekirken** (the Evangelical-Lutheran Church of Denmark, the established national church to which a large majority of Danes still belong and which frames life-rituals: baptism, confirmation, weddings, funerals, and the beloved hymn-singing of Christmas and **Grundtvig's** tradition). Active, believing faith is concentrated in low-church revival movements within the Folkekirken (**Indre Mission**, the Grundtvigian stream) and in the small **frikirke** stream — **Pinsekirken (Pentecostal), Baptistkirken, Apostolsk Kirke, evangelical free churches** — plus a small **Roman Catholic** minority. Jubilee/Inspire songs are CCM/evangelical in origin, but the Danish version must **hold for the whole Danish Body of Christ** and read with dignity in a secular, irony-comfortable culture that distrusts earnest piety performed too loudly.

- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** all traditions own: *Gud, Herren, Jesus, Kristus, Faderen, Helligånden, nåde, herlighed, Guds rige, korset, opstandelsen, Lammet, Halleluja, Amen, frelse.* Anchor Scripture echoes to the standard Bible: **Den Danske Bibel — the authorized translation of 1992 (Bibelen 1992 / den autoriserede oversættelse)**, the broadly recognized Danish Bible across Folkekirken and most churches (the **Bibelen 2020** modern paraphrase exists but is less authoritative for quotation). Iconic phrasings: John 19:30 → **"Det er fuldbragt"**; Rev 22:20 → **"Kom, Herre Jesus"**.
- **No tradition-specific imports the source lacks.** No Marian devotion, no saint/relic veneration, no high-liturgical formulas beyond what the source carries, no charismatic glossolalia naming. Christ-centered and Bible-plain is the common ground.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Address God respectfully; capitalize divine pronouns where it aids reverence (*Du, Din, Dig, Han, Hans, Ham* when referring to God — common in Danish Christian print). Danish has no formal/informal "you" split in modern use — **"du"** to God is normal and warm; capitalize *Du* for reverence. No slang anywhere.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** Bride/Bridegroom (*Bruden / Brudgommen*) is biblical and fine; keep it pure.
- **Gender/inclusive-language & family handling:** Denmark is highly egalitarian; the Folkekirken is broadly progressive while Indre Mission/frikirke are traditional. **Defer to THIS community's norms** — choose the broadest, most natural Scripture-plain form; do not import the source culture's gender debates; God keeps *Han/Faderen*.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP (the relatability layer)

- **Lead with the culture's worldview frame: guilt–innocence** (Lutheran heritage) — *skyld* (guilt/debt), *nåde* (grace), *tilgivelse* (forgiveness), *retfærdiggørelse* (justification). **Foreground these images:** sin as debt → **debt paid / gælden betalt**; the conscience accused → **acquitted (frikendt)**; declared righteous; a clean conscience (*en ren samvittighed*); peace with God. Note the strong Danish/Grundtvigian emphasis on **grace as warmth, life, and belonging** ("menneske først, kristen så" — human first, then Christian) and on God's love expressed in everyday, down-to-earth life — grace as **rest and acceptance, not performance** lands powerfully in a secular, achievement-weary culture. The cross as the place guilt is settled is the deepest entry.
- **Taboo/danger images → safe native substitutes:**
  - **"Buddy/homeboy Jesus"** / over-casual *"hej Jesus, makker"* → keep warmth but reverent: *"Jesus, min ven"* / *"min Frelser"* — never matey slang. Danish irony/distrust of earnestness means gushing piety reads as fake or naive; **understated sincerity is far more credible.**
  - **Loud triumphalism / hard-sell evangelism** clashes with **Janteloven** (the "don't think you're anything special" social code) and Danish *hygge*/modesty — avoid boastful, superior, pushy framing; warmth, honesty, and quiet conviction land.
  - **Triumphalist warfare / "conquering nations"** — prefer victory **over sin and death** (*sejr over synd og død*) to militaristic/nationalistic conquest.
  - **King/Kingdom** is natural and warm (*Konge, Guds rige*) — Denmark has the world's oldest monarchy, deeply loved, so royal imagery reads warm, not awkward; *Guds rige* is core vocabulary.
  - **"Dancing before the Lord"** — *danse* is fine in frikirke/Pentecostal settings; in Folkekirken worship it's less native (restrained, hymn-centered) — *juble, prise, synge af glæde* carry the energy broadly.
- **Native ache word / sacred-longing idiom: "længsel"** (deep longing/yearning) — reach for it where the EN aches for God or heaven (*"længsel efter Dig"*). **"vemod"** is a tender, beautiful melancholy/wistfulness woven through Danish song and hymnody — useful for lament-tinged hope. Set against this the warm Danish ideal of **"hygge"** (cozy, safe togetherness) and **"tryghed"** (security/safe-heldness) — perfect for "safe in His arms," belonging, the Father's house.
- **Worship temperature: restrained.** Default Danish expression is **understated, warm-but-modest, irony-aware, anti-hyperbolic.** A true, simply-stated, well-crafted line beats American-CCM superlatives — overstatement reads as fake or embarrassing (*pinligt*). The frikirke runs warmer, but dignity and honesty are prized everywhere. The Grundtvig/Folkekirke hymn tradition is **earthy, life-affirming, light-filled** — lean into warmth and light rather than volume.
- **Charged vocabulary to handle with care:** avoid nationalistic *fædreland/blod-og-jord* framing; keep "blood" strictly the **blood of Christ** (*Jesu blod*), reverent; militarist imagery soft; Janteloven means avoid superiority/boasting; secular ears may read *"frelst"* as "self-righteous" if used carelessly — keep it clearly the Savior word; don't let *held/lykke* (luck/happiness) crowd out *nåde*.
- **Reverence/honorific forms required when addressing God:** Danish has no honorific grammar, so reverence is carried by **register and capitalization** — use **"du/Du"** (intimate but capitalized for God), *Din/Dig/Han/Hans/Ham* capitalized. Title address: **"Herre" / "Herren,"** vocative **"o Herre," "Herre Jesus," "Fader."** Keep vocabulary elevated and clean; never slangy.
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** Denmark's hymn treasure is among the world's richest — **N.F.S. Grundtvig** and **B.S. Ingemann** (*"Dejlig er jorden," "Den signede dag," "Et barn er født i Betlehem," "I østen stiger solen op"*). These use **clean end-rhyme, regular singable meter, light/dawn/morning and life-affirming nature imagery, and warm communal "vi" (we) voice.** Reach for honest end-rhyme, steady meter, morning/light imagery, and the communal warmth of the Danish hymn. Note the Danish **stød** below (§4) — the single biggest singability difference from Swedish.

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY (Danish-specific)

- **THE STØD — the single biggest Danish singability pitfall.** Danish has a feature Swedish does NOT: the **stød**, a glottal catch/creaky-voice constriction on certain syllables (*hund, mand, ven, ånd, Gud* carry stød; near-minimal pairs like *hun'* vs *hund*, *mor* vs *mord* turn on it). On a sustained or belted note the stød cannot really be produced and often **disappears or distorts**, which can blur a word into its non-stød neighbour and mislead Suno/TTS. **Practical rule:** put words that depend on stød for their meaning on **short/unaccented notes**, and choose **smooth, open, vowel-final or non-stød words for sustained and belted notes.** Check that losing the stød on a held note doesn't change the word's sense.
- **REDUCED PRONUNCIATION — Danish "swallows" sounds.** Spoken Danish heavily reduces/elides consonants and unstressed vowels (the "soft d" /ð/ in *gud, med, ved, bed*; vocalised g/r; many silent or near-silent endings — *huset* sounds like "hu-seð," *meget* like "ma-eð"). This means **written syllables ≠ sung syllables**: a word that looks like 3 syllables may sing as 2. **Count SUNG (spoken-reduced) syllables, not letters,** and pick words whose reduced form lands cleanly on the beat. Do not pad based on the written form. This is a major difference from clearer-articulated Swedish.
- **Match syllable count; compress with the elisions Danish singers actually use.** Clitics/contractions: ***'n* (en), *'t* (det), *ka'* (kan), *ha'* (har/have), *bli'r* (bliver), *si'r* (siger), *ta'r* (tager), *å* (og, colloquial), *li'* (lige).*** These reflect how Danes actually sing. Prefer a short native synonym over padding; never pad with filler particles (*jo, vel, nu*) to fill a bar.
- **Respect Danish stress.** Stress is usually on the **first/root syllable** (*NÅ-de, HER-lig-hed, KON-ge, FREL-se-ren, GLÆ-de*); some loans shift (*Halle-LU-ja*). Put the stressed syllable on the strong beat; read aloud in **reduced** rhythm.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes.** Land big held notes on open, non-stød vowels — *-a, -e, -o, -å* (*nåde, glæde, herlighed, Halleluja*). Danish has **long vs short vowels**; put a long vowel on a sustained note. The hook "Jubiluyah" ends open (-ah) — exploit it.
- **The æ/ø/å caveat for AI vocals:** the vowels **æ [ɛ], ø [ø], å [ɔ]** are distinct phonemes — if you write bare *ae/oe/aa* or *a/o*, Suno/TTS will mis-sing and may change meaning (*"far"* father vs *"får"* sheep/gets vs *"fær-"*; *"hul"* hole vs *"hyl"* vs *"hæl"* heel). **Always write æ, ø, å in full** in sung text and titles. (Note: Danish uses **å** like Swedish but **æ/ø** where Swedish uses ä/ö — keep them distinct; do not Swedish-ize.)
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot** (lands at 0:00–0:08). The chorus arrives by the same bar.
- **Rhyme stance for Danish: USE end-rhyme (the Grundtvig/Ingemann hymn ear expects it), but never force it.** Aim to preserve AABB/ABAB **with words that genuinely belong.** The moment rhyme demands an invented word, a filler particle (*jo, vel, forvist, sandelig*), a broken inflection, or distorted meaning — **abandon it**: near-rhyme, assonance, or recast the OTHER line. A true unrhymed line beats a false rhymed one. (Danish reduced pronunciation makes many vowel-final words rhyme by ear that don't look identical — exploit honest *ear*-rhymes.)
- **Reach for the native poetic device (§1A):** Grundtvigian singable meter, clean end-rhyme, morning/light imagery, warm communal "vi."
- **Diacritics/letters mandatory** (æ, ø, å). Suno/TTS pronounce from text — bare/ASCII forms mis-sing and change meaning. Always full letters in sung body and titles.
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud **in reduced/slurred Danish** (as an AI vocal would) and check that swallowed consonants and lost stød don't garble a word into a vulgar/comic/sacred-taboo neighbour, and that *far/får, mor/mord, hun/hund* type pairs survive. Re-voice if so.

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**Danish (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, frame-appropriate):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus er grunden, vi jubler nu og her! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Kongen vender hjem — han er os nær, så nær!

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; *Jesus* (consistent name); "Jesus is the reason we are dancing now" → *Jesus er grunden, vi jubler nu og her* — I rendered "dancing" as **juble** ("rejoice/shout for joy"), which fits the restrained Danish worship temperature and reads natively across Folkekirken and frikirke alike (§1A), and SAID so; word order is V2-natural; "the King is coming back" → *Kongen vender hjem* (warm, royal-natural, Denmark's beloved monarchy); the resolve "somehow, somehow" → *han er os nær, så nær* ("He is near us, so near") swaps the vague EN tag for the Danish *tryghed*/nearness payload (§1A) — a deliberate, frame-true substitution, and SAID so; *her / nær* is an honest **ear-rhyme** (Danish reduced pronunciation makes them sing as a clean pair); the singability note: *her, nær, kongen* avoid stranding stød on the held notes; zero offense across traditions, no boastfulness.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A):** suppose you need a rhyme for *"nåde"* (grace).
- ❌ **Forced:** *"…vort held gav os nåde, forvist, på rad!"* — *held* (luck) implies salvation is luck (bad theology, §0A.9), *forvist* is archaic filler, *på rad* ("in a row") is jammed in only to rhyme.
- ✅ **Meaning-first (rhyme kept):** *"…på korset gi'r Jesus os nåde — han bærer al min skade."* — real words, sound doctrine (grace from Christ at the cross), *nåde/skade* honest near-rhyme, guilt–innocence frame (He bears all my harm); *gi'r* uses the natural sung contraction (§4).
- ✅ **Also fine (rhyme dropped):** *"…på korset gi'r Jesus os nåde; min gæld er fuldt betalt."* — no rhyme, but vivid guilt/debt-innocence imagery (my debt fully paid) and crystal-clear. **A true unrhymed line beats a false rhymed one.**

## 10. ADDICTIVENESS / RE-LISTENABILITY PASS (after the album is produced in Danish)

After the Danish album is produced, run the full addictiveness pass. This tunes the **English `Style` block (§6)** and the Suno re-gen prompts ONLY — **never** doctrine or the locked translation.

**Three listen passes:**
1. **Feeling pass** — listen as a normal Danish believer (and as a secular Dane). Where does it move you? Where does attention drift? Does the *længsel*/guilt–innocence (grace-as-rest) payload land (§1A)?
2. **Craft pass** — hook strength, groove/pocket, vocal delivery, arrangement, mix clarity, ear-candy; check the **stød/reduction** doesn't garble the vocal.
3. **Replay-trigger pass** — what makes you hit repeat? Identify the most addictive 2–4 seconds; make sure it's not buried.

**Amplify (via the English Style block + re-gen only):**
- **Hook & delivery** — make "Jubilujah"/the chorus hook land harder and earlier; sharpen vocal attack while keeping Danish understatement.
- **Groove & pulse** — target a head-nod pocket (~100–128 BPM where the style allows; keep hymnic/folk tracks at their proper tempo).
- **Texture & ear-candy** — tasteful, culturally resonant instrumentation (warm Nordic folk/hymn textures, organ or piano for hymnic; modern Scandi-pop for CCM) and small surprises; lean on warm major-key, morning-light brightness Danes love.
- **Dynamic arc & payoff** — restrained verse → fuller chorus → bridge lift → final-chorus payoff (Danish restraint rewards a delayed, earned, warm swell).
- **Loop point & album flow** — make the end invite a replay; sequence the album to flow.
- **Emotional pull & relatability** — using §1A's guilt–innocence frame, the *længsel/vemod* ache words, and the *hygge/tryghed* warmth/belonging; the light-out-of-winter-darkness arc.

**Re-rate each track + the album BEFORE/AFTER** across **hook strength, groove, replay value, dynamic payoff, emotional pull, loop-ability** (e.g. 1–10). Deliver a summary of changes, why each boosts re-listening, and the measurable before/after effect.
