## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the translator's *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to the meaning & the gospel** — the theological content must survive intact (the cross, the resurrection, the name of Jesus, repentance, the King's return). Never soften or alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, and open vowels must fit the original melody. A line a native can't sing comfortably has failed, even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant Italian** — it must sound like a native believer wrote it, not like a translated English song. No calques, no English word order; uses the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture (bathroom floor, cage door, wedding feast). Swap an image only when it is untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook preserved** — match the rhyme scheme *only when it can be done with real, sensible words*; ALWAYS preserve the hook's rhythmic placement.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**. Break a rhyme before you break the meaning or the meter.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** Every single line must make complete, natural sense to a native Italian speaker *on its own, with the rhyme ignored.* **Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement.** It is always better to ship an unrhymed (or near-rhymed) line that means something true and clear than a rhymed line that is forced, vague, or padded. If you cannot rhyme with words that genuinely belong in the sentence, **DROP THE RHYME** — use blank verse, assonance, or a near-rhyme. Never invent a word, bend grammar, or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line (delete the final rhyme word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?).
>
> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to the culture freely — but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity to be more relatable. Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form (1 Cor 1:23). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

Before finalizing any line, check it against this list. These are the failure modes a faith-based Italian speaker instantly hears as wrong.

**1. No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler words.** Never end a line with a word that's there *only* to rhyme. Real Italian offenders that creep in: **"ognor" / "allor" / "or"** (archaic poetic *now/then* — sound like 1800s opera libretto, not natural worship); **"sì / già / ormai / quaggiù / lassù"** dropped in purely for rhyme; the lazy **"-zione" stack** (*nazione, passione, unzione, emozione, redenzione, adorazione* strung for the easy `-ione`); **"amore / cuore / Signore" auto-triplet** abused (the most clichéd Italian rhyme of all); **truncated forms like "amor, cuor, splendor, ardor"** used only to grab a rhyme (apocope is fine when natural in song, filler when forced). **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.

**2. Use only REAL Italian words and CORRECT forms.** Italian traps: **congiuntivo (subjunctive)** after *che, perché (so that), affinché, prima che, benché, voglio che, spero che* — *"affinché io **canti**,"* not *"...canto"*; *"spero che **sia**,"* not *"...è."* **Auxiliary choice** (essere vs avere) and **past-participle agreement with essere**: *"lei è **salvata**,"* *"siamo **tornati**."* Don't confuse **a/ha/hai/ai/anno/hanno**, **e/è**, **o/ho**, **se/sé**, **ne/né**, **da/dà**, **li/lì**, **la/là**. Apostrophe vs accent: *un'anima* (fem., apostrophe) vs *un angelo* (masc., no apostrophe). No invented forms.

**3. Correct prepositions & set phrases — no calques.** Anglicisms to kill: *"sono **eccitato**"* (reads sexually aroused) for "excited" → **sono emozionato / pieno di gioia / non vedo l'ora**; *"realizzare"* for "to realize/understand" is colloquial but in careful register use **rendersi conto / capire** (*realizzare* properly = to accomplish); *"supportare"* (anglicism) for "support" → **sostenere / appoggiare**; *"assumere"* ≠ "assume/suppose" → **supporre**; *"eventualmente"* = possibly, NOT "eventually" → **alla fine / un giorno**; *"libreria"* = bookshop, not library; *"confidente"* false friend. Watch the **articulated prepositions** (*nel, nello, nella, sul, dal, del, alla, ai, dei*) — pick the right contraction. Verify every preposition (*credere **in** Dio*, *confidare **in** lui*) is the one a native uses.

**4. No English-calque or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** Don't write *"linea diretta col cielo"* if it reads like a phone hotline; don't calque "I'm all in" as *"sono tutto dentro"* → **mi dono tutto / anima e corpo**; don't calque "you've got my back" as *"hai la mia schiena"* → **mi proteggi / vegli su di me**; avoid hyphen-stacked English compounds — de-hyphenate into a natural Italian clause.

**5. Loanwords/register that misread in Italian.** Beware false friends and register breaks: **"caldo"** = warm/hot (NOT "cold" — the English-look trap); **"morbido"** = soft (NOT "morbid"); **"fattoria"** = farm (NOT "factory"); avoid slang that breaks reverence (*figata, sballo, che figo, mitico, da paura, sti cazzi*) in a worship line. **"Dio"** must stay reverent — never the exclamatory *"Dio mio / oddio"* register that reads as a casual exclamation. Don't drop English fillers (*"oh yeah, come on"*) into the sung Italian body unless the EN hook itself is English.

**6. Line-end adjectives & participles agree in gender/number.** Italian inflects audibly via final vowel: a female narrator says *"sono **stanca**,"* *"sono stata **salvata**,"* *"mi sento **amata**."* A dangling adjective chosen for rhyme often breaks agreement — *"l'anima **stanco**"* → **stanca** (*anima* feminine); *"mani **aperto**"* → **aperte**. Plural agreement too (*i figli **amati**, le grazie **ricevute***). **FIX NARRATOR GENDER FIRST** before any rhyme work (see §1A).

**7. No grammatically stranded tails.** Don't strand a relative *che/cui* with no head, or a genitive *di* attached to nothing. Recast so every word has a grammatical home.

**8. Imagery internally consistent.** Don't append an image that contradicts the scene, and don't introduce an object with no antecedent. Match the imagery already in the verse.

**9. Theology over rhyme.** Never let a rhyme word distort doctrine. Don't let *"il destino / la sorte / la fortuna"* (fate/luck) creep in to rhyme — keep chance subordinate to God's sovereignty (*la Sua volontà, il Suo piano*). Don't soften *"il peccato"* (sin) to *"l'errore/lo sbaglio"* for euphony. Grace comes from Christ, not from circumstance.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** read the line and silently delete its final rhyming word. Does the rest still say something true and clear? Is the final word one you'd actually use in that sentence? If no — the line is forced. Rewrite it meaning-first, even if that means no rhyme.

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

Italy is shaped by **Catholic cultural saturation** (overwhelmingly Catholic by heritage, with the Vatican at its center, though active practice has declined), alongside a **growing Evangelical/Pentecostale minority** (the Pentecostali — including the Assemblee di Dio in Italia, the largest evangelical body — plus the historic **Valdesi/Waldensian** Reformed church, Battisti, Fratelli/Brethren, and charismatic renewal Catholics). Italian-speaking Switzerland (Ticino) is similar. Jubilee/Inspire songs are CCM/evangelical in origin, but the IT version must **hold for the whole Italian Body of Christ.**

**Cross-tradition safety rules (IT-specific):**
- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** that all traditions own: *Dio, il Signore, Gesù, Cristo, il Padre, lo Spirito Santo, la grazia, la gloria, la croce, la risurrezione, l'Agnello, il Re, Alleluia, Amen, Osanna.*
- **Anchor Scripture echoes to the Nuova Riveduta (NR — the standard Italian Protestant Bible) and/or the CEI (the standard Catholic Bible)** — name both because the two communities carry slightly different phrasings; where they agree, you have the safest common ground. Echo: John 19:30 → **"È compiuto"** (both NR and CEI use this); Rev 22:20 → **"Vieni, Signore Gesù"**; Ps 23 → *"Il Signore è il mio pastore"* (NR) / *"Il Signore è il mio pastore"* (CEI — agree). Prefer phrasings the two versions share.
- **Do NOT import tradition-specific practices** the source lacks: **no Marian devotion** (no *Ave Maria, la Madonna, la Vergine, Madre di Dio, Regina del cielo, l'intercessione di Maria*), **no saint veneration** (no *San..., Sant'..., l'intercessione dei santi* — culturally pervasive in Italy but doctrinally divisive), no rosary, no one-church liturgical formulas, no glossolalia naming. Keep it Christ-centered and Bible-plain — the common ground that offends no one.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Use **"Tu"** (not *Lei/Voi*) for God — the universal Italian worship norm (intimate yet reverent). Capitalize divine pronouns where it aids reverence (*Tu, Te, Tuo, Tua, Tuoi, Egli, Lui, Suo, Colui* for God — common in Italian Christian print). Never use crude/vulgar/street-slang, and avoid casual exclamatory *Dio mio/oddio*.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** Bride/Bridegroom (*la Sposa / lo Sposo*) is biblical and fine — keep it pure, never romantic-pop longing.
- **Gender/inclusive-language & family handling: defer to THIS community's norms.** Italian evangelical/Catholic worship uses standard generic masculine (*i figli di Dio, gli uomini, fratelli*) — do **not** import secular inclusive devices (the *schwa* "ə" / asterisk forms *tuttə, car*, *frat*); they read as political and offend the receptor community. Keep traditional, Scripture-plain Italian.
- **Keep the offense of the cross, remove the offense of bad form** (1 Cor 1:23). Don't dilute sin (*il peccato*), blood (*il sangue*), repentance (*il pentimento / il ravvedimento*), judgment (*il giudizio*), or Christ's exclusivity — say them in clean, dignified Italian.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP (the relatability layer)

- **Lead with the worldview frame.** Italian Christian culture is **predominantly guilt–innocence** (the classic Western legal/moral frame — sin, confession, pardon, grace, justification, the cross), **with a strong family/honor and belonging overlay** (*la famiglia* is sacred in Italian culture; *casa, tavola, il padre, tornare a casa, la festa*). **Foreground BOTH:** the legal/relational gospel images (*il debito cancellato, perdonato, libero, giustificato, nessuna condanna* — Rom 8:1 *"nessuna condanna"*) AND the family/belonging images (*il Padre che ti corre incontro, la casa, la tavola, torni a casa, sei figlio/figlia, appartieni, la festa del ritorno*). The prodigal son (*il figlio prodigo*) and the running Father resonate deeply in this family-centered culture.
- **Worship temperature: WARM (Catholic devotional warmth) to EXUBERANT (Pentecostale).** Italian culture is expressive and affective; tender devotion and joyful celebration both read as authentic. Lean into heartfelt warmth and joyful praise; the Pentecostale wing is fully exuberant. *Festa, gioia, danzare davanti al Signore* (Ps 150, King David) is biblical and culturally welcome.
- **Taboo/danger images → safe substitutes:**
  - **"Buddy/homeboy Jesus"** (chummy slang) → keep Gesù *vicino, tenero* but never *"il mio amico Gesù"* in a flippant register, never *"Gesù, bro."* Intimacy yes, irreverence no.
  - **Marian/saint overtones** — Italy's deepest cultural-religious reflex is Marian and saintly; be vigilant. If EN says "Mother" in a maternal-God simile, render so it cannot read as the Madonna (*come una madre* simile is fine; never *la Madre* as a title). Never invoke a saint.
  - **Wine "flowing like a river"** — fine and biblical (Cana, Italy's own wine culture makes it vivid); don't let it read as drunkenness.
  - **"Fire"** — Spirit/refining fire is fine; contextualize when EN means judgment.
  - **"Conquering/army/warfare"** — keep spiritual (*più che vincitori*, Rom 8:37); avoid literal-militancy tone.
- **Native "ache" word / sacred-longing idiom:** **"struggimento"** (intense yearning/pining) and the verb **"anelare (a)"** / **"bramare"** / **"sospirare per"** (to long/sigh for — echoing Ps 42 *"Come la cerva anela ai corsi d'acqua, così l'anima mia anela a te, o Dio"* NR). Also the deep cultural **"nostalgia"** (the word is Italian-coined in feel — longing for a lost home/heaven). Use *anelare / struggimento / sospirare per* for sacred longing.
- **Charged vocabulary to handle with care:** *patria/nazione/sangue e suolo* (20th-century weight — keep "homeland" heavenly, *la patria celeste*, never earthly nationalism); *Re/regno* are natural and biblical in worship (no strong republic-allergy in religious register — *Cristo Re, il Regno* are familiar, e.g. the feast of *Cristo Re*) — fine; *catene/schiavitù/libertà* carry historical weight — use for the gospel's freedom with dignity.
- **Reverence/honorific forms required when addressing God:** **"Tu"** for God (not the formal *Lei*); capitalize *Tu, Te, Tuo, Tua, Tuoi, Egli, Lui, Suo, Colui, Chi* when they refer to God. Vocatives: *"Signore," "o Dio," "Padre," "Gesù," "Cristo"* — natural and reverent.
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** Italian's gift is **open-vowel endings** (nearly every word ends in a vowel) — this gives natural musicality and easy rhyme; use **endecasillabo/settenario**-style flowing lines and lean on **assonance** when consonant rhyme would force. **Anaphora** and **parallelismo** (very natural and Psalm-like) sing beautifully. Italian melody loves a vowel-rich, legato line.

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY (Italian-specific)

- **Open-vowel endings are a gift — exploit them.** Italian words almost all end in a vowel (a/e/i/o/u), giving natural legato and easy held notes. End big sustained/belted notes on these open vowels (*gloria, alleluia, Signore, amore, Salvatore, libertà*) — Italian is ideal for sung worship. The hook "Jubiluyah" ends open (-ah) — perfectly at home.
- **Match syllable count via Italian's natural tools.** Use **elisione** (*l'anima, dell'amore, un'ora, c'è, dov'è*), **apocope/troncamento** (dropping a final unstressed vowel where natural: *amor, cuor, Signor, andar, ben, gran, son* — natural in song BUT don't overuse for filler, §0A.1), and **sinalefe** (across word boundaries, two adjacent vowels sing as one syllable: *"ma_io," "che_amo," "lo_adoro," "Tu_eri"* — Italian's built-in syllable merge, the basis of its metrics). Sinalefe is your main syllable-fitting tool — exploit it; do not pad with filler.
- **Respect Italian stress.** Most words are **piane** (penultimate stress); place these on strong beats. **Tronche** (final-stress: *Gesù, città, libertà, virtù, perché*) carry a strong final beat well; **sdrucciole** (antepenultimate: *anima, lacrima, spirito, gloria→glò-ria*) need care so the stress lands right. Don't force an unnatural accent. Read every line aloud in rhythm.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot.** The chorus must arrive on the same bar.
- **Rhyme stance for Italian: DON'T FORCE; Italian rhymes *too* easily.** The vowel-final words make rhyme abundant — which is the trap (the lazy `-ione/-ore/-are/-ato` auto-rhymes and the *amore/cuore/Signore* cliché, §0A.1). Prefer **assonance or a true near-rhyme** over a forced consonant rhyme; recast the OTHER line of the couplet rather than cram a bad word. Internal rhyme and alliteration are bonuses, never obligations.
- **Reach for the native poetic device (§1A)** — open-vowel legato, anaphora, parallelismo, assonance — where it fits.
- **Diacritics are MANDATORY** where Italian uses them (à è é ì ò ù) — they mark stress/word identity. Suno/TTS pronounce from text: *"perche"* mis-sings; **"perché"** sings right. *"e"* (and) vs *"è"* (is), *"da"* (from) vs *"dà"* (gives), *"si"* (oneself) vs *"sì"* (yes), *"ne"* vs *"né"* — wrong/missing accent = wrong word/stress. Use the apostrophe correctly for elision (*l'amore, un'anima*). Always write full diacritics in sung body and titles.
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud slurred (as an AI vocal would) and check blurred syllables/sinalefe don't form a vulgar/comic word. Italian traps: watch joins that slur into **"cazzo," "culo," "puttana," "merda," "minchia,"** or **"figa"**; watch a sinalefe fusing two words into something crude across the boundary; watch *"-occo/-azzo/-ulo"* slurs. If a slur produces a bad word, recast.

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**IT (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, frame-appropriate):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / È Gesù la ragione, danziamo insieme ancora! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Il Re tornerà — sì, è vicina l'ora!

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; *Gesù* (consistent name); "Jesus is the reason we are dancing now" → *È Gesù la ragione, danziamo insieme ancora* — **dancing is KEPT** (no taboo; *danzare* is celebrated and biblical), and *insieme* ("together") quietly engages the **family/belonging frame** (§1A); "the King is coming back" → *Il Re tornerà — è vicina l'ora* (clear, true, royal + the imminence of His return); rhyme *ancora / l'ora* is a real, sensible pair riding Italy's open-vowel ease and a touch of assonance; capitalized *Re* for reverence; meaning intact; zero offense across traditions.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A and §1A):** Suppose you need to render the second line.
- ❌ Forced/archaic-rhyme: *"È Gesù la ragion, danziam ognor con ardor!"* — *ragion / danziam* truncated only to grab a rhyme, and *ognor* ("evermore") + *ardor* are operatic-archaic filler (§0A.1) that no Italian sings naturally today; reads as 1800s libretto, not living worship.
- ❌ Taboo/syncretism: *"La Madonna ci guida, danziam con il cuor!"* — imports **Marian devotion** (*la Madonna*), which the source lacks and which offends evangelical readers and distorts the line (extra-dangerous reflex in Italy, §1A).
- ✅ Meaning-first, culturally resonant: *"È Gesù la ragione, danziamo insieme ancora!"* — real, living words, sound doctrine, warm/joyful, *insieme* lands the belonging the culture loves, honest open-vowel rhyme. **A true, living, doctrinally clean line beats a forced-archaic or syncretistic one.**
