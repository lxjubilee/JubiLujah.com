## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the translator's *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to the meaning & the gospel** — the theological content must survive intact (the cross, the resurrection, the name of Jesus, repentance, the King's return). Never soften or alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, and open vowels must fit the original melody. A line a native can't sing comfortably has failed, even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant French** — it must sound like a native believer wrote it, not like a translated English song. No calques, no English word order; uses the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture (bathroom floor, cage door, wedding feast). Swap an image only when it is untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook preserved** — match the rhyme scheme *only when it can be done with real, sensible words*; ALWAYS preserve the hook's rhythmic placement.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**. Break a rhyme before you break the meaning or the meter.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** Every single line must make complete, natural sense to a native French speaker *on its own, with the rhyme ignored.* **Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement.** It is always better to ship an unrhymed (or near-rhymed) line that means something true and clear than a rhymed line that is forced, vague, or padded. If you cannot rhyme with words that genuinely belong in the sentence, **DROP THE RHYME** — use blank verse, assonance, or a near-rhyme. Never invent a word, bend grammar, or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line (delete the final rhyme word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?).
>
> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to the culture freely — but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity to be more relatable. Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form (1 Cor 1:23). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

Before finalizing any line, check it against this list. These are the failure modes a faith-based French speaker instantly hears as wrong.

**1. No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler words.** Never end a line with a word that's there *only* to rhyme. Real French offenders that creep in: **"enfin" / "toujours" / "ainsi" / "vraiment"** tacked on to pad; the lazy **"-tion" rhyme stack** (*nation, passion, onction, émotion, adoration, rédemption* strung together for the easy `-sion/-tion`); **"ô" added everywhere** as a filler syllable (*"ô mon Dieu, ô ô"*); **"là" / "ici-bas" / "à jamais"** dropped in purely for rhyme; **"amour / toujours"** abused as the auto-rhyme of all auto-rhymes (the most clichéd pairing in French song — avoid leaning on it). **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.

**2. Use only REAL French words and CORRECT forms.** French traps: **subjunctive** after *que, pour que, avant que, bien que, il faut que* — *"pour que je **chante**,"* not *"...je chante"* (here indicative and subj. coincide, but *"pour qu'il **fasse**,"* not *"...fait"*); agreement of the **past participle** (with *être*: agrees with subject — *"elle est **sauvée**"*; with *avoir*: agrees with a preceding direct object — *"la grâce qu'il m'a **donnée**"*); don't confuse **a** (has) / **à** (to), **ou** (or) / **où** (where), **ce** / **se**, **ces** / **ses** / **c'est** / **s'est** / **sais** / **sait**, **la** / **là**, **on** / **ont**, **sont** / **son**. No invented forms.

**3. Correct prepositions & set phrases — no calques.** Anglicisms to kill: *"je suis **excité**"* (reads sexually aroused) for "excited" → **je suis enthousiaste / rempli de joie / impatient**; *"réaliser"* for "to realize/understand" is now common but in careful register use **se rendre compte / comprendre**; *"supporter"* = to endure/tolerate, NOT "to support" → **soutenir / appuyer**; *"assumer"* ≠ "assume/suppose" → **supposer**; *"éventuellement"* = possibly, NOT "eventually" → **finalement / un jour**; *"librairie"* = bookshop, not library; *"demander"* = to ask, never "to demand." Verify every preposition (*croire **en** Dieu*, *compter **sur** lui*) is the one a native uses.

**4. No English-calque or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** Don't write *"ligne directe vers le ciel"* if it reads like a phone hotline; don't calque "I'm all in" as *"je suis tout dedans"* → **je me donne tout entier / corps et âme**; don't calque "you've got my back" as *"tu as mon dos"* → **tu veilles sur moi / tu me protèges**; avoid hyphen-stacked English compounds — de-hyphenate into a natural French clause.

**5. Loanwords/register that misread in French — and the STIFF/LITURGICAL vs WARM register trap.** French has a real danger that the obvious sacred word sounds **cold, formal, churchy** rather than warm and intimate — flag and choose deliberately:
  - **"adorer"** is good for worship BUT in everyday French *"j'adore le chocolat"* = "I love/I'm crazy about" — context must keep it sacred.
  - **"louange"** (praise) and **"glorifier"** can sound liturgical/formal; warmer alternatives in places: *te chanter, t'élever, dire ta grandeur.*
  - **"Seigneur"** is reverent but can feel archaic/distant in a tender verse — for intimacy you may use *Jésus, Père, mon Dieu*; reserve *Seigneur* for the majestic register.
  - **"saint"** is fine; **"béni"** (blessed) is warm.
  - Avoid slang that breaks reverence (*"trop bien, ouf, kiffer, c'est ouf, grave"*) in a worship line.
  - Don't drop English fillers (*"oh yeah, come on"*) into the sung French body unless the EN hook itself is English.

**6. Line-end adjectives & participles agree in gender/number.** French inflects (often silent in speech but VISIBLE and sometimes audible): a female narrator says *"je suis **fatiguée**,"* *"j'ai été **sauvée**,"* *"je me sens **aimée**."* Watch audible agreements where the feminine adds a pronounced consonant (*heureux → heureuse*, *béni → bénie* same sound but *grand → grande* differs). A dangling adjective chosen for rhyme often breaks agreement. **FIX NARRATOR GENDER FIRST** before any rhyme work (see §1A).

**7. No grammatically stranded tails.** Don't strand a relative clause or a *dont/que* with no head. Watch the *"ce dont/ce que"* trap — it needs a real referent. Recast so every word has a grammatical home.

**8. Imagery internally consistent.** Don't append an image that contradicts the scene, and don't introduce an object with no antecedent. Match the imagery already in the verse.

**9. Theology over rhyme.** Never let a rhyme word distort doctrine. Don't let *"le destin / le sort / la chance"* (fate/luck) creep in to rhyme — keep chance subordinate to God's sovereignty (*Sa volonté, Son plan, Sa providence*). Don't soften *"le péché"* (sin) to *"l'erreur"* for euphony. Grace comes from Christ, not from circumstance.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** read the line and silently delete its final rhyming word. Does the rest still say something true and clear? Is the final word one you'd actually use in that sentence? If no — the line is forced. Rewrite it meaning-first, even if that means no rhyme.

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

The French-speaking world is shaped by a **strongly secular (laïque) society in France** (Catholic by deep cultural heritage, but with low active practice and an assertive public secularism), a **committed Catholic minority**, and a **small but vibrant évangélique/protestant minority** (Reformed, Pentecostal/charismatic, Baptist, Brethren). Beyond France, **francophone Africa (DR Congo, Côte d'Ivoire, Cameroon, etc.) is heavily and actively Christian** — a huge, fervent worship audience — and Québec/Belgium/Switzerland add their own profiles. Jubilee/Inspire songs are CCM/evangelical in origin, but the FR version must **hold for the whole French-speaking Body of Christ.**

**Cross-tradition safety rules (FR-specific):**
- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** that all traditions own: *Dieu, le Seigneur, Jésus, Christ, le Père, le Saint-Esprit, la grâce, la gloire, la croix, la résurrection, l'Agneau, le Roi, Alléluia, Amen, Hosanna.*
- **Anchor Scripture echoes to the Louis Segond (LSG 1910 / NEG 1979)** — the de-facto standard, most broadly recognized French Protestant Bible (Catholics will recognize most of it; LSG is the safest common denominator for sung worship). Echo its phrasings: John 19:30 → **"Tout est accompli"**; Rev 22:20 → **"Viens, Seigneur Jésus"**; Ps 23 → *"L'Éternel est mon berger"* (note: LSG uses *l'Éternel* for the OT divine name — for sung NT/worship lyrics prefer *le Seigneur / Dieu*, but the cadence is what believers carry).
- **Do NOT import tradition-specific practices** the source lacks: **no Marian devotion** (no *Je vous salue Marie, la Vierge, Notre-Dame, Mère de Dieu, Reine du ciel, l'intercession de Marie*), no saint/relic veneration, no rosary, no one-church liturgical formulas, no glossolalia naming. Keep it Christ-centered and Bible-plain — the common ground that offends no one.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Use **"Tu"** (not *vous*) for God — the universal francophone Protestant/charismatic worship norm (intimate yet reverent); some traditional Catholic prayer uses *vous*, but *tu* is standard and safest for sung worship. Capitalize divine pronouns where it aids reverence (*Tu, Toi, Ton, Ta, Tes, Il, Lui, Son* for God — common in French Christian print). Never use crude/vulgar/street-slang.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** Bride/Bridegroom (*l'Épouse / l'Époux*) is biblical and fine — keep it pure, never romantic-pop longing.
- **Gender/inclusive-language & family handling: defer to THIS community's norms.** French evangelical/Catholic worship uses standard generic masculine (*les fils de Dieu, les hommes, frères*) — do **not** import secular *écriture inclusive* (median-dot forms like *frère·s·sœurs*, *toustes*, *iel*); they read as political and offend the receptor community. Keep traditional, Scripture-plain French.
- **Keep the offense of the cross, remove the offense of bad form** (1 Cor 1:23). Don't dilute sin (*le péché*), blood (*le sang*), repentance (*la repentance*), judgment (*le jugement*), or Christ's exclusivity — say them in clean, dignified French.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP (the relatability layer)

- **Lead with the worldview frame.** French/European Christian culture is **predominantly guilt–innocence** (legal/moral debt, pardon, grace, justification — the classic Western frame). **Foreground the legal/relational gospel images:** *la dette effacée, le pardon, libre, justifié, plus de condamnation* (Rom 8:1 LSG: *"il n'y a donc maintenant aucune condamnation"*), reconciliation with the Father, *acquitté* (acquitted). In secular France especially, the gospel often enters through **authenticity, inner emptiness/meaning, and freedom** — lean on *vérité, liberté, sens, paix intérieure, lumière dans les ténèbres*. In **francophone Africa**, add a warmer honor/belonging and deliverance register (*délivrance, puissance, victoire, famille de Dieu*) which lands powerfully there.
- **Worship temperature: RESTRAINED-TO-WARM (France/Québec); WARMER-TO-EXUBERANT (francophone Africa).** Default French aesthetic prizes **understatement, elegance, sincerity over hype** — avoid overblown hype-language; intimate, poetic, dignified joy reads as more authentic. Don't over-exclaim. (For an explicitly Africa-targeted release the temperature can rise considerably.)
- **Taboo/danger images → safe substitutes:**
  - **"Buddy/homeboy Jesus"** (chummy slang) → keep Jesus *proche, tendre* but never *"mon pote Jésus / mon gars."* Intimacy yes, irreverence no.
  - **Marian overtones** — if EN says "Mother" in a maternal-God simile, render so it cannot read as Marian (*comme une mère* simile is fine; never *la Mère* as a title, never *Notre-Dame*).
  - **Over-the-top emotionalism** can read as insincere to a French ear → prefer restraint and a true image over a shouted superlative.
  - **Wine "flowing like a river"** — fine and biblical (Cana, France's own wine culture makes it resonant); don't let it read as drunkenness.
  - **"Fire"** — Spirit/refining fire is fine; contextualize when EN means judgment.
  - **"Conquering/army/warfare"** — keep spiritual (*plus que vainqueurs*, Rom 8:37 LSG); avoid literal-militancy tone given European pacifist sensibilities.
- **Native "ache" word / sacred-longing idiom:** **"la soif (de Dieu)"** (thirst for God — *"mon âme a soif de Dieu,"* echoing Ps 42 LSG *"Comme une biche soupire après des courants d'eau, ainsi mon âme soupire après toi, ô Dieu"* — the verb **soupirer après** "to sigh/long for" is the deep sacred-longing idiom). Also **"l'attente"** (longing expectancy) and the literary **"le manque"** (the felt absence/lack). Use *soif / soupirer après / aspirer à* for sacred longing.
- **Charged vocabulary to handle with care:** *patrie/nation/sang* (heavy 20th-century weight in Europe — keep "homeland" heavenly, *la patrie céleste*, never earthly nationalism); *Roi/royaume* can feel slightly archaic in strongly republican France but are still natural and biblical in worship (*Christ Roi, le Royaume*) — fine, just don't lean political; *chaînes/esclavage/liberté* carry real historical weight (slavery, colonial history in francophone Africa especially) — use for the gospel's freedom with dignity; *laïcité* means religious language in public is culturally sensitive — keep the lyric clearly worship-context.
- **Reverence/honorific forms required when addressing God:** **"Tu"** for God (not *vous*); capitalize *Tu, Toi, Ton, Ta, Tes, Il, Lui, Son, Qui* when they refer to God. Vocatives: *"Seigneur," "ô Dieu," "Père," "Jésus," "Christ"* — natural and reverent (use *"ô"* sparingly so it doesn't become filler, §0A.1).
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** French verse is **syllable-counted, not stress-timed** — the line's music is the syllable count and the placement of the **rime** plus the elegant flow of **liaison/enchaînement**. Reach for **alternance of rich/sufficient rhyme** where natural, **assonance** when consonant rhyme would force, and the very French **understatement/litote** (saying more by saying less) and **clear imagery** over piled-up superlatives. **Anaphora** (repeating an opening word/phrase) is elegant and singable.

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY (French-specific)

- **Match syllable count to the melody — and master the "e muet" (mute e).** This is THE French singing variable. A final/internal *-e* can be **silent** in speech but **pronounced as a sung syllable** in lyric French (*"gloi-re"* can be 1 or 2 sung syllables; *"Père"* 1 or 2; *"libre"* 1 or 2). You control syllable count by deciding whether each *e muet* is sung — exploit this to fit bars *without padding*. Mark intent clearly so Suno/the vocalist knows.
- **Liaison & élision.** Use the obligatory **élisions** French singers rely on: *j'ai, l'amour, d'amour, qu'il, n'est, s'élève, jusqu'à, m'a sauvé, t'adore, c'est.* Use **liaison** (linking a normally-silent final consonant to a following vowel — *"nous_avons," "grand_amour," "ses_enfants"*) to smooth flow and fit notes. These are your two main syllable tools alongside the *e muet*.
- **Respect French rhythm.** French is **syllable-timed with the accent on the LAST sounded syllable of a rhythmic group** (not lexical stress like English). Don't force an English-style stress pattern; let the phrase-final syllable carry the weight and align it to strong beats / line-ends. Read every line aloud in rhythm.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes.** End big held notes on open vowels — **a, o, é, è, ou** (*gloire, alléluia, amour, Sauveur, voici, à toi*); a pronounced *e muet* on a sustain is soft and beautiful but light. The hook "Jubiluyah" ends open (-ah) — exploit it.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot.** The chorus must arrive on the same bar.
- **Rhyme stance for French: DON'T FORCE; classical rhyme is nice but optional.** French has a rich rhyme tradition (rime suffisante/riche) but the lazy **"-tion" and "amour/toujours"** auto-rhymes are clichés to avoid (§0A.1). Prefer **assonance or a true near-rhyme** over a forced consonant rhyme. It is usually better to **recast the OTHER line of the couplet** than to cram a bad word. Internal rhyme and alliteration are bonuses.
- **Reach for the native poetic device (§1A)** — anaphora, understatement, assonance, clear imagery — where it fits.
- **Diacritics are MANDATORY** (é è ê à â î ï ô ù û ç ë). Suno/TTS pronounce from text: *"resurrection"* mis-sings; **"résurrection"** sings right. *"a"* (has) vs *"à"* (to), *"ou"* vs *"où"*, *"sur"* vs *"sûr"* — wrong/missing accent = wrong word. Always write full diacritics in sung body and titles. Keep **ç** (*grâce → no ç, but "ça," "reçu"*).
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud slurred (as an AI vocal would) and check blurred syllables/liaisons don't form a vulgar/comic word. French traps: watch joins that slur into **"con," "cul," "merde," "pute," "bite,"** or **"salaud"**; watch a liaison creating an unintended word across the boundary (e.g. a *t*/*n* liaison fusing into something crude); watch *"-ce que"* and *"-asse"* slurs. If a slur produces a bad word, recast.

## 5. IDIOMS, IMAGERY & CULTURAL FIT

- **Translate the sense, not the words, for idioms.** "permission slip" → a natural FR equivalent (*tu n'as pas besoin d'autorisation / pas besoin de permission*); "you turned it around / did the flip" → *tu as tout renversé / tu as tout changé*; "Sunday morning bell" → *la cloche du dimanche* (church bells universal and beloved in France).
- **Keep concrete sensory anchors** (bathroom floor at 3 AM, cage door, wedding clock, wine like a river) — render them vividly in French (*le carrelage froid à trois heures du matin, la porte de la cage*).
- **Localize culture-bound daily-life anchors** (carpool line, diner, prom, credit-card debt) → everyday French equivalents (*le trajet de l'école, la table de la cuisine, les dettes qui étouffent*). **Do NOT localize away the global/heaven imagery** — every-tribe, throne, Lamb, wedding feast stay.

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**FR (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, frame-appropriate):**
> Jubiluyah ! Jubiluyah ! / C'est Jésus qui nous fait danser, libres enfin ! / Jubiluyah ! Jubiluyah ! / Le Roi va revenir — il tient sa main, sa main !

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; *Jésus* (consistent name); "Jesus is the reason we are dancing now" → *C'est Jésus qui nous fait danser, libres enfin* — keeps dancing, and adds *libres* (free) to engage the **guilt-innocence/freedom frame** the culture enters by, with restrained warmth rather than hype; "the King is coming back" → *Le Roi va revenir* (clear, true, royal imagery acceptable in worship); *enfin / sa main* is a true near-rhyme (assonance on the nasal *-in*), not forced; capitalized *Roi* for reverence; meaning intact; zero offense across traditions. (Note: *"enfin"* here is doing real work — "free at last" — not filler.)

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A and §1A):** Suppose you need to render "Jesus is the reason we are dancing now."
- ❌ Forced/cliché-rhyme: *"Jésus est la raison, dansons dans l'émotion, dans l'adoration !"* — the `-tion` stack (*émotion/adoration*) is lazy auto-rhyme filler (§0A.1), and the piled superlatives read as insincere hype to a French ear (wrong temperature, §1A).
- ❌ Calque/false-friend: *"Jésus est la raison qu'on est si **excité** !"* — *excité* reads as sexually aroused (§0A.3/5) and *"la raison qu'on..."* is an English calque (should be *la raison pour laquelle*).
- ✅ Meaning-first, culturally resonant: *"C'est Jésus qui nous fait danser, libres enfin !"* — real words, sound doctrine, restrained-but-warm, engages the freedom frame, honest near-rhyme. **A true, dignified line beats a forced or franglais one.**
