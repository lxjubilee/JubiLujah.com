## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the translator's *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to meaning & the gospel** — theology must survive intact (the cross, the resurrection, the name of Jesus, repentance, the King's return); never alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, and open vowels fit the original melody. A line a native can't sing comfortably has failed, even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant Dutch** — sounds like a native Dutch believer wrote it; no calques, no English word order (Dutch is **verb-second** in main clauses, **verb-final** in subclauses); uses the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture; swap an image only if untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook** — match the rhyme scheme ONLY with real, sensible words; always keep the hook's rhythmic placement.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**. Break a rhyme before you break the meaning or the meter.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** every line must make complete, natural sense to a native Dutch speaker *with the rhyme ignored.* **Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement.** Ship a clear unrhymed/near-rhymed true line over a forced one. Never invent a word, bend grammar, or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. If no honest rhyme fits, **DROP IT.** Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line (delete the final rhyme word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?).
>
> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to the culture freely — but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity to be more relatable. Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form (1 Cor 1:23). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

These are the exact failure modes that make a Dutch believer instantly hear "translated English." Check every line.

**1. No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler words.** Never end a line with a word that exists *only* to rhyme. Real Dutch offenders to watch: stranded particles **"toch," "wel," "maar," "nou," "immers," "voorwaar"** jammed at line-end to fill a beat; archaic tags **"weleer," "alom," "geenszins," "voorwaar"** dropped in only for rhyme; throwaway **"o Heer"** as a rhyme-plug; a forced diminutive (**"hartje," "zieltje"**) used to soften a stressed line into rhyme. **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.

**2. Only REAL words & correct forms.** Keep Dutch verb forms correct: the **'t kofschip / 't fokschaap** rule for past tense and past participle (*-te/-de*, *-t/-d*): *werken → werkte → gewerkt*; *geloven → geloofde → geloofd* — never *"geloofte," "gewerkd."* Past participle prefix **ge-** is mandatory (*gered, gezongen, gezien* — never bare *"red," "zong"* as a participle). Strong verbs keep vowel change (*zingen → zong → gezongen*; never *"zingde"*). Separable verbs split correctly (*Hij staat op* ↔ *…dat Hij opstaat*).

**3. Correct prepositions & set phrases — no calques.** Dutch prepositions don't map 1:1 to English. Caught-style traps: *"wachten **voor** jou"* (English "wait for") → **"op jou wachten"**; *"denken **over** jou"* in the sense of remembering → **"aan jou denken"**; *"afhankelijk **op**"* → **"afhankelijk **van**"**; *"vol **van** genade"* is acceptable but **"vol genade"** is cleaner; the English-calque *"Het maakt geen zin"* → **"Het heeft geen zin"** / **"Het slaat nergens op."** *"Ik mis jou"* not *"Ik mis voor jou."*

**4. No English-calque word order or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** Dutch is verb-second; in subclauses the verb goes to the **end** — never copy English SVO into a subclause (*"…omdat Jezus **houdt van mij**"* ❌ → *"…omdat Jezus **van mij houdt**"* ✅). Don't calque "reach out" as *"reik uit"* in the English emotional sense → **"ik roep naar U" / "ik strek mij uit"**; don't render "I'm on the line to heaven" as a phone line → **"ik roep tot de hemel."** De-hyphenate English-style stacked compounds.

**5. Loanwords/register that misread in Dutch.** False friends: English **"brave"** ≠ Dutch **braaf** (= obedient/well-behaved, even goody-two-shoes — wrong for "courageous" → use *moedig/dapper*); **"eventueel"** = *possibly*, NOT "eventually" (→ *uiteindelijk*); **"actueel"** = *current/topical*, not "actual" (→ *werkelijk/echt*); **"slim"** = *clever*, not "thin." Don't drop casual Anglicisms (*chillen, cool, vibe, gewoon lekker*) or slang into a reverent line. Keep register heightened, not spreektaal-casual.

**6. Adjective inflection must agree** (the **-e** rule). Attributive adjectives take **-e** except before a singular *het*-word with the indefinite article: *"een heilig**e** God"* (de-word) but *"een heilig kind"* (het-word, indefinite) and *"het heilige kind"* (definite). A rhyme-chosen adjective that breaks this is instantly wrong — fix the rhyme partner, not the grammar. (*de/het* gender errors are the #1 non-native tell.)

**7. No grammatically stranded tails.** Don't strand a dangling phrase or a relative clause with no head. Every word must have a grammatical home in the line or the next.

**8. Imagery internally consistent.** Don't append an image that fights the scene (cold floor + a sudden *vlam/gloed* only for rhyme) or introduce an object with no antecedent. Match the picture already established (the dawn = *de dageraad*, not a random *bloem*).

**9. Theology over rhyme.** No rhyme word may distort doctrine. **"Geluk"** = luck/happiness — keep luck/chance subordinate to God's sovereignty (prefer **"zegen," "genade," "heil"**); don't imply salvation is fortune. **"Lot/noodlot"** (fate) must never replace God's providence. Grace comes from Christ, never circumstance.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** silently delete the final rhyme word. Does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use? If no — rewrite meaning-first, even with no rhyme.

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

The Dutch-speaking world is the Netherlands and Flanders (Belgium). The Netherlands is heavily secular today but carries a deep **Reformed (gereformeerd / hervormd)** heritage, now largely united in the **Protestantse Kerk in Nederland (PKN)**, alongside a strict-Reformed "Bible Belt" (*bevindelijk gereformeerd*), a substantial **Roman Catholic** population (especially the south and in Flanders, which is historically Catholic), and a vibrant **evangelisch / charismatic / Pinkster** stream. Jubilee/Inspire songs are CCM/evangelical in origin, but the Dutch version must **hold for the whole Dutch-speaking Body of Christ.**

- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** all traditions own: *God, de Heer / de Heere, Jezus, Christus, de Vader, de Heilige Geest, genade, heerlijkheid, het Koninkrijk, het kruis, de opstanding, het Lam, Halleluja, Amen, verlossing, heil.* Anchor Scripture echoes to a standard Bible: the **Statenvertaling** (the historic, KJV-equivalent translation revered in Reformed circles — note it uses **"Heere"**), and the modern **NBV (Nieuwe Bijbelvertaling 2021)** or **HSV (Herziene Statenvertaling)** for contemporary register. Pick a register per album. Iconic phrasings: John 19:30 → **"Het is volbracht"**; Rev 22:20 → **"Kom, Heere Jezus"** (Statenvertaling) / **"Kom, Heer Jezus"** (modern).
- **No tradition-specific imports the source lacks.** No Marian devotion (*Wees gegroet Maria*), no saint/relic veneration, no Mass-specific formulas, no charismatic glossolalia naming. Christ-centered and Bible-plain is the common ground.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Address God respectfully; capitalize divine pronouns where it aids reverence (*U, Uw, Hem, Hij, Zijn* when referring to God — standard in Dutch Christian print). Use **"U"** for God (the reverent address; in strict-Reformed usage even prayer keeps the older *Gij* — modern churches use *U*). Avoid casual *"jij/je"* to God outside very specific contemporary-worship contexts; default to **U**. No slang anywhere.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** Bride/Bridegroom (*de Bruid / de Bruidegom*) is biblical and fine; keep it pure.
- **Gender/inclusive-language & family handling:** **defer to THIS community's norms** — Dutch churches range from strict-traditional to progressive. Choose the broadest, most natural Scripture-plain form; do not import the source culture's gender debates.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP (the relatability layer)

- **Lead with the culture's worldview frame: guilt–innocence.** The Dutch Reformed inheritance runs deep — *schuld* (guilt/debt), *genade* (grace), *rechtvaardiging* (justification), *vergeving* (forgiveness), the *Heidelbergse Catechismus* sense of "my only comfort." **Foreground these images:** sin as debt → **debt paid / schuld betaald**; the conscience accused → **acquitted (vrijgesproken)**; declared righteous (*rechtvaardig verklaard*); a clean conscience (*een rein geweten*); peace with God. The cross as the place guilt is settled lands hardest.
- **Taboo/danger images → safe native substitutes:**
  - **"Buddy/homeboy Jesus"** / over-casual *"hé Jezus, maatje"* → keep warmth but reverent: *"Jezus, mijn Vriend"* / *"mijn Heiland"* — never matey slang. Dutch directness can tip into irreverence; resist.
  - **"Dancing before the Lord"** — *dansen* is fine in evangelisch/charismatic settings but reads less natively in Reformed worship, which is **sober and word-centered**. *Juichen, jubelen, zingen* (shout/rejoice/sing) often carry the same energy more universally; *dansen* is acceptable where the source insists.
  - **Triumphalist warfare / "conquering"** — softer than in German, but prefer victory **over sin and death** (*overwinning op zonde en dood*) to nationalistic conquest. The Dutch are wary of grand triumphalism (cultural understatement — *"doe maar gewoon, dan doe je al gek genoeg"*).
  - **King/Kingdom** is fully natural (*Koning / Koninkrijk*); the Netherlands has a beloved monarchy, so royal imagery reads warm, not awkward.
  - **Wine flowing like a river** — fine; Dutch culture is not anti-alcohol broadly, though the strict-Reformed Bible Belt is more abstemious — keep it clearly the biblical/celebratory image (the wedding feast), not bar-imagery.
- **Native ache word / sacred-longing idiom: "verlangen"** (deep longing) and the heightened **"hunkeren"** / **"smachten naar"** (to yearn/pine for) — reach for these where the EN aches for God or heaven. **"heimwee"** (homesickness) works for longing for the eternal home/heaven. Dutch hymnody loves *"verlangen naar U."*
- **Worship temperature: restrained (Reformed) to warm (evangelisch).** Default Reformed worship is **sober, sincere, text-first, understated** — a true quiet line beats hype; Dutch culture distrusts overstatement and emotional showiness (*overdreven*). The evangelisch/Pinkster stream runs warmer and more expressive. Aim restrained-but-heartfelt; let warmth build. Avoid gushing American-CCM superlatives.
- **Charged vocabulary to handle with care:** WWII references (*bezetting, oorlog* — handle warfare imagery soberly given occupation memory); avoid nationalistic *vaderland/bloed* in a redemptive sense; *"de Gouden Eeuw"* / colonial echoes — steer clear of triumphalist national-glory framing. Keep "blood" strictly the **blood of Christ** (*het bloed van Jezus*), reverent.
- **Reverence/honorific forms required when addressing God:** use **"U"** (reverent address), capitalized *U/Uw/Hem/Hij/Zijn*; strict-Reformed register may use **"Gij/Gij zijt"** (archaic-reverent) — match the album's register. Title address: **"Heer" / "Heere"** (Statenvertaling), vocative **"o Heer," "Heer Jezus," "Vader."** Never casual.
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** Dutch **psalmberijming** (the metrical Psalms — the *Geneefse* psalter / *1773* and *Nieuwe Berijming*) and the rich **gezang** hymn tradition expect **regular meter and clean end-rhyme**. Reach for honest end-rhyme, steady iambic/trochaic feet, and parallelism — the psalm-singing ear is well trained, so meter that scans perfectly is rewarded.

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY (Dutch-specific)

- **Match syllable count; compress with the elisions Dutch singers actually use.** Drop the schwa-**e** of unstressed endings (*"vreugd'" for vreugde, "ziel" already short, "Heer'" , "lief'"*); contractions and clitics: ***'t* (het), *'n* (een), *'k* (ik), *z'n* (zijn), *m'n* (mijn), *d'r* (haar/er), *naar 't, in 't, op 't, aan 't, voor 't.*** Use *"da's"* (dat is) sparingly (casual). Elide vowel-clashes (*"te eeuwig" → "t'eeuwig"* only if natural). Never pad with filler particles to fill a bar.
- **Respect Dutch stress.** Stress is usually on the **stem/first syllable** (*GE-na-de, VREUG-de, KO-ning, HEER-lijk-heid, HEI-land*); some loans shift (*Halle-LU-ja*). Put the stressed syllable on the strong beat; read aloud in rhythm. The **schwa /ə/** (unstressed *e*, as in *de, genad-e*) is weak — never put it on a strong/belted beat.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes.** Land big held notes on open vowels — *-a, -o, -ee, -uu, -ie* (*genade, glorie, Halleluja, Koning*). Avoid landing a long note on the schwa or a harsh final consonant cluster. The hook "Jubiluyah" ends open (-ah) — exploit it.
- **The Dutch guttural caveat for AI vocals:** the hard **"g" / "ch"** (the *zachte/harde g*, e.g. in *God, genade, geest, heilig, lach, nacht*) is famously guttural and can sound scrapey or be mis-rendered by Suno/TTS, especially on fast runs or sustained notes. **Prefer vowel-final or open-syllable line-ends on sustained notes**, and don't stack guttural+consonant clusters (*"prachtig," "krachtig," "nachtwacht"*) on quick passages. Place words like *genade/glorie* (which START with g but resolve to open vowels) on held notes rather than words ENDING in *-cht/-g.*
- **Diminutives (-je/-tje/-pje):** Dutch loves the diminutive (*kindje, liedje, hartje*), but it carries a **tender/small/cozy** connotation — fine for intimacy (*"Kindje in de kribbe"* = baby in the manger), **wrong for majesty.** Never diminutize God or grand imagery for a rhyme (*"Koninkje," "Heertje"* are absurd/irreverent).
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot** (lands at 0:00–0:08). The chorus arrives by the same bar.
- **Rhyme stance for Dutch: USE end-rhyme (the psalm/gezang ear expects it), but never force it.** Aim to preserve AABB/ABAB **with words that genuinely belong.** The moment rhyme demands an invented word, a filler particle (*toch, wel, voorwaar*), a broken inflection, or distorted meaning — **abandon it**: near-rhyme, assonance, or recast the OTHER line. A true unrhymed line beats a false rhymed one.
- **Reach for the native poetic device (§1A):** regular psalm-metrical feet, clean end-rhyme, parallelism.
- **Diacritics/spelling mandatory where used** (ë, ï, é, the trema/diaeresis and acute): *"geërfd," "geïnspireerd," "één"* (one, with accents) vs *"een"* (a/an); *"vóór"* (before) vs *"voor"* (for) where disambiguation matters. The **ij** digraph is one sound — write *ij* (not *y*). Spell *ee/oo/aa/uu* fully (Suno pronounces from text: *"zon"* = sun vs *"zoon"* = son — get the double vowel right, it's theologically load-bearing here!).
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud (slurred, as an AI vocal would) and check blurred syllables don't form an unintended vulgar/comic word, and that **zon/zoon, man/maan, bos/boos** type minimal pairs aren't garbled. Re-voice if so.

## 5. IDIOMS, IMAGERY & CULTURAL FIT

- **Translate the sense, not the words → native idioms.** "permission slip" → *"je hoeft geen toestemming te vragen"*; "Sunday morning bell" → *"de klok op zondagmorgen"* (church bells universal in Dutch towns — works); "at the end of my rope" → *"ten einde raad"* / *"aan het einde van mijn krachten."*
- **Keep concrete sensory anchors** (bathroom floor at 3 AM, cage door, wedding feast, wine like a river) — render vividly in Dutch (*"op de koude vloer om drie uur 's nachts"*). Don't abstract them away.
- **Localize culture-bound American daily-life anchors** to emotionally equivalent Dutch scenes: *carpool line / school run* → *"op de fiets naar school," "in de file"*; *diner / drive-thru* → *"de snackbar," "de keukentafel"*; *credit-card debt* → *"de schulden die me verstikken"* (doubles with the *schuld* = guilt frame).
- **Do NOT localize away the global/heaven imagery** — every-tribe, throne, Lamb, wedding feast, New Jerusalem stay intact.

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**Dutch (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, frame-appropriate):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jezus is de reden dat we juichen nu! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / De Koning komt terug — ik reken op U!

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; *Jezus* (consistent name); "Jesus is the reason we are dancing now" → *Jezus is de reden dat we juichen nu* — I rendered "dancing" as **juichen** ("rejoice/shout for joy"), which carries the same exuberant payload while reading natively across Reformed and evangelisch alike (§1A), and SAID so; the subclause keeps Dutch verb placement natural; "the King is coming back" → *De Koning komt terug* (warm, royal-natural); the resolve "somehow, somehow" → *ik reken op U* ("I'm counting on You," reverent **U**) keeps the note of confident hope; *nu / U* is an honest rhyme; zero offense across traditions.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A):** suppose you need a rhyme for *"genade"* (grace).
- ❌ **Forced:** *"…ons geluk bracht ons genade, voorwaar, op het pade!"* — *geluk* (luck) implies salvation is fortune (bad theology, §0A.9), *voorwaar* is archaic filler, *op het pade* is a fake/strained form jammed in only to rhyme.
- ✅ **Meaning-first (rhyme kept):** *"…aan 't kruis schenkt Jezus ons genade — Hij heelt de schade."* — real words, sound doctrine (grace from Christ at the cross), *genade/schade* honest near-rhyme, guilt–innocence frame (He heals the damage/debt).
- ✅ **Also fine (rhyme dropped):** *"…aan 't kruis schenkt Jezus ons genade; mijn schuld is volledig betaald."* — no rhyme, but vivid guilt–innocence imagery (my debt fully paid) and crystal-clear. **A true unrhymed line beats a false rhymed one.**

## 10. ADDICTIVENESS / RE-LISTENABILITY PASS (after the album is produced in Dutch)

After the Dutch album is produced, run the full addictiveness pass. This tunes the **English `Style` block (§6)** and the Suno re-gen prompts ONLY — **never** doctrine or the locked translation.

**Three listen passes:**
1. **Feeling pass** — listen as a normal Dutch believer. Where does it move you? Where does attention drift? Does the *verlangen*/guilt–innocence payload land (§1A)?
2. **Craft pass** — hook strength, groove/pocket, vocal delivery, arrangement, mix clarity, ear-candy.
3. **Replay-trigger pass** — what makes you hit repeat? Identify the most addictive 2–4 seconds; make sure it's not buried.

**Amplify (via the English Style block + re-gen only):**
- **Hook & delivery** — make "Jubilujah"/the chorus hook land harder and earlier; sharpen vocal attack.
- **Groove & pulse** — target a head-nod pocket (~100–128 BPM where the style allows; keep psalm/hymnic tracks at their proper tempo).
- **Texture & ear-candy** — tasteful, culturally resonant instrumentation (church organ / psalm-style choir for hymnic; modern pop for CCM) and small surprises.
- **Dynamic arc & payoff** — restrained verse → fuller chorus → bridge lift → final-chorus payoff (Dutch sobriety rewards a delayed, earned swell).
- **Loop point & album flow** — make the end invite a replay; sequence the album to flow.
- **Emotional pull & relatability** — using §1A's guilt–innocence frame and the *verlangen* ache word.

**Re-rate each track + the album BEFORE/AFTER** across **hook strength, groove, replay value, dynamic payoff, emotional pull, loop-ability** (e.g. 1–10). Deliver a summary of changes, why each boosts re-listening, and the measurable before/after effect.
