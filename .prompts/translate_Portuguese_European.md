**Purpose:** Translate finished English (EN) Jubilee/Inspire song lyrics into **singable, natural, faith-appropriate, culturally resonant European Portuguese (pt-PT)** — not a literal gloss, and **not** a Brazilian translation. A good pt-PT translation **sings as well as the original, is instantly understandable and *relatable* to a Portuguese believer, and does not offend any faith community in Portugal** (Catholic majority, small but real evangélico/Protestant minority). Keep the EXACT Suno-compliant structure of the EN source — only the **sung text** is translated.

Do NOT reuse the Brazilian (pt-BR) translation.** EP differs from BR in vocabulary, vowels (closed/reduced), address (*tu/você/o senhor*), worship temperature (more restrained), and faith landscape (Catholic-majority, small evangelical community). ## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (skopos = performance). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to meaning & the gospel** — theology must survive intact (the cross, the resurrection, the blood, the name of Jesus, repentance, the King's return); never alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, and open vowels fit the melody.
3. **Natural & culturally resonant European Portuguese** — it sounds like a Portuguese believer wrote it; no calques, no English word order, **no Brazilianisms**; uses the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture; swap an image only if untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook** — match the rhyme scheme ONLY with real, sensible words; always keep the hook's rhythmic placement.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** every line must make complete, natural sense to a Portuguese listener with the rhyme ignored. Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement. Ship a clear unrhymed/near-rhymed true line over a forced one. Never invent a word, bend grammar, or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. If no honest rhyme fits, **DROP IT**. Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line (delete the final rhyme word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?).
>
> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to Portugal freely — but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity to be more relatable. Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form (1 Cor 1:23). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect. In Portugal's deeply Catholic-cultural setting, contextualize *toward shared Bible vocabulary* — do not import (nor mock) Marian/saint devotion to seem more "Portuguese"; stay Christ-centred and Bible-plain.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

1. **No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler words.** Don't end a line with a word there *only* to rhyme. pt-PT offenders to avoid: **outrora/dantes** (archaic "of yore" jammed for a rhyme), **lida/labuta** ("toil") dropped for an *-uta/-ida* rhyme, **azul** stuck on with no sky in the scene, **enfim/afinal** as throwaway line-enders, **deveras/decerto** (stiff archaisms used as padding), **lá/cá** stretched to fill a bar. **[EP≠BR]** the diminutive **-zinho** reads even more sentimental/folksy in EP and must never pad a worship climax. **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it.
2. **Only REAL words & correct verb forms.** EP traps: subjunctive misuse (*se eu **vou*** → *se eu **for***; *quando ele **vem*** as future → *quando ele **vier***); **[EP≠BR] EP strongly prefers the synthetic future and the inflected forms** (*estaremos, far-se-á*) and **mesoclisis** in elevated register (*dar-te-ei, levantar-me-ei*) where BR would use *vou dar*; pick the register and keep it. Past participles: *foi **pago*** (short irregular with *ser/estar*), not *pagado*.
3. **Correct prepositions & set phrases — no calques.** Caught: *apaixonado **com*** → **apaixonado POR**; "count on" → **contar COM** (not *contar em*); *cheio **com*** → **cheio DE**; "the reason that/why" → not *a razão **que*** → **a razão PELA QUAL / por isso**; "in Jesus' name" → **em nome de Jesus**.
4. **No English-calque or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** Caught: *chorei **para** um tecto* ("cried into a ceiling") → *gritei para o céu vazio*; "on the line to heaven" (phone line) → *no caminho que envio ao céu*; nominalized "the I-can't" → *cada "não consigo"*, never *o "não-posso"*; de-hyphenate stacked English compounds.
5. **Loanwords/register that misread in pt-PT.** **[EP≠BR] use EP vocabulary, not Brazilian:** *comboio* (not BR *trem*) for train, *autocarro* (not *ônibus*) for bus, *pequeno-almoço* (not *café da manhã*), *casa de banho* (not *banheiro*), *telemóvel* (not *celular*), *rapariga* = "girl" in EP (NEVER use it in BR sense — in BR it is offensive — but here we are in EP, so *rapariga* is normal and fine), *fixe* = "cool" (EP slang, keep out of reverent lines), *giro/gira* = "nice/pretty". Also: **ringue** = boxing ring → use **pista/chão**; **público** in a throne-room line reads like a concert audience → **adoradores / quem ouve / o povo**; **mandarim** the language (never *mandarina*, the fruit). Never drop EP slang (*fixe, giro, bué, ganda*) into a worship line.
6. **Adjectives & participles agree in gender/number.** A feminine narrator must not sing *estou **perdido*** → **perdida**; **FIX NARRATOR GENDER FIRST** (§4), then keep all agreement.
7. **No grammatically stranded tails** — recast so every word has a grammatical home.
8. **Imagery internally consistent** — no *fogo/chama* appended onto a cold-floor scene; no object without antecedent.
9. **Theology over rhyme** — no rhyme word distorts doctrine; *sorte/acaso* (luck/chance) stays subordinate to God's sovereignty; the Spirit is the Person *Espírito Santo*, never an impersonal *energia/força*; the struggle never "gives grace" — Christ does.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** delete the final rhyming word; if the rest isn't true, clear, and natural, the line is forced — rewrite it meaning-first, even unrhymed.

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

Portugal is **overwhelmingly Roman Catholic by culture and heritage** (with widespread secularism in practice), and has a **small but genuine evangelical/Protestant minority** (Igreja Evangélica, Baptist, Assembleias de Deus, Brethren, Pentecostal) plus growing Brazilian-led congregations. **[EP≠BR]** Unlike Brazil, the evangelical community is small and the dominant cultural-religious memory is Catholic and more reserved. Jubilee/Inspire songs are CCM/evangelical in origin, but the pt-PT version must **hold for the whole Portuguese Body of Christ.**

**Cross-tradition safety rules (PT-specific):**
- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** all traditions own: *Deus, Senhor, Jesus, Cristo, Espírito Santo, Pai, Salvador, graça, glória, Reino, a cruz, a ressurreição, o Cordeiro, Aleluia, Ámen.*
- **Lean on the standard Bible.** The **Almeida** family is the standard Portuguese Protestant Bible (**Almeida Corrigida e Fiel / Almeida Revista e Corrigida**, and **NVI-PT**); among Catholics the **Bíblia dos Capuchinhos / Difusora Bíblica** is most common. For broadest comfort echo plain Almeida phrasing (John 19:30 → **"Está consumado"**; Rev 22:20 → **"Vem, Senhor Jesus"**; Ps 23 → *"O Senhor é o meu pastor"*). Both Almeida and Catholic translations agree on these canonical lines.
- **Do NOT import tradition-specific practices** the source lacks: no Marian devotion (no *Nossa Senhora de Fátima*, no rosary/terço), no saint invocation, no liturgical formulas of one church, no glossolalia naming. **[EP≠BR]** Fátima/Marian imagery is culturally enormous in Portugal — precisely why it must stay OUT of these cross-tradition Christ-centred lyrics; neither invoke it nor mock it. Christ-centred, Bible-plain.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Capitalize divine pronouns where it aids reverence. **[EP≠BR]** EP worship is more **restrained and reverent** than BR exuberance — keep dignity foremost (see §1A). Never crude slang in a worship line.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** *Noiva/Noivo* is biblical and fine; keep it pure.
- **Keep the offense of the cross, remove the offense of bad form** (1 Cor 1:23). Say sin, blood, repentance, judgment, and Christ's exclusivity in clean, dignified European Portuguese.
- **Gender/inclusive-language & family:** defer to the Portuguese receptor community's norms (traditional in most evangelical/Catholic settings); do not import source-culture debates.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP  (the relatability layer)

- **Worldview frame — a guilt–innocence + honor–shame BLEND, leaning guilt-innocence.** Portugal is a Western European, broadly guilt-innocence culture (sin/debt/justification reads naturally) with a real Mediterranean honor–shame layer (family honour, *vergonha*, the prodigal welcomed home). **Foreground the legal/guilt-innocence images first** — *dívida paga, culpa lavada, livre da condenação, declarado justo* — and **support with the relational/restored-honour images** — the Father who runs to embrace (Luke 15), adoption into the family (*filho/filha, herdeiro*), shame lifted. **[EP≠BR]** lean less hard on the high-energy spiritual-warfare/fear-power register than you would for Brazil; keep authority-over-darkness imagery but reverent, not frenetic, and always channelled through Christ (§0A.9).
- **Taboo/danger images → safe substitutes:**
  - "Buddy/homeboy Jesus," chummy *gajo/pá Jesus* → keep dignity: *Jesus, meu Senhor / meu Amigo fiel* (*Amigo*, John 15).
  - Generic spirit-energy language → never; the Spirit is **o Espírito Santo**, a Person.
  - Fire: keep clearly **redemptive/refining** (*fogo que purifica*), tied to the Holy Spirit/Pentecost, not judgment.
  - Excess/"drunk in the Spirit"/wine-flowing → render as *alegria que transborda*, not literal drunkenness — **[EP≠BR] EP taste is more sober**, so under-do rather than over-do exuberance language.
  - Avoid *destino/sorte/fado* **as a governing force**: note that **fado** (the EP cultural concept of melancholic fate/longing) is culturally central — its *mood* (saudade) is usable, but never present fate itself as sovereign over God.
- **Native ache word / sacred-longing idiom:** **saudade** — the deep ache of loving absence and longing, **the defining Portuguese emotion** (even more culturally central in Portugal than Brazil, bound up with fado, the sea, emigration, and the discoveries). Use it for the believer's longing for God's presence, for heaven, for Christ's return (*que saudade do Teu rosto / saudade do céu*). This is your single highest-resonance key in EP.
- **Worship temperature: more RESTRAINED / reflective than Brazil.** **[EP≠BR]** Portuguese worship tends toward reverence, contemplation, and lament-tinged longing over loud celebration. Joy is real but expressed with more dignity; "dancing before the Lord" is acceptable but lands better as *celebrar/cantar/erguer as mãos* than literal *dançar* in many congregational settings — keep *dançar* where the song demands it, but don't amplify exuberance beyond the EN. Reach instead for depth, awe, and saudade.
- **Charged vocabulary to handle with care:** *energia/força/vibração* off-limits for the divine; *fado/destino* never sovereign; Brazilianisms read as foreign/wrong (a constant trap — see §0A.5). Colonial/discoveries/"conquering the nations" imagery — handle warfare/conquest language carefully given Portugal's colonial history; prefer *toda a terra ouvirá / todos os povos* over conquest framing. *Rei/Reino* (King/Kingdom) is fine (Portugal was a monarchy; no anti-royal baggage).
- **Reverence forms:** **[EP≠BR] EP has a sharper address hierarchy.** *Tu* (intimate/poetic — common in hymns and prayer to God, *Tu és, Tu vens, contigo*) is the heightened sacred register and very natural in EP worship; *você* is socially distant/formal in EP (NOT the warm-intimate it is in BR — do **not** default to *você* with God as you might in BR); *o Senhor* is highly formal. **Prefer *Tu* with correct verb forms for addressing God**, and keep one register per album.
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** the **redondilha** (5- and 7-syllable lines) of traditional Portuguese verse and fado; flowing assonance and the melancholy-longing cadence; restraint and a strong final image over hammered end-rhyme.

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY (pt-PT-specific)

- **FIX NARRATOR GENDER FIRST.** Decide the narrator's gender (or stay neutral) BEFORE translating, then make every self-referential adjective/participle agree (*cansado/cansada, perdido/perdida, salvo/salva*).
- **Match syllable count; compress with EP elisions singers actually use.** EP allows *pra* (para), *prò/prà* (para o/a), *tá* (está, colloquial), *num/numa* (em um/uma), *d'água, n'alma, co'a*. **[EP≠BR] crucially, EP REDUCES and often deletes unstressed vowels**, especially final/atonic **-e** (*verdade* sung ≈ *ver-DAD'*; *noite* ≈ *NOIT'*; *forte* ≈ *FORT'*) and a "swallowed" atonic *a/o*. This actually **helps the syllable count** — EP fits more text per bar than BR because BR keeps those vowels fuller. Plan held notes on the STRESSED open vowel, since the trailing vowel may vanish.
- **Respect EP stress and closed-vowel phonology.** **[EP≠BR] EP vowels are more closed and centralized**: stressed *e* tends closed/central, atonic *o → u*, atonic *e → ə (often dropped)*, atonic *a → ɐ*. Sibilants palatalize: final/pre-consonant **-s → "sh"** (*os santos* ≈ *ush SHAN-tush*); plan this so a held note doesn't land on a hissing tail. Nasals (*-ão, -ã, -em, -om*) ring well on sustained notes.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes.** Land big notes on **á, ã, ó, ão, é** (*glória, aleluia, coração, Senhor, Jesus, amor*). Avoid landing a long note on a reduced/dropped final vowel or an "sh"-sibilant tail.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot** (0:00–0:08); the chorus arrives by the same bar.
- **Rhyme is OPTIONAL and last.** Portuguese rhymes easily — a trap inviting filler. Prefer flowing **assonance and the melancholy redondilha cadence** over forced perfect rhyme; the moment a rhyme forces awkward syntax, an archaism (*outrora, deveras*), or distorted meaning — **drop it**. Recast the OTHER line of the couplet instead.
- **Reach for the native poetic device (§1A):** redondilha (5-/7-syllable), assonance, restrained longing cadence; a strong closing image beats a hammered rhyme.
- **Diacritics are mandatory** (á â ã à é ê í ó ô õ ú ç). **[EP≠BR]** mind EP-specific spellings/accents per the Acordo Ortográfico as adopted in Portugal (e.g. EP traditionally *facto, contacto, óptimo*-type forms vary by edition) — keep one consistent EP orthography. Suno/TTS pronounce from text: *"coracao"* mis-sings; **"coração"** sings right.
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud, slurred as an AI vocal would, and check blurred syllables don't form a vulgar/comic word. EP traps: avoid slurs producing **caralho/foda/puta/cona/merda/cu**; watch the EP vowel-deletion making two words collide into an obscene onset; re-voice if any appear. (Note *rapariga* is fine in EP though offensive in BR — but this is the EP file.)

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**pt-PT (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, frame-appropriate, restrained):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus é a razão, por isso celebramos! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / O Rei vai regressar — e nós cantamos!

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; **Jesus** consistent; **[EP≠BR]** "dancing now" rendered as *celebramos* rather than literal *dançamos* — fitting EP's **more restrained** worship temperature (§1A) while keeping the joy; "the reason that" rendered *a razão, por isso* (NOT the calque *a razão **que***); **[EP≠BR]** "coming back" → *vai regressar* (EP-natural; BR would lean *vai voltar*); rhyme *celebramos / cantamos* is real and sensible; meaning intact; offense-free across Catholic and evangelical PT.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A):** suppose you need a rhyme for *agora* ("now").
- ❌ Forced: *"…a luta deu-nos força e **outrora**!"* — "the struggle gave us strength and *of-yore*": *outrora* is archaic filler AND "the struggle gave strength" is bad theology AND *força* drifts toward energy-language.
- ✅ Meaning-first: *"…Jesus deu-nos vitória **agora**!"* — real words, sound doctrine, still rhymes, Christ is the source (note EP enclitic *deu-nos*, not BR-leaning *nos deu*).
- ✅ Also fine (rhyme dropped): *"…Jesus venceu; a vitória é d'Ele!"* — no rhyme with *agora*, but clear and true. **A true unrhymed line beats a false rhymed one.**

## 10. ADDICTIVENESS / RE-LISTENABILITY PASS (after the album is produced in pt-PT)

Run AFTER production, on the produced pt-PT audio. Three listen passes:
1. **Feeling pass** — does it move you the first time? Where does attention drift?
2. **Craft pass** — hook clarity, vocal delivery, mix, groove pocket, arrangement; confirm the accent reads European, not Brazilian.
3. **Replay-triggers pass** — the loop point, the ear-candy moment, the payoff.

Then amplify, tuning the **English `Style` block (§6)** and re-gen prompts ONLY — never doctrine, never the locked translation:
- **Hook & delivery** — make the "Jubiluyah" hook front-loaded and unmistakable.
- **Groove & pulse** — target a head-nod pocket (~100–128 BPM where the style allows); **[EP≠BR]** EP styles often sit a touch more reflective/mid-tempo than BR — respect that pull.
