## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the translator's *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to the meaning & the gospel** — the theology must survive intact (the cross, the resurrection, the name of Yeshua, repentance, the King's return). Never soften or alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, and open vowels fit the melody. A line a native can't sing comfortably has failed, even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant Hebrew** — it must sound like a native Hebrew-speaking believer wrote it, not a translated English song. No calques, no English word order; it uses the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture (bathroom floor, cage door, wedding feast). Swap an image only when it is untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo in Israel (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook preserved** — match the rhyme scheme *only with real, sensible words*; ALWAYS preserve the hook's rhythmic placement.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**. Break a rhyme before you break the meaning or the meter.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** Every line must make complete, natural sense to a native Hebrew speaker *with the rhyme ignored.* **Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement.** Ship a clear unrhymed/near-rhymed true line over a forced one. Never invent a word, bend grammar (no wrong binyan/gender/construct just to fake a rhyme), or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. If no honest rhyme fits, **DROP IT** — use blank verse, assonance, or near-rhyme. Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line (delete the final rhyme word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?).
>
> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** Adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to Hebrew/Israeli culture freely — but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Yeshua's exclusivity to be more relatable. **Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form** (1 Cor 1:23). In a Jewish-majority context this law is acutely tested: it is exactly the divine sonship of the Messiah (**בֶּן־הָאֱלוֹהִים**) that the surrounding culture rejects — **contextualize the form, never deny the doctrine** (see §2). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

Before finalizing any line, check it against this list — these are the failure modes a native Hebrew-speaking believer instantly hears as wrong.

**1. No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler words.** Don't end a line with a word that's there only to rhyme. Real offenders to avoid: an empty **הִנֵּה** ("behold," dropped in as filler), **אַכֵּן / אָכֵן** ("indeed," over-archaic filler), a stranded **כֵּן** ("yes/thus"), **לָנֶצַח** ("forever") jammed where it doesn't belong, **בְּלִי סוֹף** ("endless") as a generic tail, or archaic **עֲדֵי־עַד** stuck on for the rhyme. **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.
**2. Only REAL words & correct grammar.** No invented roots; respect the **binyan** system (use the right verb pattern: *הִצִּיל* hifil "rescued" vs *נִצַּל* nifal "was rescued") and correct tense/person. Common traps: wrong gender on the verb (Hebrew conjugates for the *subject's* gender — see #6), wrong construct (*סְמִיכוּת*) form (*בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ* good; don't keep the article on the first noun), mixing up similar roots.
**3. Correct prepositions & set phrases — no calques.** Hebrew verbs govern specific prepositions: *חיכה **ל*** (waited for), not *חיכה על*; *האמין **ב*** (believed in), not *האמין על*; *חשב **על*** (thought about). Don't calque English "depend on," "give up on," "look forward to" — use the real Hebrew idiom (*נשען על*, *ויתר על*, *מצפה ל*).
**4. No English-calque or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** Don't render "on the line to heaven" as a phone line (*על הקו לשמיים* reads as a phone line in Hebrew too), or "reach out" / "connect" / "signal" as networking metaphors. Recast into native sensory images (a spring/*מַעְיָן*, a gate/*שַׁעַר*, light/*אוֹר*).
**5. Loanwords/register that misread.** Modern Hebrew is full of English/Yiddish/Arabic loans, but they break a sacred register fast: *אוֹקֵיי, סְבַּבָּה* (cool/great, Arabic-origin slang), *יָאללָה* (Arabic "let's go"), *פִיצֶ'ר/וַיְבּ* all jar in worship. Beware false register: a casual Modern-Hebrew word where a more elevated/biblical one is needed. Keep one dignified register.
**6. Gender agreement — FIX NARRATOR GENDER FIRST.** Hebrew is **heavily gendered**: verbs, adjectives, and pronouns inflect for the speaker's and addressee's gender (*אֲנִי אוֹהֵב* = a male loves; *אֲנִי אוֹהֶבֶת* = a female loves; *אַתָּה* vs *אַתְּ* "you"). **Before translating, decide the narrator's gender** (per the song's vocal/persona) and keep every first-person form consistent. When addressing God, second-person masculine singular is standard (*אַתָּה, לְךָ, אוֹתְךָ*). A rhyme that breaks gender agreement is rejected — fix the rhyme partner.
**7. No grammatically stranded tails.** No construct chain whose second noun never arrives; no relative *שֶׁ/אֲשֶׁר* clause with no head; no dangling preposition.
**8. Imagery internally consistent.** Don't append an image that contradicts the scene or introduce an object with no antecedent. Match the imagery already in the verse.
**9. Theology over rhyme.** No rhyme word that distorts doctrine. Keep grace from Messiah alone; keep luck/fate (**מַזָּל**, "luck" — heavily used colloquially, even *מזל טוב*) strictly subordinate to God's sovereignty — never let "luck/mazal" stand where the gospel means God's deliberate hand.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** silently delete the final rhyming word — does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use? If no, the line is forced; rewrite it meaning-first, even unrhymed.

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

The Hebrew-speaking Body of Christ is **small and embattled**: the **Messianic Jewish** congregations (*קהילות משיחיות*) who confess Yeshua as Messiah while keeping Jewish identity, plus **Hebrew-speaking Arab Christians**, and a scattering of evangelical/Protestant believers — all inside an overwhelmingly **Jewish-majority, partly secular, partly Orthodox** society where faith in Yeshua is a sensitive, sometimes contentious, minority position. Jubilee/Inspire songs are CCM/evangelical in origin, but the Hebrew version must **hold for this whole community** — and be **HIGH-sensitivity** toward the surrounding Jewish culture it lives within.

**Cross-tradition safety rules (Hebrew-specific):**
- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** the believing community owns: *אֱלוֹהִים / הָאָדוֹן (אֲדוֹנָי) / יֵשׁוּעַ / הַמָּשִׁיחַ / הָאָב / רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ / חֶסֶד / כָּבוֹד / מַלְכוּת / הַצְּלָב / הַתְּקוּמָה / הַשֶּׂה / הַלְלוּיָהּ / אָמֵן.*
- **Anchor Scripture echoes to the Modern Hebrew New Testament** — the **Delitzsch** translation and the modern **"הברית החדשה"** edition of *החברה לכתבי הקודש בישראל* (the Bible Society in Israel) are the most broadly recognized. For the Tanakh, the Hebrew text itself is the source. When a line quotes/echoes Scripture, echo these: John 19:30 ("It is finished") → **"כִּלָּה"** / singable **"נִשְׁלַם"**; Rev 22:20 → **"בּוֹא נָא הָאָדוֹן יֵשׁוּעַ"**.
- **Honor the Jewish roots; AVOID supersessionism.** Do **not** frame the church as having *replaced* Israel, or speak of the "old" covenant/law with contempt; the gospel here is *fulfillment*, not replacement. Lean into the Jewish-rooted joy — God's faithfulness to Israel, the Messiah of Israel for all nations. No "we are the true Israel now" framing.
- **Do NOT import practices the source lacks** that would jar this community: no Marian/saint/icon devotion (foreign to both Jewish and evangelical sensibility), no church-specific liturgical formulas, no glossolalia naming. Christ-centered, Bible-plain.
- **Reverent register toward God,** and **extra care around the Divine Name.** Observant Jewish sensibility avoids writing/voicing the Tetragrammaton (יהוה) casually; use **אֲדוֹנָי / הָאָדוֹן** (the Lord) or **הַשֵּׁם** ("the Name") in reverent reference rather than vocalizing the Tetragrammaton in a pop lyric. No street slang in worship (avoid *סבבה, יאללה, אחלה* in reverent lines).
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** Bride/Bridegroom (**הַכַּלָּה / הֶחָתָן**) is biblical (and resonates with Jewish wedding imagery) — keep it pure.
- **Keep the offense of the cross, remove the offense of bad form** (1 Cor 1:23). Don't dilute sin, blood, repentance, judgment, or Yeshua's exclusivity — say them in clean, dignified Hebrew.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP (the relatability layer)

- **Worldview frame: HONOR–SHAME (with a strong covenant/belonging thread).** Lead here. Foreground images of: **belonging to the people of God / being grafted in** (covenant family, *עַם / מִשְׁפָּחָה*), **the faithful God of the covenant who keeps His word** (*נֶאֱמָן*, faithfulness/*אֱמוּנָה*), **return from exile / restoration** (*שִׁיבָה / גְּאֻלָּה*, redemption — a word loaded with national-spiritual hope), **the Father who runs to embrace** the returning child, and **honor restored** to the ashamed. The fear-power note (Messiah's authority over darkness) is present but secondary. Guilt-innocence/legal framing is true but not the primary emotional entry.
- **Taboo / danger images → safe native substitutes:**
  - "Buddy/homeboy Jesus," casual chumminess → **NO.** Warmth yes, but with reverence; *הָאָדוֹן יֵשׁוּעַ*, not a back-slapping pal.
  - **Dancing** — actually *welcome* here: dancing before the Lord is deeply Jewish/biblical (David danced; *רִקּוּדִים* at every celebration). Keep **רוֹקְדִים / נָרוּצָה בְּשִׂמְחָה** — this is a rare place where "dance" lands natively and joyfully.
  - **Wine** — biblical and culturally fine (Kiddush, Shabbat, joy); *יַיִן* as the wine of gladness/communion is welcome, just keep it the biblical image, not drunkenness.
  - **Cross / crucifix imagery** — handle with care: the physical *cross/צלב* historically connotes Christian persecution of Jews; keep the **theology of the atoning death and resurrection** central and clear, but be aware the bare symbol carries painful history — foreground Yeshua's sacrifice and rising rather than crucifix iconography.
  - **Fire** — keep clearly refining/Spirit vs judgment.
- **Native ache word / sacred-longing idiom: כִּסּוּפִים (kissufim)** — deep yearning/longing (used in liturgical poetry for longing toward God/Zion); also **גַּעְגּוּעַ / גַּעְגּוּעִים (ga'agu'im)** (aching longing/homesickness) and **כְּמִיהָה (kemihah)** (yearning). Reach for *כיסופים/כמיהה* for the soul's longing for God. **Worship temperature: REVERENT + CELEBRATORY (Messianic)** — Israeli/Messianic worship blends awe with exuberant, dance-ready joy (think Davidic celebration); embrace both the trembling and the simcha.
- **Charged vocabulary — handle with care:** **king/kingdom** (מֶלֶךְ/מַלְכוּת) is fine and biblical (God as King is core Jewish liturgy — *מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם*); **warfare/army/"conquering"** imagery is extremely sensitive in the Israeli context (active conflict, military service) — keep any "battle" strictly *spiritual* (Messiah's victory over death/sin), never militaristic or tied to current events; **homeland/land/nation/blood** (*אֶרֶץ / עַם / דָּם*) is politically explosive — the gospel's "kingdom" is heavenly and **for every tribe**, never an ethno-national or territorial claim. Avoid anything readable as a political statement on the conflict.
- **Reverence forms required when addressing God:** second-person masculine singular **אַתָּה** (You), **לְךָ** (to You), **אוֹתְךָ** (You-obj), vocative **אֲדוֹנָי / הָאָדוֹן / אֵלִי** ("my God"). Keep divine address in this elevated register; never the casual *אַתָּה* paired with slang.
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** **biblical/Hebraic parallelism** (*תקבולת* — the cornerstone of Psalms: synonymous, antithetic, and climactic parallel lines) and **construct-chain compactness** (*סְמִיכוּת*, dense noun phrases). Reach for parallel couplets that echo the Psalms; this *is* the native sacred-poetic instinct.

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY (Hebrew-specific)

- **Match syllable count to the melody.** Compress with natural elisions/contractions Hebrew singers use: the prefixed prepositions/conjunctions are single syllables that attach (**בְּ־ / לְ־ / מִ־ / כְּ־ / וְ־ / שֶׁ־ / הַ־**), e.g., *בַּלֵּב* (in the heart, one word), *לָנוּ* (to us); the definite article assimilates into a preposition (*בְּ + הַ → בַּ*). Use the directional **־ָה** suffix (*הַבַּיְתָה* "homeward") to save a preposition. Never pad with filler particles to fill a bar.
- **Respect Hebrew stress.** Modern Hebrew stress is usually **final-syllable (milra')**, sometimes penultimate (mil'el); put the natural stress on strong beats (e.g., *shaLOM, kaVOD, yeshuAH* are milra'). Don't force an unnatural accent. Read every line aloud in rhythm.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes** — end big held notes on open **a / o / e / u / i** (e.g., *הַלְלוּיָהּ*, *כָּבוֹד*, *שִׂמְחָה*, *יְשׁוּעָה*). The hook "Jubiluyah" already ends open (-yah) — exploit it. Avoid landing a long note on a closed cluster.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot** (lands at 0:00–0:08; keep it there); the chorus arrives by the same bar.
- **Rhyme stance for Hebrew: don't force it.** Hebrew rhymes readily (rich morphology means many shared suffixes — *־ים, ־וֹת, ־ָה*), but that ease tempts cheap suffix-rhymes (every line ending in *־ים* is monotonous, not clever). Prefer meaningful rhyme; the moment it forces bad syntax, an invented word, a filler tail, or a gender break — **abandon it** (near-rhyme/assonance/blank). Recast the OTHER line of the couplet to a natural rhyme-word. A true unrhymed line beats a false rhymed one.
- **Reach for the native poetic device (§1A):** Psalmic parallelism (*תקבולת*) and compact construct chains.
- **Niqqud (vowel points) MANDATORY.** Hebrew is normally written *without* vowels, so an unpointed line is ambiguous and **will mis-sing** (*דבר* could be *davar* "word," *diber* "spoke," *dever* "plague," *davér* "speak!"). **Point the full sung body and titles** with niqqud, and use the *malé* (full/plene) spelling plus niqqud as needed so the vocal pronounces correctly (e.g., write **יֵשׁוּעַ**, **נִשְׁלַם**, not bare *ישוע*, *נשלם*). This is the Hebrew counterpart of "always write full diacritics."
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud slurred (as the AI vocal would) and check that blurred/elided syllables across word boundaries don't form a vulgar, comic, or sacred-taboo word (Hebrew has short words that collide easily).

## 5. IDIOMS, IMAGERY & CULTURAL FIT

- **Translate the sense, not the words.** Use native idioms: "you don't need permission" → *אֵינְךָ צָרִיךְ רְשׁוּת*; "Sunday morning bell" → note Israel's week centers on **Shabbat**, not Sunday, and church bells aren't the universal cue — render the gathering/holiness of the day rather than literal Sunday bells, or use the believer's-congregation context explicitly.
- **Keep concrete sensory anchors** (bathroom floor at 3 AM, cage door, wedding feast, wine like a river) — render vividly in Hebrew (**רִצְפַּת הָאַמְבַּטְיָה בְּשָׁלוֹשׁ לִפְנוֹת בֹּקֶר**), applying the §1A screen (wine/dance are *fine* here; the cross-symbol carries history).
- **Localize culture-bound daily-life anchors** (carpool, diner, prom, credit-card debt) to emotionally equivalent native scenes (the morning commute, the kitchen table, *חוֹב* debt) — do **not** localize away the global/heaven imagery (every-tribe, throne, Lamb, wedding feast).

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**Hebrew (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, honor-shame/covenant frame, with niqqud, male narrator):**
> גֻ'בִּילוּיָה! גֻ'בִּילוּיָה! / יֵשׁוּעַ הוּא הַסִּבָּה — רוֹקְדִים עַכְשָׁו! / גֻ'בִּילוּיָה! גֻ'בִּילוּיָה! / הַמֶּלֶךְ עוֹד יָשׁוּב — בְּטוּחַ, זֶה עַכְשָׁו!

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; **יֵשׁוּעַ** (correct name, **never ישו**); "the reason we are dancing now" → **יֵשׁוּעַ הוּא הַסִּבָּה — רוֹקְדִים עַכְשָׁו** — note the §1A note: **"dancing" is KEPT (רוֹקְדִים)** because dancing before the Lord is natively Jewish and joyful (David danced); no taboo flip needed here, unlike Arabic; "the King is coming back" → **הַמֶּלֶךְ עוֹד יָשׁוּב** (heavenly King, *מֶלֶךְ* — resonant Jewish liturgy, not political); rhyme **עַכְשָׁו / עַכְשָׁו** is a true repetend (and *בָּטוּחַ* "for sure" carries the "somehow"). Fully pointed so the vocal pronounces it. Reverent yet celebratory (§1A worship temperature).

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A):**
- ❌ Forced/wrong: *"ישו הוא הסיבה, נרקוד אכן הנה"* — uses the **pejorative ישו** (offensive, wrong), piles filler **אָכֵן הִנֵּה** ("indeed behold") just to fill the bar, and is **unpointed** → mis-sings; also gender-ambiguous.
- ✅ Meaning-first: the line above — **יֵשׁוּעַ** (right name), no filler, full niqqud, consistent male-narrator forms, joyful-but-reverent. **A true, correctly-named, pointed line beats a forced, pejorative one.**

## 10. ADDICTIVENESS / RE-LISTENABILITY PASS (after the album is produced in Hebrew)

Run **three listen passes:** (1) **Feeling** — does it move you (reverent awe + celebratory simcha, §1A) on first hear? (2) **Craft** — hook clarity, melodic memorability, vocal delivery, instrumentation color. (3) **Replay triggers** — what makes you hit repeat?

Then amplify, tuning **only the English `Style` block (§6) and re-gen prompts — never doctrine or the locked translation:**
- **Hook & delivery:** make "גֻ'בִּילוּיָה" land harder; consider congregational call-and-response (deeply native to Israeli worship).
- **Groove & pulse:** lock a head-nod/dance pocket (~100–128 BPM where the style allows); Davidic-celebration claps and hand percussion aid replay.
- **Texture & ear-candy:** acoustic guitar, oud/violin lines, hand drums, tasteful klezmer-clarinet color — distinctive, not cluttered.
- **Dynamic arc & payoff:** build to a soaring, danceable final chorus/bridge; earn the lift.
- **Loop point & album flow:** make the outro invite the next track; order tracks for an emotional arc (longing → restoration → joy).
- **Emotional pull & relatability:** lean on §1A's honor-shame/covenant frame and the ache word **כיסופים** — longing/exile → return/redemption → celebratory joy.

**Re-rate each track + the album BEFORE/AFTER** across **hook strength, groove, replay value, dynamic payoff, emotional pull, loop-ability** (e.g., 1–10). Deliver a summary of changes, why each boosts re-listening, and the measurable before/after effect.
