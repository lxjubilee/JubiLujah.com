## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the translator's *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to meaning & the gospel** — the theological content must survive intact (the cross, the blood, the resurrection, the name of Jesus, repentance, the King's return). Never alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, open vowels fit the melody. Indonesian is clean and phonetic (a gift for AI vocals) but agglutinating affixes lengthen words; a line a native can't sing comfortably has failed even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant Indonesian** — it must sound like a native Indonesian believer wrote it, not like a translated English song. No calques, no English word order; use the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture (bathroom floor, cage door, wedding feast). Swap an image only when untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook** — match the rhyme scheme ONLY with real, sensible words; always keep the hook's rhythmic placement. Indonesian rhymes easily (open syllables, shared affix endings) — never let easy rhyme override sense.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**. Break a rhyme before you break the meaning or the meter.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** Every single line must make complete, natural sense to a native Indonesian *on its own, with the rhyme ignored.* **Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement.** Ship a clear unrhymed or near-rhymed TRUE line over a forced one. Never invent a word, bend grammar, or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. (Indonesian's easy *-kan/-lah/-nya* endings tempt lazy rhyme — resist.) If no honest rhyme fits, **DROP IT** — use blank verse, assonance, or near-rhyme. Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line: delete the final rhyme word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?

> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** Adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to the culture freely — warm, exuberant, communal worship — **but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity to be more relatable.** Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form (1 Cor 1:23). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

1. **No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler words.** Indonesian rhymes too easily because of shared affix endings; do not jam a word at the line end purely for sound. Real offenders to avoid: tacking on **-nya** ("its/the") to nothing meaningful just for the *-nya* rhyme; throwaway **deh / dong / kok / sih / lho** (colloquial particles — wrong register for worship) used as padding; vague filler nouns like **suasana** (ambience), **nuansa** (nuance), **pesona** (charm) dropped in to rhyme; pantun-style filler first lines that say nothing. **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.
2. **Only REAL words & correct affix forms.** Indonesian meaning rides on **affixes** (me-, di-, ber-, ter-, ke-…-an, pe-…-an, -kan, -i). Wrong affix = wrong/nonexistent word. Caught patterns: dropping the verbal prefix in formal lines (*aku **kasih*** colloquial → *aku **memberi***); confusing **ke-…-an** (state/abstract: *keselamatan* = salvation) with **pe-…-an** (process: *penyelamatan* = the act of saving) — pick the right one; **ter-** (accidental/superlative: *terselamatkan*) vs **di-** (passive: *diselamatkan*). Use the active/passive voice the line actually needs.
3. **Correct prepositions & set phrases — no calques.** Caught: English "thank you to God" calqued; "reason that…" calqued as *alasan **bahwa*** where natural Indonesian wants **karena / sebab itu**; "fall in love with" / English phrasal verbs calqued. Watch **di / ke / dari** (at / to / from) and the locative **di** vs the passive prefix **di-** (spacing: *di rumah* "at home" = two words; *dirumahkan* = one word) — a real, common error. **yang** relative clauses must attach correctly.
4. **No English-calque or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** Don't render "on the line to heaven" as a phone line (*saluran/sinyal*); don't calque "download," "connect," "recharge." Use native images. De-hyphenate English compound stacks into a natural Indonesian clause (*answer-in-the-dark* → *jawaban di tengah gelap*).
5. **Loanwords/register that misread in Indonesian.** Choose the warm middle register, not stiff bureaucratic Indonesian nor heavy slang. Traps: **gereja** = church (building/body) — fine; but beware Arabic-Islamic-colored religious vocabulary that signals a mosque context if misapplied (**masjid, sholat, ibadah haji, sajadah**) — use Christian terms (**ibadah** "worship/service" is shared and fine; **berdoa** "pray"). Watch **kasih** (love/give — context-dependent) vs **cinta** (romantic love — avoid for God except pure *kasih*). **sayang** = "dear/affection/pity/what-a-waste" — ambiguous, handle with care. Regional/Jakarta slang (**gue/lo, banget, gokil**) is wrong for worship. Dutch/English loans (*kristen, gereja, rohani*) are normal; flag any that misread.
6. **Affixed words & plurals must be correct (number, voice).** Indonesian has no gender (a gift — narrator gender is a non-issue) and marks plural by **reduplication** (*anak-anak* "children") or context — don't reduplicate where context already plurals it, and don't drop reduplication where it changes meaning (*mata* eye / *mata-mata* spy!). Match active/passive voice to the subject.
7. **No grammatically stranded tails.** Don't strand a *yang*, a *-nya*, or a possessive with no head. Every word needs a grammatical home.
8. **Imagery internally consistent.** Don't append an image that contradicts the scene (cold floor + a "blaze") or introduce an object with no antecedent.
9. **Theology over rhyme.** Never let a rhyme word distort doctrine: don't let **nasib / takdir** ("fate/destiny," fatalism-laden) become the agent of salvation — keep luck/chance subordinate to God's sovereignty (**kehendak Tuhan**, His will). Don't let **untung/keberuntungan** ("luck") replace grace. Grace comes from Christ, not fortune.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** silently delete the final rhyming word. Does the rest still say something true and clear, and is that final word one you'd actually use here? If no — rewrite meaning-first, even unrhymed.

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

Indonesian is the national language of the **world's largest Muslim-majority nation** — but Indonesia officially recognizes Christianity and has **large, open, worshipping Christian communities**: **Protestant** (the biggest blocs — e.g., GBI, HKBP, GPdI, Bethany, charismatic/Pentecostal megachurches) and **Catholic**, with Christian-majority regions (North Sumatra/Batak, North Sulawesi/Minahasa, Nusa Tenggara Timur, Papua, parts of Maluku). Sensitivity is **moderate (moderate-MBB)**: open worship is normal and protected, yet inter-religious tact matters nationally. The Indonesian version must **hold for the whole Indonesian Body of Christ** (Protestant + Catholic) and stay nationally gracious.

- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** all traditions own: *Tuhan, Yesus Kristus, Anak Bapa / Anak Tunggal, Roh Kudus, Bapa, Juruselamat, anugerah, kemuliaan, Kerajaan, salib, kebangkitan, Anak Domba, Haleluya, Amin* — drawn from the standard Indonesian Bible (below). (Per STRICT SOP #18 the bare token **"Allah"** is NOT used in lyrics — not even in *Anak Allah / Kerajaan Allah*; see §2.)
- **Anchor Scripture echoes** to the **Alkitab Terjemahan Baru (TB), Lembaga Alkitab Indonesia (LAI)** — the most broadly recognized Indonesian Bible across Protestant and (with deuterocanon) Catholic use. Canonical echoes: John 19:30 "It is finished" → **"Sudah selesai"**; Rev 22:20 "Come, Lord Jesus" → **"Datanglah, Tuhan Yesus"**. Allude to native Indonesian worship/hymnody (the rich *lagu rohani / pujian* tradition) where it strengthens trust.
- **No tradition-specific imports** the source lacks: no Marian/saint devotion, no one-church liturgical formula, no glossolalia naming. Christ-centered, Bible-plain — common ground across Protestant and Catholic.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Address God respectfully and warmly; capitalize divine pronouns where it aids reverence (**-Mu, -Nya, Engkau, Kau-** capitalized for God, standard in Indonesian Christian print). No vulgar/street-slang (*gue/lo, anjir, bangsat*) anywhere.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** Bride/Bridegroom (*Mempelai / Pengantin*) is biblical and fine; keep it pure — never *cinta/sayang* romantic-pop longing.
- **Gender/inclusive-language & family handling:** defer to THIS community's norms (Indonesian itself is largely gender-neutral, which simplifies this); do not import source-culture debates.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP  (the relatability layer)

- **Lead with the worldview frame: HONOR–SHAME** (with a real undercurrent of **fear–power**). Indonesian culture is deeply communal and face-conscious: *malu* (shame/embarrassment), *menjaga muka* (saving face), belonging to the *keluarga/jemaat* (family/congregation), and *rukun* (harmony). There's also a felt world of unseen powers (spirits, ancestral fear) across many islands. Foreground these biblical images (same doctrine, different emotional entry):
  - *Honor–shame:* the outcast **received, cleansed, given a new name and a place at the table**; the Father **running to embrace the returning child** (Luke 15); **adoption/belonging** to God's family (*diangkat menjadi anak, masuk keluarga Tuhan / keluarga Bapa* — not *keluarga Allah*, per §2); *malu* (shame) lifted and turned to honor; *muka* (face) restored before the community.
  - *Fear–power:* **Christ's authority over every dark power and spirit** (*berkuasa atas segala kuasa gelap*); **light swallowing darkness**; freedom from fear of fate/spirits because the living God reigns.
- **Taboo / danger images → safe native substitutes:**
  - **Wine "flowing like a river" / drunkenness** — alcohol is socially/religiously fraught nationally → keep the biblical wedding-feast and "new wine/overflowing cup" joy, lean on **the feast and overflowing blessing**, not drunkenness.
  - **Buddy/"homeboy" Jesus** — over-casual *bro/cuy/sob* register is offensive to the honor frame → use *Tuhan, Sahabat sejati* with reverent warmth.
  - **Dancing before the Lord** — generally acceptable in exuberant Indonesian charismatic worship (*menari/bergembira di hadapan Tuhan*), but keep it celebratory praise, not sensual; for the flagship hook, see §9.
  - **Fire** — redemptive/refining fire (*api yang memurnikan*) is fine; pair with light/refining, keep distinct from destructive/judgment-only contexts.
  - **Father imagery** — tender and strong; embrace the Father who runs and welcomes (very resonant with the family/honor frame).
  - **Pork/dog/left-hand** and other Islam-sensitive daily images — avoid gratuitous use nationally.
- **Native "ache" word / sacred-longing idiom:** **rindu** (deep longing/yearning — "rinduku pada-Mu," my longing for You) is the central ache word; **dahaga / haus** (thirst of the soul, Ps 42); **sanubari / lubuk hati** (the innermost heart) for depth of feeling. **Worship temperature: WARM-EXUBERANT** — joyful, communal, hands-raised celebration, with tender intimate moments.
- **Charged vocabulary to handle with care:** *nasib/takdir* (fate — subordinate to God, never the savior); *untung/keberuntungan* (luck — never replaces grace); Islam-colored religious terms (*sholat, masjid, sajadah, halal/haram* — don't borrow for Christian content); national/ethnic/religious harmony is law-protected (*SARA* sensitivities) — keep the gospel offered graciously, never othering another community.
- **Reverence/honorific forms required when addressing God:** Indonesian reverence is **lexical, not heavily grammatical** (no honorific verb conjugation like Korean/Javanese). Use the elevated/literary second person **Engkau / Kau-** (not casual *kamu*, never slang *lo/elu*) and the polite first person **aku** in worship (or **-ku** suffix), and **capitalize divine pronoun affixes -Mu / -Nya / Engkau / Dia**. (If a Javanese/Sundanese regional cut is ever requested, those languages DO have speech levels — but standard Bahasa Indonesia does not; keep to Engkau/-Mu.)
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** **pantun / syair** sensibility — Indonesian's natural **a-b-a-b** quatrain rhyme and **rima** on open final syllables; parallelism and call-and-response (very natural in Indonesian congregational worship — *seruan dan sahutan*). Reach for these where they fit, without forcing rhyme over sense.

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY (Indonesian-specific)

- **Match syllable count; affixes lengthen words.** Indonesian is open-syllable and very singable, but *me-…-kan / ke-…-an* affixes add beats. Compress with the natural elisions singers use: **tak** (for *tidak*), **'kan** (for *akan*), **'tuk / 'ntuk** (for *untuk*), **ku-/-ku, kau-/-mu, -nya** clitics (*kasih-Mu, di hati-ku*), **'lah** dropped where not needed. Choose the shorter true synonym (*beri* vs *memberikan*) to fit. Never pad with particles (*deh/dong/sih*) to fill a bar.
- **Respect Indonesian stress (penultimate, light).** Stress is mild and usually on the penultimate syllable; the language is fairly syllable-timed and even — good for melody. Don't force an unnatural accent. Read every line aloud in rhythm.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes.** Indonesian is vowel-rich (a, i, u, e, o) and ends most words on open vowels or sonorants — exploit it. End big held notes on **a, u, i** (e.g., *mulia, Yesus, kasih-Mu, sukacita, Raja*); the hook "Jubiluyah" ends open (-yah). Avoid landing a long note on a final -k/-t/-p glottal stop.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot;** the chorus arrives on the same bar.
- **Rhyme stance for Indonesian: rhyme comes easily — keep it OPTIONAL and last anyway.** Open syllables and shared affix endings make a-b-a-b and a-a-b-b rhyme cheap; that's exactly why it's a trap. Use rhyme only with words that genuinely belong; the moment it forces an empty *-nya*, a filler noun, or wrong syntax, **abandon it** (near-rhyme/assonance/blank). Prefer recasting the OTHER line. **A true unrhymed line beats a forced rhymed one.**
- **Reach for the native poetic device (§1A)** — pantun/syair quatrain rhyme on open vowels, parallelism, call-and-response.
- **Clean phonetic spelling is a gift for the AI vocal** — write standard Indonesian orthography exactly; it pronounces from the text. Watch **e** (it's pronounced two ways: *é* as in "enak" vs schwa *ê* as in "emas") — Suno may guess; choose words whose default reading is right, or accept the standard. No diacritics are normally written, but be aware of the *e* ambiguity for sung clarity.
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud slurred (as an AI vocal would) and check that blurred syllables don't form a vulgar Indonesian word (Indonesian has easy collisions and some words shift to slang/obscene meaning — verify the line-boundaries don't create one).

Localize culture-bound American daily-life anchors (carpool, diner, prom, credit-card debt) to emotionally equivalent native scenes (*antar-jemput sekolah, warung, beban utang*) — but **do NOT localize away the global/heaven imagery** (every-tribe, throne, Lamb, wedding feast — those stay).

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**Indonesian (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, frame-appropriate):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Yesus alasannya, kita menari kini! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Sang Raja 'kan datang — pasti, pasti!

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat (clean phonetic spelling; echoes Haleluya); *Yesus* consistent; **"dancing"** is kept as **menari** — exuberant celebratory worship dance is normal and welcome in Indonesian charismatic praise (§1A), so no substitution is needed here (kept pure, not sensual); "the King is coming back" → **Sang Raja 'kan datang** (*'kan* elision of *akan* keeps the syllable count; *Sang* lends honor); the "somehow, somehow" certainty becomes **pasti, pasti** ("surely, surely") — a confident hope that rhymes naturally with *kini* on open vowels. Meaning intact; warm-exuberant temperature; gracious across Protestant and Catholic.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A):**
- ❌ **Forced / wrong:** *Jubiluyah! Nasib bawa kita menari, ya deh suasananya!* — "Fate brought us to dance, yeah, the vibe!": **nasib** (fate) as the agent is bad theology, **ya deh** is colloquial filler (wrong register), and **suasananya** is an empty ambience-noun jammed on for the *-nya* rhyme — three §0A violations for cheap sound.
- ✅ **Meaning-first fix:** *Jubiluyah! Yesus beri hidup, mari menyanyi!* — "Jesus gives life — come, let's sing!": real words, sound doctrine (Yesus, not fate, is the agent), warm and exuberant (*mari menyanyi*), lands on open vowels, no filler. **A true line beats a fate-and-filler rhymed one.**
