# Jubilee Music — Korean (KO) Lyrics Translation Engine

**Purpose:** Translate finished English (EN) Jubilee/Inspire song lyrics into **singable, natural, faith-appropriate, culturally resonant Korean (KO)** — not a literal gloss. A good KO translation **sings as well as the original, is instantly understandable and *relatable* to a native Korean believer, and does not offend any Korean faith community** (the large, vibrant Protestant majority and the Catholic minority). ## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the translator's *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to the meaning & the gospel** — the theology must survive intact (the cross 십자가, the resurrection 부활, the name 예수, repentance 회개, the King's return 주님의 재림). Never soften or alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — **syllable count (§4)**, rhythm, and open vowels must fit the melody. A line a native can't sing comfortably has failed, even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant Korean** — it must sound like a Korean believer wrote it, not a translated English song. No calques 번역투, no English word order; use the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture; swap an image only when it is untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo in Korea (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook preserved** — see §4: **Korean prefers syllable-count and parallelism over end-rhyme — do NOT force rhyme.** Preserve the hook's rhythmic placement always.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** Every line must make complete, natural sense to a native *with any rhyme ignored.* **Rhyme is the lowest priority — and in Korean, syllable-count/parallelism matter far more than end-rhyme (§4).** Ship a clear true line; never invent a word, bend grammar, or append a meaningless noun/ending to chase a sound. Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line: delete the final word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use? Do NOT pad lines with empty fillers (그저／막／뭐 jammed in, or stacked 요/네/죠) — a held vowel can carry a mora instead.

> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** Adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to Korean culture freely — honor-shame entry points, the depth of **한 (han)**, fervent-yet-reverent worship, parallelism — but **never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity** to be more relatable. Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form (1 Cor 1:23). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect. Never import a shamanist/Buddhist/Confucian-ritual term into a Christian slot (see §2).

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

1. **No nonsense / forced filler.** Never end a line with a word/ending that is there *only* for sound. KO offenders: stacked sentence-enders **요／죠／네／라** padded just to fill syllables; filler adverbs **그저／막／뭐／참** dropped in; an empty noun like **것／거** dangling; over-use of **〜는 것** to stretch a line. **Test:** cover the last word; if it adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.
2. **Only REAL words & correct grammar.** No invented compounds, no broken conjugation. KO traps: **honorific agreement** — if the subject is God you MUST conjugate honorifically (하신다, not 한다 — see §1A); particle errors **은/는 vs 이/가**, **을/를 vs 이/가**, **에 vs 에서**; wrong connective ending (〜고/〜서/〜며 misuse); spacing/spelling (띄어쓰기/맞춤법) errors that change meaning; mismatched speech level mid-song.
3. **Correct collocations & set phrases — no calques (번역투).** Caught: literal *마음을 열다* forced unnaturally; "fall in love with God" as *사랑에 빠지다* (romantic infatuation — do NOT use of God, see §1A); over-use of passive *되어지다* (double passive, ungrammatical → use 되다); English "the fact that" calqued as *〜라는 사실*; pronoun over-use — Korean drops 나/너 constantly, so *나는 나의 마음을* is wrong. Verify each particle and idiom is what a native actually says.
4. **No English-calque or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** Caught: "on the line to heaven" reading as a phone line (전화선); "download/recharge/signal" gadget metaphors; literal English compounds and hyphen-stacks. De-tech every image into natural Korean.
5. **Loanwords/register that misread (Konglish traps).** Don't drop unneeded English/Konglish (러브 for love, 갓 for God — never! use 사랑/하나님) into reverent lines; avoid youth slang (대박, 헐, ㄹㅇ) and stiff officialese (〜에 있어서, 〜에 관하여) in worship. Konglish carries a casual/pop register — keep it out of worship.
6. **HONORIFIC AGREEMENT (Korean-critical — see §1A).** When God is the subject, the verb MUST take the honorific infix **시 (-si-)** and a deferential ending (하시다, 오시다, 사랑하시다, 다스리시다). Omitting it (하다, 온다, 사랑한다 of God) is **shockingly rude** and instantly marks the line as wrong. Also use honorific nouns/particles for God: 께서 (honorific subject marker), 께 (honorific "to"), 말씀 (His word), 보혈 (His blood). This is the #1 line-level check.
7. **No grammatically stranded tails.** A dangling 〜의／〜을／〜가 with nothing to attach to is rejected; every word has a grammatical home. (체언 종결, ending on a noun, is allowed and common in lyrics — but must be intentional.)
8. **Imagery internally consistent.** Don't append a cosmic/grand image onto an intimate 3-AM-floor scene with no antecedent; match imagery already in the verse.
9. **Theology over rhyme/sound.** No word distorts doctrine. 운명 (fate) and 운/복 (luck/fortune) must stay **subordinate to God's sovereignty (하나님의 주권)** — never imply fate or luck saved you. 구원 is from Christ, not from circumstance. Beware 복 (blessing/fortune) sliding into shamanist 기복 (luck-seeking).
10. **EUPHONY/HOMOPHONE SCREEN.** Korean has resyllabification across word boundaries when sung — confirm run-together syllables don't form a vulgar/comic word (see §4).

[**DROP-THE-RHYME/FILLER TEST (one line):** delete the final word — if the remainder isn't true, natural, and a word you'd really use, the line is forced; rewrite it meaning-first. (Korean leans on syllable-count + parallelism, not end-rhyme — §4.)]

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

South Korea has one of the **largest, most vibrant Protestant Christian populations in Asia** — **Presbyterian (장로교, the largest), Methodist (감리교), Baptist (침례교), Holiness (성결교), and Pentecostal/charismatic** (the Yoido Full Gospel Church 여의도순복음교회 is among the world's largest congregations) — plus a significant **Catholic Church (천주교)** minority. Worship/CCM culture is **mature, fervent, and musically sophisticated** (대표적으로 찬양 worship). Jubilee/Inspire songs are CCM/evangelical; the KO version must **hold for the whole Korean Body of Christ** (and read naturally to the large evangelical worship audience).

**Cross-tradition safety rules (KO-specific):**
- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** Protestants own (and Catholics largely recognize): 하나님, 주님/주, 예수, 그리스도, 성령, 아버지, 구주, 은혜, 영광, 나라/왕국, 십자가, 부활, 어린양, 할렐루야, 아멘.
- **Anchor Scripture echoes to the 개역개정 (Korean Revised Version — the standard Protestant Bible** carried in almost every Korean church). When a line quotes/echoes Scripture, echo 개역개정 — e.g. John 19:30 → **"다 이루었다."** (it is finished); Rev 22:20 → **"주 예수여, 오시옵소서."** (Come, Lord Jesus — note the high-deferential 옵소서 ending). Allude to beloved worship songs/hymns (e.g. 《주 은혜임을》, 《은혜 아니면》, 《나 같은 죄인 살리신》 Amazing Grace) where it strengthens trust — allude, never quote wholesale.
- **No tradition-specific imports the source lacks.** No Marian/saint devotion (avoid the Catholic 천주교 framing for an evangelical default album), no denomination-specific formulas. Keep it Christ-centered and Bible-plain — the common ground.
- **Reverent register toward God — honorific grammar is mandatory (§1A).** Worship temperature is **fervent yet reverent** — Korean worship is passionate (통성기도 culture: loud, simultaneous, heartfelt prayer; raised hands; tears) AND deeply reverent toward God. Express fervor freely; never with crude/slang words.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** 신부／신랑 (Bride/Bridegroom) is biblical and fine; keep it pure. Never use 사랑에 빠지다 / 연애 (romantic infatuation) of God.
- **Keep the offense of the cross, remove the offense of bad form** (1 Cor 1:23). Don't dilute 죄 (sin), 보혈 (blood), 회개 (repentance), 심판 (judgment), or Christ's exclusivity — just say them in clean, dignified Korean.
- **Gender/inclusive-language & family handling:** defer to THIS community's norms — Korean church usage is plain and traditional; do not import source-culture inclusive-language debates.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP  (the relatability layer)

- **Lead with the worldview frame: HONOR–SHAME (deep, primary — 체면/face and 효 filial honor), with fear–power present (shamanist/spirit substrate) and guilt–innocence meaningful.** Same doctrine, different emotional entry:
  - **Honor–shame** — Korea is intensely honor/face-oriented. Foreground: shame 수치 lifted and replaced with God-given honor and a **new name**; the failure/outcast **restored and received**; **belonging and adoption into God's family (하나님의 자녀, "children of God")** — huge in a family/lineage culture; the Father who runs to embrace the prodigal (counter-cultural tenderness toward a wayward child — use deliberately). The ache of academic/economic pressure, loneliness, and not-being-enough lands powerfully.
  - **Fear–power** — the shamanist (무속) substrate of spirits, fate, and 기복 luck-seeking still pulses under modern Korea. Foreground **Christ's authority over every dark power**, light into darkness 어둠을 이기는 빛, no fear of spirits/curses because Jesus is Lord, freedom from the tyranny of fate. Lands strongly, especially in Pentecostal contexts.
  - **Guilt–innocence** — sin forgiven 죄 사함, debt cancelled — fully real; weave it through.
- **Taboo / danger images → safe substitutes:**
  - **Worldly "dancing/partying/club" imagery** — Korean evangelical culture is fervent but conservative about 춤/클럽 connotations; keep the JOY but render exuberant praise as **뛰며 찬양 ("leaping in praise"), 손 들고 찬양 (hands-up praise), 기뻐 뛰노네 (leaping for joy)** rather than nightclub 춤. (Korean worship *does* leap and dance before the Lord — frame it as that, not as the world's party. In the flagship hook "dancing" may stay as holy rejoicing — see §9.)
  - **"Buddy/homeboy Jesus"** — over-casual friendship reads as disrespect (honor culture). Jesus is 주님 (Lord) AND 친구 (friend, John 15:15) — keep the friendship, never chummy/banmal-slangy.
  - **Wine "flowing like a river"** — render as the **joy/abundance of the wedding feast (혼인 잔치의 기쁨)**, not drunkenness.
  - **Fire** — distinguish refining/Holy-Spirit fire (성령의 불, 정금같이 연단) — *very* resonant in Korean fervent worship — from judgment fire.
  - **Father imagery** — tender Father love is *deeply* healing where fathers are often stern/distant (Confucian patriarchy); render it warm and dignified (하늘 아버지의 사랑), fervent but not saccharine.
  - **Shamanist/Buddhist/ancestral-rite objects** — never use 부적 (talisman), 굿 (shaman ritual), 제사/차례 (ancestral rites), 부처 (Buddha), 사주/팔자 (fate-reading), 점 (fortune-telling) in a Christian image. Keep 복 as God's blessing, never 기복 luck-seeking.
- **Native ache word / sacred-longing idiom:** **한 (han)** — the deep, accumulated ache of sorrow, grief, unresolved longing and injustice that sits at the heart of the Korean soul. The gospel meets and resolves 한; use it where the song carries deep pain/longing (한이 풀리다 — the ache released/resolved). Also **사모하다** (to earnestly long for / yearn after God, Ps 42) and **그리움** (longing). **Worship temperature: fervent yet reverent** (통성기도 culture — passionate, tearful, hands raised, AND deeply honoring of God).
- **Charged vocabulary to handle with care:** **전쟁/군대/승리 (war/army/victory)** — keep strictly **spiritual** (영적 전쟁 against sin/darkness); given the divided peninsula and the Korean War's living memory, avoid any literal militarist/political-conflict framing. **왕/나라 (king/kingdom)** — fine as Christ's heavenly reign (만왕의 왕, 하나님 나라); keep it heavenly, not tied to earthly politics or North/South. Nation/homeland/unification imagery is emotionally and politically charged — keep the kingdom heavenly (천국/하나님 나라). Avoid anything that reads as political.
- **Reverence/honorific forms REQUIRED when addressing/speaking of God (MANDATORY — see §0A.6):**
  - **Honorific infix 시 (-si-) on every verb whose subject is God:** 하시다 (does), 오시다 (comes), 다스리시다 (reigns), 사랑하시다 (loves), 구원하시다 (saves), 살아 계시다 (is alive). Plain forms (하다/온다/사랑한다) of God are **shockingly rude.**
  - **Honorific particles/nouns:** subject marker **께서** (하나님께서), dative **께** (주께/하나님께), honorific nouns 말씀 (word), 보혈 (precious blood), 존전 (His presence).
  - **Deferential speech level / vocatives:** address God as **주여／주님／아버지／하나님 아버지**; high-deferential prayer endings exist (〜소서, 〜옵소서 — "오시옵소서") for solemn lines. Use the **합쇼체/하소서체** register toward God — never 반말 (casual/intimate plain speech) and never the merely-polite 해요체 dropped to plain mid-line.
  - Maintaining this honorific register consistently is the single most important Korean correctness check.
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** Korean traditional verse (시조 sijo, 가사) is built on **syllable-count and rhythmic feet (3·4 / 4·4 조), and PARALLELISM / repetition** — NOT end-rhyme. Reach for **parallel matched lines (대구), repetition/anaphora (반복) — which also suits 통성/responsive worship — and clean syllable cadence.** **Do NOT force end-rhyme (§4).**

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY ( Korean-specific )

- **Syllable count governs Korean singing.** Each Hangul block (음절) = one syllable = (roughly) one note. **Count syllables to the melody**, matching the EN syllable count to the note count. Korean is often longer than English; **compress with the contractions/short forms Koreans actually sing**: 나는→난, 너는→넌, 나의→내, 너의→네, 저는→전, 무엇을→뭘, 것은→건, 하여→해, 하시어→하셔, 되어→돼, 〜이어요→〜여요, 위하여→위해. **Never pad with filler** (그저/막/요-stacking) — a held vowel can carry the note.
- **Korean prefers syllable-count and PARALLELISM over end-rhyme — DO NOT force rhyme (LOCKED).** Traditional Korean verse (시조, 가사) organizes by **syllable count and rhythmic feet (3·4 / 4·4), repetition, and parallelism (대구)** — NOT end-rhyme. Because Korean has very regular endings (verb/particle endings repeat naturally), "rhyme" is trivial and chasing it produces filler endings stacked just to match sound (§0A.1). **Do not match the EN rhyme scheme.** Reach instead for the native devices (§1A): parallel matched lines, anaphora/repetition (also suits responsive/통성 worship), and clean syllable cadence. A natural sound-echo is fine if it happens — never bend a word for it.
- **Stress/rhythm:** Korean is relatively syllable-timed (each syllable ~even length) with phrase-level intonation — that even pulse suits melody. Place key/content words on strong beats; don't force an unnatural accent. Read every line aloud in rhythm.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes:** land big held notes on open vowels — **ㅏ(아), ㅗ(오), ㅓ(어), ㅜ(우), ㅐ(애), ㅏ(야)** (e.g. 주여 -yeo, 사랑 -rang, 영광 -gwang→glory, 할렐루야 -ya, 왕 wang). Avoid landing a long note on a heavy final consonant cluster (받침) like ㄺ/ㄶ.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot** (it lands at 0:00–0:08; keep it there); the chorus arrives on the same bar.
- **Compression toolkit (above) — never sacrifice the honorific 시/honorific particles to save syllables.** If a line is too long, recast it; do NOT drop the honorific toward God to make it fit (§0A.6, §1A). E.g. keep 다스리시네, not 다스리네.
- **Script mandatory & correct:** Suno/TTS pronounce from Hangul — correct **맞춤법 (spelling) and 띄어쓰기 (spacing)** matter (wrong spacing/spelling can change the word). Do not insert Hanja into sung text. No romanization except the coined hook.
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** Korean **resyllabifies across word boundaries when sung** (연음 — a final consonant slides onto the next syllable's vowel). Sing the line aloud at speed and confirm the run-together syllables don't form a vulgar, comic, or sacred-taboo word, and that no blurred phrase reads as something unintended.

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**KO (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, honorific intact, NO forced rhyme, frame-appropriate; 하나님/예수님):**
> Jubiluyah！Jubiluyah！/ 예수님 한 분으로, 우린 지금 뛰노네！/ Jubiluyah！Jubiluyah！/ 왕이 다시 오시네 — 반드시, 반드시！

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; **예수님** consistent honorific name; "Jesus is the reason we are dancing now" → **예수님 한 분으로, 우린 지금 뛰노네** ("because of Jesus alone, we now leap for joy") — keeps the JOY of "dancing" but renders it as **뛰노다** (leap/dance for joy), which is exuberant *worship* (뛰며 찬양), not worldly 춤 (§1A); **우린** = contraction of 우리는 to fit the syllable count (§4); "the King is coming back" → **왕이 다시 오시네** uses **오시네** — the **honorific 시 (-si-) is mandatory** because God is the subject (§1A, §0A.6); 다시 ("again") = His return; **반드시, 반드시** ("surely, surely") replaces the vague "somehow, somehow" with the **certainty of the Second Coming** (better theology), preserving the double-beat. **No end-rhyme was forced** — the lines work by **parallel cadence and the repeated 〜네 worship-ending** (a natural Korean device, not a stretched rhyme; §4). Syllable counts match the melody. Fervent-yet-reverent; zero offense across Presbyterian/Methodist/Pentecostal.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A & §4):** you're tempted to drop the honorific or pad to "rhyme."
- ❌ Dropped honorific + fate + filler: *"왕이 다시 온다, 운명처럼, 그저！"* — three failures: (1) **온다 is plain-form** — saying God "comes" without 시 is **shockingly rude** (§0A.6, §1A) → must be 오시네/오신다; (2) **운명 (fate)** steals God's sovereignty (§0A.9, §2 — His return is His promise, not fate); (3) trailing **그저** is pure filler to fill syllables (§0A.1).
- ✅ Meaning-first, honorific intact: *"왕이 다시 오시네 — 반드시, 반드시！"* — honorific 오시네, sound doctrine (His certain return), parallel cadence, fervent-reverent, no forced rhyme.
- ✅ Also fine (parallelism/anaphora): *"오시네, 우리 왕 오시네 — 영광 중에 오시네!"* — repeats 오시네 (anaphora, §1A device), honorific throughout, vivid and true. **A reverent, parallel, unrhymed line is exactly right in Korean.**

## 10. ADDICTIVENESS / RE-LISTENABILITY PASS (after the album is produced in Korean)

2. **Craft pass** — hook clarity, groove, vocal delivery, mix, arrangement.
3. **Replay-triggers pass** — what makes you hit repeat? (earworm hook, a turnaround, a payoff moment, a loop point).

Then **amplify** (tuning the ENGLISH `Style` block §6 and the Suno re-gen prompt ONLY — never doctrine or the locked translation):
- **Hook & delivery** — make the "Jubiluyah" hook front-loaded, sticky, well-placed; tighten the vocal performance note (Korean worship loves a soaring, fervent lead + big group/choir lift).
- **Groove & pulse** — target a head-nod pocket (~100–128 BPM where the style allows); steady, anthemic, sing-along feel suited to the fervent register.
- **Texture & ear-candy** — tasteful fills, counter-melodies, and **culturally resonant instrumentation from §1A** (gayageum/daegeum/janggu/haegeum accents, or polished K-CCM worship-band textures) for distinctiveness; tasteful, not gimmicky.

- **Loop point & album flow** — strong intro/outro so the track loops and the album sequences well.
- **Emotional pull & relatability** — lean on §1A's **honor-shame/fear-power frame** and the **한 (han)** ache (and 사모함/그리움) so the feeling lands deeply for a Korean listener.
