## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the translator's *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to meaning & the gospel** — the theological content must survive intact (the cross, the blood, the resurrection, the name of Jesus, repentance, the King's return). Never alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, open vowels fit the melody. Hindi words can be long and consonant-heavy; a line a native can't sing comfortably has failed even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant Hindi** — it must sound like a native Hindi believer wrote it, not like a translated English song. No calques, no English word order; use Hindi's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture (bathroom floor, cage door, wedding feast). Swap an image only when untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook** — match the rhyme scheme ONLY with real, sensible words; always keep the hook's rhythmic placement. Hindi/Urdu film-song tradition loves *tukbandī* (rhyme) — but never let it override sense.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**. Break a rhyme before you break the meaning or the meter.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** Every single line must make complete, natural sense to a native Hindi speaker *on its own, with the rhyme ignored.* **Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement.** Ship a clear unrhymed or near-rhymed TRUE line over a forced one. Never invent a word, bend grammar (especially gender agreement — see §0A), or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. If no honest rhyme fits, **DROP IT** — use blank verse, assonance, or near-rhyme. Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line: delete the final rhyme word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?

> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** Adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to the culture freely — bhajan/qawwali warmth, devotional intimacy, honor-and-belonging language — **but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity to be more relatable.** Above all, **never import a Hindu deity name, avatar framing, or bhakti object** (Ram, Krishna, Shiva, Bhagwan-as-deity, *ishvara* as a Hindu Absolute, *avatār*, *darshan* of an idol) to fill a Christian slot. Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form (1 Cor 1:23). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

1. **No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler words.** Hindi/Urdu song writing leans hard on *tukbandī*; do not jam a rhyme-only noun at the line end. Real offenders to avoid: ending on a stray **रे / री / ओ रे** ("rey/ree" vocative particles) used only as padding; throwaway **यार** ("buddy" — chummy, wrong register for God); filler abstracts like **समां** (atmosphere/ambience, film-song cliché) or **नज़ारा** (a "view/scene") dropped in just to rhyme with *सहारा/किनारा*; **दिलदार/जानम/सनम** (romantic-film endearments) jammed in for sound. **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.
2. **Only REAL words & correct verb/gender forms.** Hindi verbs, adjectives, and participles **inflect for gender** — the single most common error. The narrator's gender (the singer) and the gender of every subject/object must agree. Caught patterns: a male singer using **मैं आई / मैं गई / मैं डरी** (feminine "I came/went/was afraid") when the VOCAL GENDER is male → **मैं आया / गया / डरा**; feminine adjective on a masculine noun (**टूटा दिल** masc. vs **टूटी आशा** fem.). **FIX NARRATOR GENDER FIRST**: read the VOCAL GENDER footer, then make every first-person verb, participle (*-ता/-ती, -या/-यी, रहा/रही*), and predicate adjective agree. God is addressed/described as masculine.
3. **Correct postpositions & set phrases — no calques.** Hindi uses **postpositions** (ने, को, से, में, पर, का/के/की), not prepositions; English word order calques read as broken. Caught: English "thank you to God" calqued as *परमेश्वर **को** धन्यवाद देना* is fine, but *आसमान **पर** जाना* for "go to heaven" should be *स्वर्ग **में/को*** depending on sense; "the reason that…" calqued as *कारण **कि*** → use **इसलिए / जिस वजह से**. Verify the *ने* ergative on perfective transitive verbs (**मैंने देखा**, not *मैं देखा*).
4. **No English-calque or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** Don't render "on the line to heaven" with a phone-line image (*लाइन*); don't calque "download grace," "signal," "connect." Use native images: *स्वर्ग की ओर पुकार* ("a cry toward heaven"). De-hyphenate English compound stacks into a natural Hindi clause.
5. **Loanwords/register that misread in Hindi.** Beware over-Sanskritized (शुद्ध हिन्दी) register that sounds like a government bulletin, OR over-Persianized/Urdu register that may carry mosque connotations in a worship line — choose the warm **Hindustani middle register** ordinary believers speak. Traps: **प्रेम** (refined love) vs **प्यार** (warm everyday love) vs **इश्क़** (romantic/Sufi passion — avoid for God except as a deliberate qawwali-warmth cue, never erotic); **मंदिर** ("temple," Hindu connotation) for church → use **गिरजाघर / कलीसिया (the Church/body)**; **पूजा/आरती** (Hindu ritual worship) → use **आराधना / स्तुति / उपासना**; **प्रसाद** (Hindu temple offering) → avoid. **भजन** as a *musical form* is fine (and resonant), but it carries Hindu-devotional default — frame as worship song, content Christ-centered.
6. **Line-end adjectives/participles agree in gender & number.** (See #2.) A rhyme-chosen adjective often breaks agreement — fix the agreement, then fix the rhyme on the OTHER line.
7. **No grammatically stranded tails.** Don't strand an *-का/के/की* genitive or a postposition with no head noun. Every word needs a grammatical home.
8. **Imagery internally consistent.** Don't append an image that contradicts the scene (cold floor + a "blaze") or introduce an object with no antecedent.
9. **Theology over rhyme.** Never let a rhyme word distort doctrine: don't let *किस्मत/नसीब* ("fate/luck/destiny," fatalism-laden) become the agent of salvation — keep luck/chance subordinate to God's sovereignty (**परमेश्वर की इच्छा**, His will). Don't let *कर्म* ("karma," reincarnation-laden) slip in for "deeds/works." Grace comes from Christ, not from *भाग्य*.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** silently delete the final rhyming word. Does the rest still say something true and clear, and is that final word one you'd actually use here? If no — rewrite meaning-first, even unrhymed.

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

Hindi is spoken across the North-Indian "Hindi belt" in an overwhelmingly **Hindu-majority** society, with significant **Muslim** communities and a small, often **scrutinized Christian minority** (high sensitivity around conversion; "anti-conversion" laws in several states). Indian Christians are mostly **Catholic, mainline Protestant (CNI — Church of North India), Pentecostal/charismatic, and independent/evangelical** fellowships. The Hindi version must **hold for the whole Hindi-speaking Body of Christ** and avoid handing ammunition to anyone watching for "foreign religion" or syncretism.

- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** all traditions own: *परमेश्वर, प्रभु, यीशु मसीह, परमेश्वर का पुत्र, पवित्र आत्मा, पिता, उद्धारकर्ता, अनुग्रह, महिमा, राज्य, क्रूस, पुनरुत्थान, मेम्ना, हल्लिलूय्याह, आमीन* — drawn from the standard Hindi Bible (see below).
- **Anchor Scripture echoes** to the **पवित्र बाइबल** — the **Hindi Old Version (हिन्दी OV / पुरानी हिन्दी बाइबल)** for classic phrasing, and the **Easy-to-Read / सरल हिन्दी** versions for plain modern lines. Canonical echoes: John 19:30 "It is finished" → **"पूरा हुआ"** ; Rev 22:20 "Come, Lord Jesus" → **"हे प्रभु यीशु, आ"**. Allude to native Christian hymnody (Hindi bhajan-style worship) where it strengthens trust.
- **No tradition-specific imports** the source lacks: no Marian/saint devotion, no one-church liturgical formula, no glossolalia naming. Equally, **no Hindu ritual vocabulary** (आरती, पूजा, प्रसाद, मंत्र, ॐ) and **no Hindu deity names**. Christ-centered, Bible-plain — that is the common ground and the safety line.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Address God respectfully (use the honorific/plural verb forms — see §1A); capitalize divine pronouns in print where it aids reverence. No vulgar/street-slang (*अबे, साला, यार* as address to God) anywhere.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** Bride/Bridegroom (*दुल्हन / दूल्हा*) is biblical and fine; keep it pure — never the romantic-film *सनम/जानम* register.
- **Gender/inclusive-language & family handling:** defer to THIS community's norms (generally traditional family language); do not import source-culture debates.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP  (the relatability layer)

- **Lead with the worldview frame: HONOR–SHAME blended with FEAR–POWER.** Hindu-majority culture runs on *izzat/maryādā* (honor, family face, shame/*sharm*) AND on a vivid sense of unseen spiritual powers (fate, spirits, ancestral debt, ritual fear). Foreground these biblical images (same doctrine, different emotional entry):
  - *Honor–shame:* the outcast/defiled **made clean and given a new name and family**; the Father **running to embrace the returning child** (Luke 15); **adoption and belonging** (*अपनाया गया, परिवार में लिया गया*); shame lifted, head raised, face restored before the community.
  - *Fear–power:* **Christ's authority over every dark power, every spirit, every curse, every fate** (*हर शक्ति पर प्रभुता*); **light swallowing darkness**; freedom from the dread of *भाग्य/किस्मत* because the living God reigns. This lands hard where fear of the unseen is real.
- **Taboo / danger images → safe native substitutes:**
  - **"Dancing before the Lord"** — dance in Hindu devotion is tied to deity worship (e.g., temple dance); render joy carefully as **जय-जयकार / नाचो-गाओ in celebration**, framed as exuberant praise, not ritual. (For the flagship hook, see §9.)
  - **Buddy/"homeboy" Jesus** — chummy *यार/दोस्त* register for God is offensive to the honor frame → use *प्रभु, साथी* with reverent verbs.
  - **Wine "flowing like a river"** — alcohol is socially fraught (and taboo for many) → keep the biblical wedding-feast/new-wine image but lean on **the feast, the joy, the overflowing cup** rather than drunkenness.
  - **Fire** — redemptive/refining fire is fine (*शुद्ध करने वाली आग*), but distinguish clearly from cremation-fire connotations; pair with light/gold-refining, not the pyre.
  - **Father imagery** — tender and powerful; embrace it (the Father who runs) — but be aware some carry painful father experiences; keep it the *good* Father.
  - **Cow / beef / leather** imagery — avoid entirely (sacred-cow sensitivity).
- **Native "ache" word / sacred-longing idiom:** **तड़प (taḍap)** — the deep yearning/pining of the soul; also **विरह (virah)** — the ache of separation from the beloved (classical bhakti longing, here redirected to longing for God) and **प्यास (pyās)** — thirst of the soul (Ps 42). Use these for the soul's cry. **Worship temperature: WARM-DEVOTIONAL** — intimate, heartfelt, tearful-joyful (bhajan/qawwali emotional resonance).
- **Charged vocabulary to handle with care:** *किस्मत/नसीब/भाग्य* (fate — subordinate to God, never the savior); *कर्म* (karma — avoid for "works"); *मोक्ष* (mokṣa — Hindu "release from rebirth," NOT Christian salvation → use **उद्धार / मुक्ति-in-Christ** and define it as rescue from sin, not the rebirth cycle); *आत्मा* (see §2 — soul/self vs Spirit baggage); avoid nationalist/communal flags (no "every Hindu/Muslim" othering); keep the gospel offered, never coercive (anti-conversion-law sensitivity).
- **Reverence/honorific forms required when addressing God:** Hindi reverence is **grammatical**. Use the **honorific second person आप** (not तू/तुम) and its **plural-honorific verb agreement** for God in formal worship: *आप हैं, आपने किया, आप आएँ*. (Note: some intimate bhakti/Bible traditions use **तू** for God as tender closeness — like the KJV "thou"; this is acceptable as a deliberate intimacy register, but **be consistent album-wide** and never let तू slide into the disrespectful/chummy सड़क register.) Default to **आप** for breadth and safety unless an album is deliberately set in the intimate तू register.
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** *radīf* and *qāfiyā* (repeated refrain-word + rhyme, ghazal/qawwali tradition) for hypnotic repetition; *antarā/sthāyī* verse-refrain structure of bhajan; call-and-response praise. Reach for these where they fit the melody — they make the song feel *Indian* and devotional without changing doctrine.

## 3. COINED / BRAND WORDS, PROPER NOUNS & LOANWORDS

- **Coined brand hooks stay verbatim.** "Jubilujah"/"Jubiluyah" (Jubilee + Hallelujah) is the signature earworm and name — **keep it exactly**, including the stripped-syllable chant *"Ju-bi-loo-yah"* and the spelling-chant **J-U-B-I-L-U-J-A-H** (same Latin letters; do not transliterate the chant). Rendered in Devanagari for the AI vocal it reads **जुबिलूय्याह** — its echo of **हल्लिलूय्याह** is a feature. Same policy for any future coined hook.
- **Greek theological terms** localized in spelling where standard: *kairos* → **कैरॉस**, *chronos* → **क्रोनॉस** ("Kairos Hour" → **"कैरॉस की घड़ी"**).
- **Language & place names in Hindi forms**, keep the multilingual sweep: Swahili → **स्वाहिली**, Mandarin → **मंदारिन / चीनी (भाषा)** (use चीनी for "Chinese," not a fruit-misread risk), Spanish → **स्पेनी**, Hindi → **हिन्दी**, Portuguese → **पुर्तगाली**, Arabic → **अरबी**, Hebrew → **इब्रानी**.
- **Modern loanwords** only where natives actually speak that way (Hinglish is real in song: *दिल, time, party* occur naturally) — but prefer a clean Hindi word in reverent lines; a brand title may keep an English coined word.

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY (Hindi-specific)

- **Match syllable count; Hindi runs long and consonant-clustered.** Compress with the natural elisions singers use: the **schwa-deletion** Hindi already does (*मन* = "man" not "ma-na"; *प्रभु* = "prabh") — exploit it to hit beats. Use contractions/sandhi: *है ना → हैना*, *क्या है → क्या-है*, *मैं हूँ → मैं हूँ* (nasal can ride a note). Don't pad with फिलर particles (रे/जी/ना) to fill bars.
- **Respect Hindi rhythm (mātrā / weight-based).** Hindi verse is traditionally **mora-timed** (laghu/guru — light/heavy syllables), not English stress-timed. Place **guru (long/heavy) syllables on strong beats** and held notes. Read every line aloud in the melody's rhythm; a film-song/bhajan sense of *laya* (groove) matters more than English-style stress.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes.** End big held notes on **आ, ओ, ए, ई, ऊ** (e.g., *महिमा, आनंद, यीशु, राजा, आ*) — the hook "Jubiluyah" ends open (-yah/आ); exploit it. Avoid landing a long note on a hard cluster.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot;** the chorus arrives on the same bar.
- **Rhyme stance for Hindi: rhyme is welcome but OPTIONAL and last.** Hindi/Urdu song (*tukbandī*, qawwali) rhymes richly and a good rhyme adds devotional pleasure — BUT the moment a rhyme forces awkward syntax, an invented word, a wrong gender form, or a distorted meaning, **abandon it** (near-rhyme/assonance/blank). Prefer recasting the OTHER line of the couplet. **A true unrhymed line beats a forced rhymed one.**
- **Reach for the native poetic device (§1A)** — *radīf* (repeated refrain word) + *qāfiyā* (rhyme) for hypnotic worship; bhajan refrain-return; call-and-response.
- **Devanagari script is mandatory in all sung text and titles** — Suno/TTS pronounce from the text. Write full **mātrā, nukta (़), anusvāra (ं), chandrabindu (ँ), and halant/virāma (्)**: *पवित्र* (not पवितर), *आँसू* (chandrabindu nasal), *ज़, फ़, क़* with nukta where the word needs it (e.g., *ज़िंदगी, फ़रिश्ता*). A missing mātrā mis-sings the word.
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud slurred (as an AI vocal would) and check that blurred syllables don't form a vulgar Hindi word or an unintended Hindu-ritual term. (Hindi has easy near-collisions across the स/श/ष and क/क़ sets — verify.)

## 5. IDIOMS, IMAGERY & CULTURAL FIT

- **Translate the sense, not the words → native idioms.** "permission slip" → *किसी की इजाज़त नहीं चाहिए* ("you don't need anyone's permission"); "Sunday morning bell" → *रविवार की घंटी* (church bells exist, but church culture is small — a **bhajan/aarti-time of gathering** image may resonate more; choose per song); "did the flip" → *खुशी लौट आई / दिल पलट गया*.
- **Keep concrete sensory anchors** (bathroom floor at 3 AM, cage door, wedding feast, wine like a river) — render them vividly in Hindi (*रात के तीन बजे ठंडे फ़र्श पर*). Localize culture-bound American daily-life anchors (carpool, diner, prom, credit-card debt) to emotionally equivalent native scenes (*स्कूल का रास्ता, घर की रसोई, उधार का बोझ*) — but **do NOT localize away the global/heaven imagery** (every-tribe, throne, Lamb, wedding feast — those stay).

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**Hindi (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, frame-appropriate):**
> जुबिलूय्याह! जुबिलूय्याह! / यीशु ही वजह है, आज हम झूम रहे! / जुबिलूय्याह! जुबिलूय्याह! / राजा फिर आएँगे — देखना, देखना!

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat (Devanagari for the AI vocal; echoes हल्लिलूय्याह); *यीशु* consistent; **"dancing"** — because temple/ritual dance is theologically loaded in a Hindu context (§1A), it's rendered as **झूम रहे** ("swaying/reveling in joy") — celebratory exuberance, not ritual dance — *and this substitution is intentional* (frame-safe joy). "The King is coming back" → **राजा फिर आएँगे** (honorific plural आएँगे = reverent); *देखना, देखना* ("just watch / surely") carries the "somehow, somehow" certainty-of-hope with a natural near-rhyme on *रहे / आएँगे*. Meaning intact; zero offense across traditions; warm-devotional temperature.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A):**
- ❌ **Forced / wrong-frame:** *जुबिलूय्याह! किस्मत ने हमें नाच दिया, ओ यार!* — "Fate made us dance, O buddy!": **किस्मत** (fate) as the agent is bad theology, **यार** ("buddy") is chummy-irreverent toward the moment, and *नाच* unframed reads as ritual dance — three §0A violations stacked for a cheap rhyme.
- ✅ **Meaning-first fix:** *जुबिलूय्याह! यीशु ने जीवन दिया, उठो गाओ!* — "Jesus gave life — rise, sing!": real words, sound doctrine (Jesus, not fate, is the agent), reverent, exuberant praise (*उठो गाओ*) instead of loaded "dance," and it still lands the joy on open vowels. **A true, frame-safe line beats a forced, syncretism-tinged one.**

## 10. ADDICTIVENESS / RE-LISTENABILITY PASS (after the album is produced in Hindi)

After the Hindi album is produced, run three listen passes and tune for replay. This tunes the **English `Style` block (§6)** and re-gen prompts ONLY — never doctrine, never the locked translation.

- **Pass 1 — feeling:** Does the chorus move you on first listen? Does the warm-devotional temperature (§1A) land — does it ache and rejoice like a bhajan/qawwali a believer would replay?
- **Pass 2 — craft:** hook strength & vocal delivery; **groove & pulse** (head-nod pocket where the style allows, ~100–128 BPM for the upbeat tracks; slower bhajan tempos for the tender ones — keep the *laya*); texture & ear-candy (tabla/dholak fills, harmonium swell, bansuri hook, qawwali taali claps, sarangi pad); dynamic arc & payoff; loop point & album flow.
- **Pass 3 — replay triggers:** the *radīf*/refrain repetition (§1A), the emotional pull & relatability via the honor-shame/fear-power frame and the ache word (तड़प/विरह/प्यास), the spelling-chant earworm.

- **Re-rate each track + the album BEFORE/AFTER** across **hook strength, groove, replay value, dynamic payoff, emotional pull, loop-ability**; deliver a summary of changes, why each boosts re-listening, and the measurable effect.
