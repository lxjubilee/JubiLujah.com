## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the translator's *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to meaning & the gospel** — theology must survive intact (the cross, the resurrection, the name of Jesus, repentance, the King's return); never alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, and open vowels fit the original melody. German words are LONG; a line a native can't sing comfortably has failed, even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant German** — sounds like a native German believer wrote it; no calques, no English word order (the verb-second / verb-final rules are non-negotiable); uses the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture; swap an image only if untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook** — match the rhyme scheme ONLY with real, sensible words; always keep the hook's rhythmic placement.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**. Break a rhyme before you break the meaning or the meter.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** every line must make complete, natural sense to a native German speaker *with the rhyme ignored.* **Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement.** Ship a clear unrhymed/near-rhymed true line over a forced one. Never invent a word, bend grammar (no fake compounds, no broken cases), or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. If no honest rhyme fits, **DROP IT.** Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line (delete the final rhyme word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?).
>
> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to the culture freely — but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity to be more relatable. Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form (1 Cor 1:23). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

These are the exact failure modes that make a German believer instantly hear "translated machine-English." Before finalizing any line, check it against this list.

**1. No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler words.** Never end a line with a word that exists *only* to rhyme. Real German offenders to watch: stranded **"fürwahr"**, **"sodann"**, **"allzeit"**, **"hienieden"** (archaic "down here on earth") and **"droben"** dropped in just for a rhyme; padding particles **"ja", "doch", "wohl", "halt", "eben"** jammed at line-end to fill a syllable; **"o Herr"** / **"fürwahr"** as throwaway rhyme tags; nonsense diminutives (**"Herzelein", "Sünderlein"**) used to soften a stressed line into a rhyme. **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.

**2. Only REAL words & correct forms — no invented compounds.** German lets you glue nouns together, which tempts fake words. Never coin a non-lexical compound to hit a meter (e.g. **"Gnadenstrom-Licht"**, **"Kreuzes-Freudenton"** stacked for a rhyme are not words a hymn-writer uses). Keep verb conjugation and case correct: **subjunctive I/II** must be right in prayer/wish lines (*"Komm, Herr Jesus"* imperative — not *"Du kämest"*); separable verbs must split or stay together correctly (*"Er steht auf"* ↔ *"…dass er aufersteht"*; never *"auf-er-steht"* mid-line for rhythm). Strong verbs keep their vowel change (*"er gab", "er kam", "er sang"* — never regularized *"er gebte"*).

**3. Correct prepositions, cases & set phrases — no calques.** German prepositions govern cases and do NOT map 1:1 to English. Caught-style traps: *"warten **für** dich"* (English "wait for") → **"auf dich warten"**; *"abhängig **auf**"* → **"abhängig **von**"**; *"voll **von** Gnade"* is OK but *"erfüllt **mit** Gnade"* is more idiomatic → prefer **"voller Gnade" / "Gnade erfüllt"**; *"denken **über** dich"* (think about) → **"an dich denken"**; the calque *"Es macht keinen Sinn"* is now common but *"Es ergibt Sinn"* reads more native in a reverent line.

**4. No English-calque word order or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** German is **verb-second** in main clauses and **verb-final** in subordinate clauses — never copy English SVO into a subclause (*"…weil Jesus **liebt mich**"* ❌ → *"…weil Jesus mich **liebt**"* ✅). Don't calque *"eine Verbindung **machen**"* (make a connection) → **"Verbindung aufnehmen"**; don't render "reach out" as *"erreiche aus"* (non-word) → **"ich strecke mich aus" / "ich rufe dich"**; avoid hyphen-stacked English-style compounds (*"3-Uhr-nachts-Boden"*) → de-hyphenate to a clause (*"auf dem kalten Boden um drei"*).

**5. Loanwords/register that misread in German.** *"Beamer"* in German = video projector, NOT a BMW — avoid in any line. *"Handy"* = mobile phone (false friend) — never means "useful." English **"Gift"** looks like German **Gift** = *poison* — never let the eye trip there. Don't drop Denglisch ad-words (*"chillen", "Easy", "Vibe", "Hype"*) or street slang (*"krass", "geil", "Alter"*) into a reverent line — *"geil"* especially is vulgar in origin and unusable in worship. Keep register heightened, not Denglisch-casual.

**6. Adjective endings must agree** (gender/number/case + strong/weak/mixed declension). A rhyme-chosen adjective that breaks its ending is instantly wrong: *"ein heilig**er** Gott"* (masc. nom.) but *"dem heilig**en** Gott"* (dat.); *"deine groß**e** Gnade"* not *"deine groß Gnade."* Fix the rhyme partner, not the grammar.

**7. No grammatically stranded tails.** Don't strand a genitive or a dangling participle with no head (*"…der Stimme, des Liedes"* attached to nothing). German tolerates long sentences, but every case-marked word must have a grammatical home in the line or the next.

**8. Imagery internally consistent.** Don't append an image that fights the scene (cold floor + a sudden *"Flamme/Glut"* for rhyme) or introduce an object with no antecedent. Match the picture already established (the dawn = *die Morgenröte*, not a random *Blume*).

**9. Theology over rhyme.** No rhyme word may distort doctrine. **"Glück"** = luck/happiness/fortune — keep luck/chance subordinate to God's sovereignty; do not let *"Glück"* imply salvation is fortune (prefer **"Segen", "Gnade", "Heil"**). **"Schicksal"** (fate) must never replace God's providence. Grace comes from Christ, never from circumstance.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** silently delete the final rhyme word. Does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use? If no — rewrite meaning-first, even with no rhyme.

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

The German-speaking world (Germany, Austria, German-speaking Switzerland) is historically split between **Evangelisch (Lutheran/Reformed, organized in the EKD)** and **Roman Catholic** (roughly co-equal nationally; Catholic-majority in Bavaria, Austria, much of the south; Protestant-majority in the north and east), plus a smaller but vital **Freikirchen / evangelikal** minority (Baptisten, Freie evangelische Gemeinden / FeG, Pfingstler/charismatisch, Brüdergemeinden, EFG). Many Germans are also secular/unchurched. Jubilee/Inspire songs are CCM/evangelical in origin, but the German version must **hold for the whole German-speaking Body of Christ.**

- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** that all traditions own: *Gott, der Herr, Jesus, Christus, der Vater, der Heilige Geist, Gnade, Herrlichkeit, das Reich Gottes, das Kreuz, die Auferstehung, das Lamm, Halleluja, Amen, Erlösung, Heil.* Anchor Scripture echoes to the **Luther 2017** translation (the broadest cultural touchstone; *Schlachter 2000* is the preferred literal Bible in many Freikirchen) — e.g. John 19:30 → **"Es ist vollbracht"**; Rev 22:20 → **"Komm, Herr Jesus"**.
- **No tradition-specific imports the source lacks.** No Marian devotion (*Ave Maria*, *Mutter Gottes*), no saint/relic veneration, no Mass-specific liturgical formulas, no charismatic glossolalia naming ("in Zungen"). Stay Christ-centered and Bible-plain — that is the common ground.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Address God respectfully; capitalize divine pronouns where it aids reverence (*Du, Dein, Dich, Er, Sein, Ihm* when referring to God — standard in German Christian print). Use **"Du"** for God (the intimate/believing address used in German prayer and hymnody — *not* the formal "Sie"). No vulgar/street-slang anywhere (*geil, krass, Alter* are out).
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** Bride/Bridegroom (*die Braut / der Bräutigam*) is biblical and fine; keep it pure — never render it as romantic-pop *Liebeslied* longing.
- **Gender/inclusive-language & family handling:** German church culture has live debates over *gendern* (e.g. *"Geschwister"* for "brothers and sisters"). **Defer to THIS community's norms** — choose the broadest, most natural Scripture-plain form; do not import the source culture's gender debates, and do not force experimental gender-star spellings (*"Christ\*innen"*) into sung lyrics.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP (the relatability layer)

- **Lead with the culture's worldview frame: guilt–innocence.** German Protestant heritage (Luther) is the deep grammar here — *Schuld* (guilt/debt), *Rechtfertigung* (justification), *Gnade allein* (grace alone), *Vergebung* (forgiveness). **Foreground these images:** the law/conscience that accuses → acquittal; **debt cancelled** (*Schuld bezahlt / Schuldschein zerrissen*); the **verdict reversed**, the sinner declared righteous (*gerecht gesprochen*); freedom from the *bad conscience* (*ein reines Gewissen*); peace with God. The cross as the place where guilt is settled lands hardest. (Honor–shame and fear–power images still work, but make the guilt–innocence entry primary.)
- **Taboo/danger images → safe native substitutes:**
  - **"Buddy/homeboy Jesus"** / over-casual *"Hey Jesus, mein Kumpel"* → keep warmth but reverent: *"Jesus, mein Freund"* / *"mein Heiland"* — never chummy slang.
  - **Triumphalist warfare / "conquering nations"** reads badly given 20th-century German history — handle militaristic imagery carefully; prefer *Sieg über Sünde und Tod* (victory over sin and death) and inner/cosmic victory over nationalistic conquest language. **Avoid "Marsch", "Heer", flag-and-nation conquest rhetoric.**
  - **"Blood and soil" / homeland-nation imagery** is historically toxic in German — never use *Blut und Boden*, *Vaterland*, *Heimaterde* in a redemptive sense. Heaven is *die Heimat* in a spiritual sense only, handled gently.
  - **"Dancing before the Lord"** is fine and biblical but reads less natively in restrained German worship than in the EN source — *"tanzen"* is acceptable; *"jubeln, jauchzen, frohlocken"* (rejoice/shout for joy) often land more naturally for the same energy.
  - **King/Kingdom** is fully natural (*König / Reich Gottes*) — Germany's republicanism doesn't make biblical kingship awkward; it's heard as theological, not political.
- **Native ache word / sacred-longing idiom: "Sehnsucht"** — the deep, almost holy longing for the eternal/transcendent (Lewis borrowed exactly this German word). Reach for *Sehnsucht* where the EN expresses aching for God, home, or heaven. Related: *Geborgenheit* (the warm safety of being held/sheltered) for "safe in His arms."
- **Worship temperature: restrained-to-warm.** Default German (esp. EKD) worship is **reserved, dignified, text-centered** — understatement and a true, quiet line outperform American-CCM hyperbole. Freikirchen/charismatic settings run warmer and more expressive. Aim restrained-but-sincere; let warmth build rather than open at full volume. Avoid gushing superlatives that ring hollow (*"der allerbeste, größte, awesome Gott"*).
- **Charged vocabulary to handle with care:** *Vaterland / Heimat / Blut und Boden* (nationalism — avoid); *Führer* (never as a leadership word — it carries one meaning; use *"der dich leitet/führt"* as a verb, never the noun for Christ); militaristic *Heer/Marsch/Sieg-Heil-adjacent* phrasing; *Volk* in a nationalistic sense.
- **Reverence/honorific forms required when addressing God:** use **"Du"** (intimate believing address), with capitalized *Du/Dein/Dir/Dich*. Title address: **"Herr"** / vocative **"o Herr", "Herr Jesus", "Vater"**. Never formal "Sie" to God; never slangy.
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** German hymnody's strength is **strong metrical regularity and end-rhyme** (the *Choral* tradition — think *"Lobe den Herren," "Großer Gott, wir loben dich," "Stille Nacht"*), plus alliteration (*Stabreim*, the old Germanic device: *"Gnade und Güte," "Treue und Trost"*). Reach for clean trochaic/iambic feet and honest end-rhyme — German *can* carry full rhyme well, so use it where it's honest, and lean on alliteration for extra binding.

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY (German-specific)

- **Match syllable count; German runs LONG.** Compress with the elisions German singers actually use: drop schwa-**e** endings (*"Freud'" for Freude, "Seel'" for Seele, "hab'" for habe, "geh'n" for gehen, "Lieb'" for Liebe*); contractions *"ich hab's," "du bist's," "auf's," "in's," "an's," "fürs," "zum, zur, beim, vom, ans, im."* Prefer short native synonyms (*Heil/Lob/Ruhm* over a four-syllable word) instead of padding. **Never** invent a compound just to fill a bar.
- **Respect German stress.** German is stress-timed; native words usually stress the **first/root syllable** (*HEI-land, GNA-de, FREU-de, KÖ-nig, HERR-lich-keit*). Put the stressed root syllable on the strong beat. Loan/Greek words shift (*Halle-LU-ja, Kai-ROS*). Read every line aloud in rhythm; do not force an unnatural accent.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes.** Land big held notes on open vowels — *-a, -o, -e, -u* (*Gnade, Halleluja, König, Ruh', Freud'*). Avoid landing a long note on a final consonant cluster or a closed -*cht/-mpf/-rzt*. The hook "Jubiluyah" ends open (-ah) — exploit it.
- **The "ch" and "r" caveat for AI vocals:** the German **"ch"** (ich-Laut / ach-Laut) and uvular **"r"** can sound harsh or be mis-rendered by Suno/TTS — prefer vowel-final or open-syllable line-ends on sustained notes, and don't stack consonant clusters (*"durchschritt'st," "Herbstmonds"*) on fast runs.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot** (lands at 0:00–0:08; keep it there). The chorus arrives by the same bar.
- **Rhyme stance for German: USE end-rhyme (German rhymes richly), but never force it.** The German *Choral* tradition expects clean rhyme — so aim to preserve AABB/ABAB **with words that genuinely belong.** The moment a rhyme demands an invented compound, an archaic filler (*fürwahr, hienieden*), a broken case, or distorted meaning — **abandon it**: near-rhyme, assonance, or recast the OTHER line of the couplet. A true unrhymed line beats a false rhymed one.
- **Reach for the native poetic device (§1A):** strong regular meter + honest end-rhyme + occasional **Stabreim** (alliteration: *"Gnade und Güte," "Heiland und Hort"*).
- **Diacritics/umlauts mandatory** (ä, ö, ü, ß). Suno/TTS pronounce from the text: *"schon"* (already) vs *"schön"* (beautiful), *"Gott"* vs *"Götter,"* *"fur"* vs *"für,"* *"Buße"* vs *"Busse."* Always write full umlauts and **ß** (not "ss") where standard. **ß rule:** *Buße, Größe, Fuß, weiß, heißt* keep ß; do not "ss"-flatten in sung text (Swiss orthography drops ß, but use ß for the broad German/Austrian market unless producing specifically for Switzerland).
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud (slurred, as an AI vocal would) and check blurred syllables don't form an unintended word. Watch for the eye-trap *Gift* (poison), and for run-together syllables producing *"Sau-/Arsch-/Scheiß-"* fragments across word boundaries. Re-voice if so.

## 5. IDIOMS, IMAGERY & CULTURAL FIT

- **Translate the sense, not the words → native idioms.** "permission slip" → *"du brauchst keine Erlaubnis"*; "Sunday morning bell" → *"die Glocke am Sonntagmorgen"* (church bells are universal in German towns — works); "at the end of my rope" → *"am Ende meiner Kraft"* (not the literal rope).
- **Keep concrete sensory anchors** (bathroom floor at 3 AM, cage door, wedding feast, wine like a river) — render them vividly in German (*"auf dem kalten Boden um drei Uhr nachts"*). Don't abstract them away.
- **Localize culture-bound American daily-life anchors** to emotionally equivalent German scenes: *carpool line / school run* → *"der Weg zur Schule," "im Stau auf der Autobahn"*; *diner / drive-thru* → *"die Imbissbude," "der Küchentisch"*; *credit-card debt* → *"die Schulden, die mich erdrücken"* (which doubles beautifully with the *Schuld* = guilt frame).
- **Do NOT localize away the global/heaven imagery** — every-tribe, throne, Lamb, wedding feast, the New Jerusalem stay intact.

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**German (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, frame-appropriate):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus ist der Grund, drum jubeln wir jetzt laut! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Der König kehrt zurück — ich hab' vertraut!

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; *Jesus* (consistent name); "Jesus is the reason we are dancing now" → *Jesus ist der Grund, drum jubeln wir jetzt laut* — I rendered "dancing" as **jubeln** ("rejoice/shout for joy"), which fits restrained German worship temperature (§1A) better than literal *tanzen* while keeping the same exuberant payload, and SAID so; word order is verb-second and natural; "the King is coming back" → *Der König kehrt zurück* (majestic, non-political); *laut / vertraut* is a real, honest rhyme; the resolve "somehow, somehow" → *ich hab' vertraut* ("I have trusted") keeps the note of confident hope; zero offense across traditions.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A):** suppose you need a rhyme for *"Gnade"* (grace).
- ❌ **Forced:** *"…und unser Glück bringt uns die Gnade, fürwahr, am Pfade!"* — *Glück* (luck) implies salvation is fortune (bad theology, §0A.9), *fürwahr* is archaic filler, *am Pfade* ("on the path") is jammed in only to rhyme.
- ✅ **Meaning-first (rhyme kept):** *"…am Kreuz schenkt Jesus seine Gnade — er heilt den Schaden."* — real words, sound doctrine (grace from Christ at the cross), *Gnade/Schaden* an honest near-rhyme, guilt–innocence frame (Christ heals the damage/debt).
- ✅ **Also fine (rhyme dropped):** *"…am Kreuz schenkt Jesus seine Gnade; der Schuldschein ist zerrissen."* — no rhyme, but vivid guilt–innocence imagery (the debt-note torn up) and crystal-clear. **A true unrhymed line beats a false rhymed one.**

## 10. ADDICTIVENESS / RE-LISTENABILITY PASS (after the album is produced in German)

After the German album is produced, run the full addictiveness pass. This tunes the **English `Style` block (§6)** and the Suno re-gen prompts ONLY — **never** doctrine or the locked translation.

**Three listen passes:**
1. **Feeling pass** — listen as a normal German believer. Where does it move you? Where does attention drift? Does the *Sehnsucht*/guilt–innocence payload land emotionally (§1A)?
2. **Craft pass** — listen for hook strength, groove/pocket, vocal delivery, arrangement, mix clarity, ear-candy moments.
3. **Replay-trigger pass** — what makes you hit repeat? Identify the single most addictive 2–4 seconds and make sure it's not buried.

**Amplify (via the English Style block + re-gen only):**
- **Hook & delivery** — make "Jubilujah"/the chorus hook land harder and earlier; sharpen vocal attack.
- **Groove & pulse** — target a head-nod pocket (~100–128 BPM where the style allows; keep hymnic tracks at their proper tempo).
- **Texture & ear-candy** — add tasteful, culturally resonant instrumentation (organ/choir/Choral pads for hymnic; modern pop production for CCM) and small surprises.
- **Dynamic arc & payoff** — build → restrained verse → fuller chorus → bridge lift → final-chorus payoff (German restraint rewards a delayed swell).
- **Loop point & album flow** — make the end invite a replay; sequence the album to flow.
- **Emotional pull & relatability** — using §1A's guilt–innocence frame and the *Sehnsucht* ache word.

**Re-rate each track + the album BEFORE/AFTER** across **hook strength, groove, replay value, dynamic payoff, emotional pull, loop-ability** (e.g. 1–10). Deliver a summary of changes, why each boosts re-listening, and the measurable before/after effect.
