## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the translator's *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to the meaning & the gospel** — the theological content must survive intact (the cross, the resurrection, the name of Jesus, repentance, the King's return). Never soften or alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, and open vowels must fit the original melody. A line a native can't sing comfortably has failed, even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant Spanish** — it must sound like a native believer wrote it, not like a translated English song. No calques, no English word order; uses the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture (bathroom floor, cage door, wedding feast). Swap an image only when it is untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook preserved** — match the rhyme scheme *only when it can be done with real, sensible words*; ALWAYS preserve the hook's rhythmic placement.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**. Break a rhyme before you break the meaning or the meter.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** Every single line must make complete, natural sense to a native Spanish speaker *on its own, with the rhyme ignored.* **Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement.** It is always better to ship an unrhymed (or near-rhymed) line that means something true and clear than a rhymed line that is forced, vague, or padded. If you cannot rhyme with words that genuinely belong in the sentence, **DROP THE RHYME** — use blank verse, assonance (Spanish loves it), or a near-rhyme. Never invent a word, bend grammar, or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line (delete the final rhyme word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?).
>
> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to the culture freely — but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity to be more relatable. Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form (1 Cor 1:23). Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

Before finalizing any line, check it against this list. These are the failure modes a faith-based Spanish speaker instantly hears as wrong.

**1. No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler words.** Never end a line with a word that's there *only* to rhyme. Real Spanish offenders that creep in: **"sí"/"ya"/"pues" tacked on** to pad a bar; **"jamás" / "además" / "compás"** dropped in just for the easy `-ás` rhyme when they add nothing; **"de verdad"** as line-end filler; abstract abstract-noun chains like **"con tu bondad y tu piedad y tu deidad"** strung only for `-dad`; **"corazón" forced** at every line-end (the `-ón`/`-azón` lazy rhyme — *canción, pasión, unción, emoción* stacked meaninglessly); **"luz/cruz/Jesús"** triplet abused as auto-rhyme. **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.

**2. Use only REAL Spanish words and CORRECT verb forms.** Spanish subjunctive/mood traps: after *para que, cuando* (future), *antes de que* you need subjunctive — *"para que **cante**,"* not *"para que canta."* Don't confuse **haya** (subj. of haber) / **halla** (finds) / **allá** (over there); **ves** (you see) vs **vez** (time); **vaya / valla / baya**. Preterite vs imperfect must be right (*"Él me **salvó**"* one-time act vs *"me **amaba**"* ongoing). No invented forms (*"satisfació"* → *satisfizo*; *"andó"* → *anduvo*).

**3. Correct prepositions & set phrases — no calques.** Anglicisms to kill: *"estoy **excitado**"* (means sexually aroused) for "excited" → **emocionado / lleno de gozo**; *"realizar"* for "to realize/understand" → **darse cuenta** (*realizar* = to carry out); *"en orden a"* (calque of "in order to") → **para**; *"aplicar"* for "to apply (oneself)" → **dedicarse**; *"asumir"* for "to assume/suppose" → **suponer**; *"soportar"* for "to support" → **apoyar / sostener** (*soportar* = to endure/tolerate); *"introducir"* for "to introduce a person" → **presentar**; *"bendecir a alguien **con**"* — check the real regimen. Verify every preposition and idiom is one a native actually uses.

**4. No English-calque or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** Don't write *"línea directa al cielo"* if it reads like a phone hotline; don't calque "I'm all in" as *"estoy todo adentro"* (meaningless) → **me entrego por completo**; don't calque "you've got my back" as *"tienes mi espalda"* → **me cubres las espaldas / me cuidas**; avoid hyphen-stacked English compounds — de-hyphenate into a natural clause.

**5. Loanwords/register that misread in Spanish.** Beware false friends in worship lines: **"gracia"** is perfect for *grace* but also = *funny/wit* and the plural *gracias* = *thank you* — fine and even a nice double meaning, but don't let a line accidentally read "how funny." **"templo"** vs **"iglesia"** (building vs congregation) — pick the right one. Avoid regional slang that breaks reverence (*"chido, padrísimo, guay, bacano, qué chévere, la neta"*) in a worship lyric. **"rico"** in a praise line can read "tasty/sexy" — avoid. Don't drop English fillers (*"oh yeah, come on, let's go"*) into the sung Spanish body; keep them only if the EN hook itself is in English.

**6. Line-end adjectives & participles agree in gender/number.** Spanish inflects: a female narrator says *"estoy **cansada**,"* *"fui **rescatada**,"* *"me siento **amada**."* A dangling adjective chosen for rhyme often breaks agreement — *"el alma **limpio**"* → **limpia** (*alma* is feminine despite *el*); *"mano **abierto**"* → **abierta**. **FIX NARRATOR GENDER FIRST** before any rhyme work (see §1A).

**7. No grammatically stranded tails.** Don't strand a relative clause or a genitive with no head: *"la gloria de, en lo alto"* → recast so every word has a grammatical home. Watch the *"lo"* neuter trap — *"lo que"* needs a real referent.

**8. Imagery internally consistent.** Don't append an image that contradicts the scene (cold tile floor + a "blaze/fire" right beside it unless intended), and don't introduce an object with no antecedent. Match the imagery already in the verse.

**9. Theology over rhyme.** Never let a rhyme word distort doctrine. Don't let *"suerte/destino"* (luck/fate) creep in to rhyme — keep chance subordinate to God's sovereignty (*Su voluntad, Su plan*). Don't soften *"pecado"* (sin) to *"error/fallo"* for euphony. Grace comes from Christ, not from circumstance.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** read the line and silently delete its final rhyming word. Does the rest still say something true and clear? Is the final word one you'd actually use in that sentence? If no — the line is forced. Rewrite it meaning-first, even if that means no rhyme (assonance is enough in Spanish).

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

The Spanish-speaking world (Spain + Latin America, ~500M speakers) is **predominantly Roman Catholic by heritage**, with a **large and fast-growing Evangelical/Pentecostal ("evangélico") movement** across Latin America (Brazil aside, Spanish-speaking countries like Guatemala, Honduras, Chile, and much of Central America now have huge evangelical populations), plus historic Reformed/Baptist/Brethren communities and charismatic renewal Catholics. Jubilee/Inspire songs are CCM/evangelical in origin, but the ES version must **hold for the whole Spanish-speaking Body of Christ.**

**Cross-tradition safety rules (ES-specific):**
- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** that all traditions own: *Dios, el Señor, Jesús, Cristo, el Padre, el Espíritu Santo, la gracia, la gloria, la cruz, la resurrección, el Cordero, el Rey, Aleluya, Amén, Hosanna.*
- **Anchor Scripture echoes to the Reina-Valera 1960 (RVR60)** — the de-facto standard, most broadly recognized Spanish Bible across Protestant and much of the wider Christian world. Echo its phrasings when a line quotes Scripture: John 19:30 → **"Consumado es"**; Rev 22:20 → **"Ven, Señor Jesús"**; Ps 23 → *"El Señor es mi pastor; nada me faltará"*; the Annunciation/greeting register and Psalms cadence of RVR60 are what believers carry. (Catholic readers will also recognize most of this; RVR60 is the safest common denominator for sung worship.)
- **Do NOT import tradition-specific practices** the source lacks: **no Marian devotion** (no *Ave María, Virgen, Madre de Dios, Reina del cielo, intercesión de María*), no saint/relic veneration, no rosary imagery, no one-church liturgical formulas, no glossolalia naming. Keep it Christ-centered and Bible-plain — that is the common ground that offends no one.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Address God with respect; capitalize divine pronouns where it aids reverence (*Tú, Ti, Él, Su, Suyo* for God — common in Spanish Christian print). Use *Tú* (not *usted*) for God — this is the universal, intimate-yet-reverent worship norm across all Spanish traditions. Never use crude/vulgar/street-slang in a worship lyric.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** Bride/Bridegroom (*la Esposa / el Esposo*, or *la Novia / el Novio*) is biblical and fine — keep it pure, never romantic-pop longing.
- **Gender/inclusive-language & family handling: defer to THIS community's norms.** Spanish-speaking evangelical/Catholic worship overwhelmingly uses the standard generic masculine (*los hijos de Dios, los hombres, hermanos*) — do **not** import "@/x/e" inclusive forms (*niñes, todxs, hermanes*); they read as secular-political and offend the receptor community. Keep traditional, Scripture-plain Spanish.
- **Keep the offense of the cross, remove the offense of bad form** (1 Cor 1:23). Don't dilute sin (*pecado*), blood (*la sangre*), repentance (*arrepentimiento*), judgment (*juicio*), or Christ's exclusivity — say them in clean, dignified Spanish.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP (the relatability layer)

- **Lead with the worldview frame.** Latin-American/Iberian Christian culture is **predominantly guilt–innocence** (legal/moral debt and forgiveness — strongly shaped by Catholic confession and the cross), **with a powerful honor–shame/family overlay** (*la familia, la vergüenza, el honor, la dignidad, el qué dirán, the prodigal-son welcome home*). **Foreground BOTH:** the legal/debt → freedom images (*deuda perdonada, cadenas rotas, libre, justificado, no hay condenación*) AND the honor/belonging images (*el Padre que corre a abrazarte, la casa, la mesa, vuelves a casa, ya no hay vergüenza, eres hijo/hija, perteneces, restaurado*). The prodigal son (*el hijo pródigo*) and the running Father land with enormous force in this culture.
- **Worship temperature: EXUBERANT.** This is the celebration culture par excellence — *gozo, fiesta, palmas, gritos de júbilo, baile, danza* are welcome and expected. Lean into joyful, embodied praise. *Danzar/bailar ante el Señor* is biblical (David, Ps 150) and culturally beloved — **no need to soften "dancing."**
- **Taboo/danger images → safe substitutes:**
  - **"Buddy/homeboy Jesus"** (overly chummy slang for the Lord) → keep Jesus *cercano* (close, tender) but never *"mi pana/mi cuate/mi brother Jesús."* Intimacy yes, irreverence no.
  - **Marian overtones** — if an EN line says "Mother" in a maternal-God metaphor, render carefully so it cannot read as Marian (*como una madre* simile is fine; never *la Madre* as a title).
  - **Wine "flowing like a river"** — fine and biblical (Cana, the new wine of the Kingdom); just don't let it read as drunkenness/*borrachera*.
  - **"Fire"** — redemptive/Spirit fire (*fuego del Espíritu, refinador*) is beloved; only contextualize when EN clearly means judgment.
  - **"Conquering/army/warfare"** — generally fine (*más que vencedores* is RVR60, beloved); just avoid sounding like literal political militancy in conflict-touched regions.
- **Native "ache" word / sacred-longing idiom:** **"anhelo"** (deep longing/yearning — *"mi alma te anhela,"* echoing *"Como el ciervo brama por las corrientes de las aguas, así clama por ti, oh Dios, el alma mía"* Ps 42 RVR60). Also **"sed de Dios"** (thirst for God) and **"añoranza"** (wistful longing for home/heaven). Use *anhelo/anhelar* as the go-to for sacred longing.
- **Charged vocabulary to handle with care:** *patria/nación/sangre y tierra* (nationalist overtones — keep "homeland" heavenly, *la patria celestial*, not earthly nationalism); *Rey/reino* are perfectly natural in Spanish (no republic-allergy — beloved imagery, *Cristo Rey, el Reino*); *cadenas/esclavitud/libertad* carry real historical weight across Latin America (slavery, colonial history) — use them for the gospel's freedom with dignity, never glibly.
- **Reverence/honorific forms required when addressing God:** use **"Tú"** (familiar) for God, not *usted* — universal worship norm; *vosotros/ustedes* per region for plural address to people. Capitalize *Tú, Ti, Él, Su, Suyo, Quien* when they refer to God. Vocatives: *"¡Señor!", "¡Oh Dios!", "Padre", "Jesús", "Cristo"* — all natural and reverent.
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** **assonance** (rhyming only the vowels, e.g. *-a—o*, *-e—a*) is the deep native poetic tradition (romances, coplas) and is *fully* satisfying to a Spanish ear — use it freely instead of forcing consonant rhyme. Also **paralelismo** (parallel couplets, very Psalm-like and very natural in Spanish hymnody) and the **estribillo** (refrain) and **call-and-response** ("¡Aleluya!" — "¡Gloria a Dios!") common in Latin worship.

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY (Spanish-specific)

- **Match syllable count to the melody.** Spanish words run longer than English; compress with the **synalepha** (Spanish's built-in vowel-merging across word boundaries — *"mi alma"* sings as 2 syllables, not 3; *"de_Él," "que_es," "lo_amo," "Tú_eres"* merge naturally) and with contractions Spanish singers actually use: *pa'* (para), *to'* (todo — informal, use sparingly), *na'* (nada — sparingly), and the obligatory **al** (a+el) and **del** (de+el). Synalepha is the single most powerful syllable-fitting tool — exploit it; do **not** pad with filler.
- **Respect Spanish stress.** Most words are *llanas* (penultimate stress); put stressed syllables on strong beats and place *agudas* (final-stress: *Jesús, Señor, Salvador, redención*) where a strong beat lands on the last syllable. Don't force an unnatural accent. Read every line aloud in rhythm.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes.** Spanish is vowel-rich — end big held notes on **a, o, e** especially (*gloria, aleluya, Señor, amor, cantaré, Salvador*); avoid landing a long sustain on a closed cluster. The hook "Jubiluyah" ends open (-ah) — exploit it.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot.** The chorus must arrive on the same bar.
- **Rhyme stance for Spanish: DON'T FORCE consonant rhyme.** Spanish rhymes easily — *too* easily, which is the trap (the lazy `-ón/-ar/-ad/-ús` auto-rhymes, §0A). Prefer **assonance** (vowel-only rhyme), the deep native tradition that fully satisfies a Spanish ear, over a strained consonant rhyme. It is usually better to **recast the OTHER line of the couplet** than to cram a bad word onto the current one. Internal rhyme and alliteration are bonuses, never obligations.
- **Reach for the native poetic device (§1A)** — assonance, paralelismo, estribillo, call-and-response — where it fits.
- **Diacritics are MANDATORY** (á é í ó ú, ñ, ü). Suno/TTS pronounce from text and stress from the written accent: *"corazon"* mis-sings; **"corazón"** sings right. *"si"* (if) vs *"sí"* (yes), *"el"* (the) vs *"él"* (he/He), *"mas"* (but) vs *"más"* (more) — wrong accent = wrong word and wrong stress. Always write full diacritics in sung body and titles. Keep **ñ** (*niño, Señor, mañana*).
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud slurred (as an AI vocal would) and check blurred syllables don't form a vulgar/comic word. Spanish traps: avoid sequences that slur into **"caga-," "puta," "coño," "pija/pinga,"** or **"verga"**; watch *"-on culo," "mi erga"*-type accidental joins across word boundaries; watch the *"polla"* (vulgar in Spain) reading; check that synalepha doesn't fuse two words into something crude. If a slur produces a bad word, recast.

## 5. IDIOMS, IMAGERY & CULTURAL FIT

- **Translate the sense, not the words, for idioms.** "permission slip" → a natural ES equivalent (*no necesitas permiso / no tienes que pedir permiso*); "you turned it around / did the flip" → *lo cambiaste todo / le diste la vuelta*; "Sunday morning bell" → *la campana del domingo* (church bells universal in the Hispanic world).
- **Keep concrete sensory anchors** (bathroom floor at 3 AM, cage door, wedding clock, wine like a river) — render them vividly in Spanish (*el piso frío a las tres de la madrugada, la puerta de la jaula*).
- **Localize culture-bound daily-life anchors** to emotionally equivalent native scenes (carpool line, diner, prom, credit-card debt) → everyday Hispanic equivalents (*el camino a la escuela, la mesa de la cocina, la deuda que ahoga*). **Do NOT localize away the global/heaven imagery** — every-tribe, throne, Lamb, wedding feast stay.

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**ES (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, frame-appropriate):**
> ¡Jubiluyah! ¡Jubiluyah! / ¡Jesús es la razón, hoy bailamos sin temor! / ¡Jubiluyah! ¡Jubiluyah! / ¡El Rey va a volver — Aleluya, sí, mi Señor!

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; *Jesús* (consistent name); "Jesus is the reason we are dancing now" → *Jesús es la razón, hoy bailamos sin temor* — **dancing is KEPT** (no taboo in this exuberant culture; *bailar* is celebrated, and *sin temor* "without fear" adds a native honor-shame note of shame/fear lifted); "the King is coming back" → *El Rey va a volver* (majestic, beloved royal imagery, no republic-allergy); rhyme *temor / Señor* (consonant) is real and sensible, and the line also rides assonance — fully satisfying to a Spanish ear; meaning intact; zero offense across traditions; capitalized *Rey/Señor* for reverence.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A and §1A):** Suppose you need to render "The King is coming back."
- ❌ Forced/wrong-frame: *"¡El Rey regresará — qué emoción, qué emoción!"* — *emoción* is dropped in purely for the easy `-ón` rhyme (filler, §0A.1), it's vague, and "qué emoción" trivializes the Second Coming into pop sentimentality.
- ❌ Taboo/syncretism: *"¡La Reina del cielo nos lleva al Señor!"* — imports **Marian devotion** (*la Reina del cielo* = Queen of Heaven), which the source lacks and which offends evangelical readers and distorts the line.
- ✅ Meaning-first, culturally resonant: *"¡El Rey va a volver — Aleluya, sí, mi Señor!"* — real words, sound doctrine (the King's literal return), reverent, joyful, rhyme honest (*volver / Señor* near-rhyme + assonance), and *Aleluya* lands the celebration the culture loves. **A true, joyful, doctrinally clean line beats a forced or syncretistic one.**
