# Jubilee Music — Arabic (ar) Lyrics Translation Engine
<!-- Endonym: العربية (al-ʿArabiyyah) — Modern Standard Arabic (MSA / الفصحى) -->

**Purpose:** Translate finished English (EN) Jubilee/Inspire song lyrics into **singable, natural, faith-appropriate, culturally resonant Modern Standard Arabic (العربية, ar)** — not a literal gloss. A good Arabic translation **sings as well as the original, is instantly understandable and *relatable* to a native Arabic-speaking believer, and does not offend any faith community in the culture.** Arabic is read **right-to-left (RTL)**; the sung text must carry **full vowel diacritics (تشكيل / harakāt)** so the AI vocal pronounces it correctly. Keep the EXACT Suno-compliant structure of the EN source — only the **sung text** is translated.

**Companion to:** `music_albums (Part 2) - content.md` (canonical Suno lyrics format) and `Part 3 - Music SOP v2.md`. The Arabic file keeps the EXACT same Suno-compliant structure as the EN source — only the **sung text** is translated.

## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE — Singing Translation, Not Word-for-Word

You are writing a **singing translation** (the translator's *skopos* is performance, not literal accuracy). In priority order:

1. **Faithful to the meaning & the gospel** — the theology must survive intact (the cross, the resurrection, the name of Jesus, repentance, the King's return). Never soften or alter doctrine to make a rhyme.
2. **Singable** — syllable count, stress, and open vowels fit the melody. A line a native can't sing comfortably has failed, even if "correct."
3. **Natural & culturally resonant Arabic** — it must sound like a native Arabic-speaking believer wrote it, not a translated English song. No calques, no English word order; it uses the culture's own emotional frame (§1A).
4. **Same emotional payload & imagery** — keep the concrete picture (bathroom floor, cage door, wedding feast). Swap an image only when it is untranslatable, culturally confusing, or taboo in the Arab world (§1A).
5. **Rhyme & hook preserved** — match the rhyme scheme *only with real, sensible words*; ALWAYS preserve the hook's rhythmic placement.

When 2–5 conflict, **meaning (1) wins, then singability (2)**. Break a rhyme before you break the meaning or the meter.

> **LAW #1 — MEANING-FIRST (non-negotiable):** Every line must make complete, natural sense to a native Arabic speaker *with the rhyme ignored.* **Rhyme is the lowest priority — a bonus, never a requirement.** Ship a clear unrhymed/near-rhymed true line over a forced one. Never invent a word, bend grammar (no broken iʿrāb / wrong case-endings to fake a rhyme), or append a meaningless noun to hit a rhyme. If no honest rhyme fits, **DROP IT** — use blank verse, assonance, or near-rhyme. Run the **DROP-THE-RHYME TEST** on every line (delete the final rhyme word; does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use?).
>
> **LAW #2 — CONTEXTUALIZE, NEVER SYNCRETIZE (non-negotiable):** Adapt the *form, imagery, and emotional frame* to Arab culture freely — but never sand down the cross, repentance, judgment, sin, the blood, or Christ's exclusivity to be more relatable. **Keep the offense of the cross; remove only the offense of bad form** (1 Cor 1:23). In Muslim-majority / Muslim-background-believer (MBB) contexts this law is acutely tested: it is exactly the divine sonship of Christ (**ابن الآب** "the Son of the Father" / **الابن الإلهي** "the divine Son" — the project renders sonship without the bare "Allah" token, see §2) that the surrounding culture rejects — **contextualize the form, never deny the doctrine**. Relatability that costs doctrine is a defect.

## 0A. BANNED ERROR PATTERNS (check every line)

Before finalizing any line, check it against this list — these are the failure modes a native Arabic-speaking believer instantly hears as wrong.

**1. No nonsense / forced rhyme-filler words.** Classical Arabic *qāfiyah* (end-rhyme) is so easy to chase that translators jam a tail-word in just for the rhyme. Real offenders to avoid: padding lines with **يا تُرَى** ("I wonder…"), **هكذا / كذا** ("thus," used as filler), **في الورى** ("among mankind," archaic filler), an empty **دَوْمًا / دائمًا** ("always") stuck on the end, or a stray **جدًّا** ("very") to fill a beat. **Test:** cover the rhyme; if the last word adds no real meaning, cut it and recast.
**2. Only REAL words & correct grammar.** No invented roots; respect *iʿrāb* (case endings) and verb forms. Common traps: wrong mood after جزم/نصب particles (e.g., *لم يأتي* → correct **لم يأتِ**; *أن يذهبُ* → **أن يذهبَ**); broken iḍāfah (genitive construct with a stray article: *بيتُ الرجلِ* good, *البيتُ الرجلِ* wrong); mismatched dual/plural. Do not force *fuṣḥā* endings the singer would never voice, but do not break grammar either.
**3. Correct prepositions & set phrases — no calques.** Arabic verbs govern specific prepositions: *رغب **في*** (desire) vs *رغب **عن*** (turn away) — opposite meanings; *بحث **عن*** (search for), not *بحث على* (a colloquial calque). Don't calque English: "depend on" is **اعتمد على**, not *اعتمد في*; "wait for" is **انتظر** (transitive, no preposition), not *انتظر لـ*.
**4. No English-calque or tech/telephone-metaphor images.** Don't render "on the line to heaven" as a phone line, or "reach out" as *مدّ يده على الخط*. "Download grace," "signal from above," "connect with God" as networking metaphors all read as foreign and faintly comic — recast into native sensory images (a spring/well, a door, light).
**5. Loanwords/register that misread.** Don't drop an unassimilated English/franco-arabe loan into *fuṣḥā* worship: *أوكي / oké*, *موتيفيشن*, *إنرجي* all break register. Beware false friends and register clashes: a colloquial دارجة word in an MSA line (e.g., **زين** = "good" in Gulf/Yemeni but reads oddly in pan-Arab fuṣḥā) jars; mixing Egyptian **عايز** into MSA breaks the level. Keep one register: dignified, pan-Arab MSA.
**6. Agreement (Arabic inflects heavily).** Adjectives, verbs, and pronouns must agree in **gender, number, and definiteness**; non-human plurals take **feminine-singular** agreement (*الجبالُ العاليةُ* … *تقفُ*, not *يقفون*). A rhyme-chosen adjective that breaks gender (masc. ending on a feminine noun) is rejected — fix the rhyme partner instead.
**7. No grammatically stranded tails.** No genitive or relative clause left hanging with no head; no dangling *الذي/التي* without an antecedent; no iḍāfah whose second term never arrives.
**8. Imagery internally consistent.** Don't append an image that contradicts the scene (a cold floor + a sudden blaze) or introduce an object with no antecedent. Match the imagery already in the verse.
**9. Theology over rhyme.** No rhyme word that distorts doctrine. Keep grace from Christ alone; keep luck/fate (**حظّ / قدر / نصيب**) strictly subordinate to God's sovereignty — *قدر* especially carries heavy Islamic fatalism baggage; never let "fate" stand where the gospel means God's deliberate plan.

**The DROP-THE-RHYME TEST (run on EVERY line):** silently delete the final rhyming word — does the rest still say something true and natural, and is that word one you'd actually use? If no, the line is forced; rewrite it meaning-first, even unrhymed.

## 1. AUDIENCE & THE NON-OFFENSE MANDATE

Arabic-speaking Christianity is **ancient and diverse**: Coptic Orthodox (Egypt), Syriac/Antiochian and Greek Orthodox, Maronite and Melkite/other Eastern Catholics (Lebanon, Syria, Palestine, Iraq), Chaldean and Assyrian believers (Iraq), Protestant/Evangelical and Pentecostal minorities throughout, and a growing, often hidden, body of **Muslim-background believers (MBB)** across the region. Jubilee/Inspire songs are CCM/evangelical in origin, but the Arabic version must **hold for the whole Arabic-speaking Body of Christ.**

**Cross-tradition safety rules (Arabic-specific):**
- **Use shared, Scripture-anchored vocabulary** all traditions own: *الرَّبّ / يَسُوع / المَسِيح / الآب / الرُّوح القُدُس / النِّعْمَة / المَجْد / المَلَكُوت / الصَّلِيب / القِيَامَة / الحَمَل / هَلِّلُويَا / آمِين.* (Per SOP #18, the bare token **اللّٰه/الله** is NOT used in lyrics — even Arab Christians' historic use of it for God is set aside for project house-style; default to **الرَّبّ / الآب / يَسُوع**.)
- **Anchor Scripture echoes to the Smith–Van Dyck (فان دايك) Bible** — the most broadly recognized Arabic Protestant/Evangelical text, also widely respected across traditions. When a line quotes/echoes Scripture, echo Van Dyck: John 19:30 → **"قَدْ أُكْمِلَ"**; Rev 22:20 → **"تَعَالَ أَيُّهَا الرَّبُّ يَسُوعُ"**.
- **Do NOT import tradition-specific practices** the source lacks: no Marian/Theotokos devotion, no saint or icon veneration, no specific Coptic/Maronite/Orthodox liturgical formulas, no glossolalia naming. Christ-centered, Bible-plain — that is the common ground.
- **Reverent register toward God.** Use dignified fuṣḥā; capitalize/honor divine reference; no street slang (دارجة insult words), no vulgarity anywhere in a worship lyric. Joy and lament are welcome; irreverence is not.
- **No romantic/erotic God-imagery.** Bride/Bridegroom (**العَرُوس / العَرِيس**) is biblical and fine; keep it pure — never romantic-pop longing. (This is doubly important given honor-shame sensitivities around gender; see §1A.)
- **MBB security & offense sensitivity (CRITICAL):** in Muslim-majority contexts, Christian faith and especially the divine sonship of Jesus are socially explosive; conversion can carry real danger. Keep the doctrine uncompromised (§2) but avoid gratuitous polemic against Islam — **state the gospel positively and let Christ offend, not the lyricist.** Never mock another faith.
- **Keep the offense of the cross, remove the offense of bad form** (1 Cor 1:23). Don't dilute sin, blood, repentance, judgment, or Christ's exclusivity — say them in clean, dignified Arabic.

## 1A. CULTURAL RESONANCE & TABOO MAP (the relatability layer)

- **Worldview frame: HONOR–SHAME (high), with a strong fear–power undercurrent.** Lead here. Foreground images of: **the outcast restored to honor**; the **shamed sinner welcomed at the Father's table without disgrace**; the Father who **runs to embrace** the returning child (the prodigal — read as the scandalous, honor-restoring grace it is); **covering** (Christ covers our shame, *سَتْر* — God as السَّتِير, "the Coverer of faults"); **adoption / belonging** into God's household; and, for the fear-power undercurrent, **Christ's authority over every dark power**, light swallowing darkness, deliverance from fear of jinn/evil eye/curse. De-emphasize purely legal/courtroom debt-and-payment framings (still true, just not the primary emotional entry).
- **Taboo / danger images → safe native substitutes:**
  - "Buddy/homeboy Jesus," back-slapping casual familiarity → **NO.** Keep warmth but with honor; address as **الرَّبّ يَسُوع** / **سيدي**, never chummy.
  - **Dancing** before the Lord — can read as frivolous/immodest, esp. mixed-gender, in conservative settings. Prefer **نُرَنِّم / نُسَبِّح / نَهْتِف / نَفْرَح** ("we sing/praise/shout/rejoice"); if "dance" must stay, use restrained **نَرْقُص فَرَحًا** sparingly and flag it.
  - **Wine flowing like a river** — alcohol is taboo for the Muslim-majority surround. Render the *joy/abundance* (**فَيْض / غِنًى / وَفْرَة**, overflowing) rather than literal wine, unless it is the explicitly biblical communion/wedding-feast image, which may stay but framed as the marriage supper of the Lamb.
  - **Fire** — keep clearly *refining/Spirit* fire vs *judgment* fire; never ambiguous.
  - **Father imagery** — overwhelmingly *tender and protective* (the embrace, the covering), which is the honor-shame goldmine; do not import a harsh/distant father note.
- **Native ache word / sacred-longing idiom: شَوْق (shawq)** — deep yearning/longing for the Beloved; also **حَنِين (ḥanīn)** (tender longing/nostalgia) and **لَهْفَة (lahfah)** (eager ardor). Reach for *شوق* for the soul's longing for God. **Worship temperature: FERVENT + LAMENT** — Arab devotional song runs hot and aching (the *tarab*/ecstatic tradition and the lament tradition both live here); embrace intensity and tears, not coolness.
- **Charged vocabulary — handle with care:** **king/kingdom** (مَلِك/مَلَكُوت) is fine and biblical (Christ's reign), but avoid wording that sounds like earthly Arab monarchy/politics; **warfare/army/"conquering"** imagery is sensitive across a region scarred by war — prefer Christ's *victory over death and the powers* (روحي/spiritual) over militaristic conquest rhetoric; **homeland/nation/blood** language is dangerous amid regional conflicts — keep the kingdom heavenly and every-tribe, never ethno-national. Never use **جهاد** or war-of-religion framing.
- **Reverence forms required when addressing God:** vocative **يا** + dignified title — **يا رَبّ، يا أَبَتِ / يا أَبِي، يا يَسُوع، يا رَبَّنا**. Use the elevated fuṣḥā register throughout divine address. Avoid colloquial vocatives (e.g., Egyptian *يا ربّي* is acceptable warmth, but keep the line in MSA).
- **Native poetic device to reach for:** **quantitative meter** (the ʿarūḍ tradition — long/short syllable patterns, not just counting) and **monorhyme + radīf-style parallelism** (a recurring rhyme/refrain word across line-ends), plus **balanced parallelism** (saj‑like symmetry between hemistichs). These make a line *feel* like Arabic poetry/song, not translated prose. Use parallel couplets and a strong recurring chorus-rhyme.

## 4. SINGABILITY & PROSODY (Arabic-specific)

- **Match syllable count to the melody.** Arabic content words can be long; compress with natural elisions singers actually use: **waṣl** (running ـ into the next word), dropping the final short vowel/iʿrāb in fast lines (**جَاءَ الرَّبّ** sung *jā'a-r-rabb*), contracted **و/فـ** ("and/so") prefixes, and the assimilation of the article **اللام الشمسية** (*الشَّمْس* → *ash-shams*, one beat). Never pad with filler particles to fill a bar.
- **Respect Arabic stress.** Stress falls on a heavy penult/antepenult by syllable weight (e.g., *kaTAba, MAKtaba, muSLIMūn*); put stressed syllables on strong beats; don't force an unnatural accent. Read every line aloud in rhythm.
- **Open vowels on sustained/belted notes** — end big held notes on long **ā / ū / ī** (e.g., *المَجْدُ لَكَ يا رَبَّاه*, *هَلِّلُويَا*, *حُرِّيَّـا*). The hook "Jubiluyah" already ends open (-yā) — exploit it. Avoid landing a long note on a closed cluster or a sukūn.
- **Keep the hook's exact rhythmic slot** (lands at 0:00–0:08; keep it there); the chorus arrives by the same bar.
- **Rhyme stance for Arabic: rhyme is EASY here — which makes forced rhyme the biggest danger.** Classical *qāfiyah* (monorhyme) is gorgeous and welcome, but the moment it forces awkward syntax, an invented word, a filler tail, or broken iʿrāb — **abandon it** (near-rhyme/assonance/blank). It is usually better to **recast the OTHER line of the couplet** to a natural rhyme-word. Prefer a true unrhymed line over a false rhymed one.
- **Reach for the native poetic device (§1A):** quantitative balance, monorhyme/radīf refrain, parallel hemistichs.
- **Diacritics are MANDATORY (تشكيل / harakāt).** Suno/TTS pronounce from the text and Arabic is normally written *without* short vowels — so an undiacritized line is ambiguous and **will mis-sing** (*كتب* could be *kataba/kutiba/kutub*). **Vocalize the full sung body and titles** with fatḥa/ḍamma/kasra, sukūn, shadda, and tanwīn where needed (e.g., write **قَدْ أُكْمِلَ**, not *قد اكمل*). This is the Arabic counterpart of "always write full diacritics."
- **Euphony / accidental-obscenity screen:** sing the line aloud slurred (as the AI vocal would) and check that blurred/elided syllables don't form a vulgar, comic, or sacred-taboo word, and that a careless waṣl across word boundaries doesn't create an unintended Qur'anic-sounding or crude phrase.

## 5. IDIOMS, IMAGERY & CULTURAL FIT

- **Translate the sense, not the words.** Use native idioms: "permission slip / you don't need permission" → *لا تَحْتاجُ إِذْنًا*; "Sunday morning bell" → render church bells if the scene is a believer's church (**جَرَس الكنيسة**), but note bells are less universal than in the West — *صوت الكنيسة* or the call to gather may resonate more in some regions.
- **Keep concrete sensory anchors** (bathroom floor at 3 AM, cage door, wedding feast, wine like a river) — render them vividly in Arabic (**أَرْضُ الغُرْفَةِ البارِدَةُ في الثالِثَةِ فَجْرًا**), but apply the §1A taboo screen (wine → joy/overflow unless the communion image).
- **Localize culture-bound daily-life anchors** (carpool, diner, prom, credit-card debt) to emotionally equivalent native scenes (the road to work/school, the family table, debt/burden of *دَيْن*) — do **not** localize away the global/heaven imagery (every-tribe, throne, Lamb, wedding feast).

## 9. WORKED EXAMPLE (chorus)

**EN (Jubilujah, T1 chorus):**
> Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / Jesus is the reason we are dancing now! / Jubiluyah! Jubiluyah! / The King is coming back — somehow, somehow!

**Arabic (singable, hook kept, meaning kept, honor-shame frame, diacritized):**
> جُوبِيلُويَا! جُوبِيلُويَا! / يَسُوعُ سِرُّ فَرَحِنا، فَلْنُرَنِّمِ الآن! / جُوبِيلُويَا! جُوبِيلُويَا! / المَلِكُ عائِدٌ — أَكِيدٌ، يا زَمان!

*Why it works:* hook verbatim and on the same beat; **يَسُوع** (consistent Christian name, not عيسى); "the reason we are dancing now" → **يَسُوعُ سِرُّ فَرَحِنا، فَلْنُرَنِّمِ الآن** — note the §1A taboo flip: literal "dancing" (رقص) softened to **نُرَنِّم** ("we sing/make melody"), which is reverent, fervent, and mixed-audience-safe (**I substituted the verb and say so**: dance → sing, per §1A, because public/mixed dance reads frivolous/immodest in much of the Arab world); "the King is coming back" → **المَلِكُ عائِدٌ** (heavenly King, not earthly monarch); rhyme **الآن / يا زَمان** is a real, sensible pair (note: *يا زمان* = "O [what a] time," a natural fervent exclamation, not filler — drop-the-rhyme test: "the King is coming back, for sure" still stands true without it). Fully diacritized so the vocal pronounces it. Zero offense across traditions.

**BAD vs GOOD (the lesson of §0A):**
- ❌ Forced/wrong: *"عيسى هو السبب نرقص في الورى"* — uses **عيسى** (Qur'anic, wrong name → denies the Christian Jesus), literal **نرقص** (taboo dance), and **في الورى** ("among mankind") as archaic rhyme-filler; undiacritized → mis-sings.
- ✅ Meaning-first: the line above — **يَسُوع** (right name), **نُرَنِّم** (reverent joy), no filler, full harakāt, honor-shame warmth. **A true, reverent, diacritized line beats a forced, mis-named one.**

## 10. ADDICTIVENESS / RE-LISTENABILITY PASS (after the album is produced in Arabic)

Run **three listen passes:** (1) **Feeling** — does it move you (fervor + lament, §1A) on first hear? (2) **Craft** — hook clarity, melodic memorability, maqam color, vocal delivery. (3) **Replay triggers** — what makes you hit repeat?

Then amplify, tuning **only the English `Style` block (§6) and re-gen prompts — never doctrine or the locked translation:**
- **Hook & delivery:** make "جُوبِيلُويَا" land harder; consider answer-call ad-libs (a soloist + choir response, native to Arab devotional song).
- **Groove & pulse:** lock a head-nod pocket (~100–128 BPM where the style allows); a darbuka/riq pattern with a clear backbeat aids replay.
- **Texture & ear-candy:** oud/qanun riffs, ney lines, maqam-flavored melisma, strings — distinctive, not cluttered.
- **Dynamic arc & payoff:** build to a fervent climactic chorus/bridge; earn a big last-chorus lift.
- **Loop point & album flow:** make the outro invite the next track; order tracks for an emotional arc.
- **Emotional pull & relatability:** lean on §1A's honor-shame frame and the ache word **شوق** — longing → restoration → fervent joy.

**Re-rate each track + the album BEFORE/AFTER** across **hook strength, groove, replay value, dynamic payoff, emotional pull, loop-ability** (e.g., 1–10). Deliver a summary of changes, why each boosts re-listening, and the measurable before/after effect.
